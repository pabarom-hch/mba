## Discussion Questions

### Strategic Analysis

1. **Efficiency vs. Resilience Trade-off**: How should companies balance the cost of resilience (higher inventory, multiple suppliers) against the cost of disruption? What's the right level of insurance?

2. **Reshoring Reality**: Many companies announced reshoring initiatives. Is this economically sustainable long-term, or a temporary reaction? What factors will determine success?

3. **Structural Change**: Will the supply chain changes from COVID be permanent, or will companies revert to pre-pandemic practices as memory fades and cost pressures return?

### Operational Questions

4. **Tier-N Visibility**: Most companies lack visibility beyond Tier 1 suppliers. How can companies practically gain insight into Tier 2 and Tier 3 without excessive cost?

5. **Inventory Segmentation**: How should a company decide which items deserve higher safety stock and which should remain lean? Develop a framework.

6. **Supplier Relationships**: The crisis showed the value of deep supplier partnerships. But not every relationship can be "strategic." How do you choose?

### Policy and Industry Questions

7. **Industrial Policy**: Is government intervention in semiconductor supply chains (CHIPS Act) good policy? What are the risks of politicizing supply chains?

8. **Competitive Dynamics**: Did the crisis create lasting competitive advantage for well-prepared companies (like Toyota's buffer stock), or will advantages erode as others adapt?

## Key Takeaways

1. **JIT has limits** - Efficiency optimization went too far in some cases
2. **Visibility is foundational** - Can't manage what you can't see (Tier 2, 3)
3. **Concentration is risk** - Geographic, supplier, and component concentration creates vulnerability
4. **Buffers have value** - Strategic inventory is insurance, not waste
5. **Relationships matter in crisis** - Transactional relationships fail under stress
6. **Adaptation takes time** - Supply chains can't be restructured overnight
7. **Risk management is continuous** - Crises are inevitable; preparation is the variable
