# Case Study: Zara - Fast Fashion Supply Chain

## Case Overview

**Company**: Zara (Inditex Group)
**Industry**: Fashion Retail
**Focus**: Speed, vertical integration, and responsive supply chain
**Time Period**: 1990s-2023

---

## Background

### Company Profile

Zara, founded in 1975 by Amancio Ortega in Spain, revolutionized the fashion industry with its "fast fashion" model. As the flagship brand of Inditex, Zara operates over 2,000 stores in 96 countries and generates over 70% of Inditex's revenue.

## Key Supply Chain Strategies

### Postponement and Flexibility

```
POSTPONEMENT STRATEGY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TRADITIONAL APPROACH:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Design → Dye Fabric → Cut → Sew → Ship

All decisions made 6+ months early
If color is wrong, entire batch fails

ZARA'S APPROACH:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Purchase undyed fabric → Wait for trends →
Dye to current demand → Cut → Sew → Ship

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                 │
│  Undyed Fabric    Trend      Final Product     │
│  (Inventory)   Feedback     (Made to demand)   │
│                                                 │
│      ░░░░░░ ────▶ Data ────▶ ████ (Black)     │
│      ░░░░░░              ────▶ ▓▓▓▓ (Gray)     │
│      ░░░░░░              ────▶ ████ (Navy)     │
│                                                 │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Benefits:
• React to actual demand
• Reduce markdown risk
• Test colors in small batches
• Scale winners, drop losers


SMALL BATCH PRODUCTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Traditional:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
One style: 10,000 units
Risk: If style fails, 10,000 units to mark down

Zara:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Same style: 500-1,000 units initially
Test: See how it sells
React: If successful, produce more
        If not, discontinue

Artificial scarcity:
• Limited quantities create urgency
• "Buy it now or miss it"
• Customers visit stores frequently
• Zara stores: 17 visits/year vs. 3 for competitors
```

## Financial Impact

### Comparison with Competitors

```
FINANCIAL COMPARISON:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

                    Zara    Gap     H&M
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gross margin        ~57%    ~37%    ~53%
Inventory turns     10-12   4-5     3-4
Full-price sell     85%     45%     60%
Markdown rate       15%     40%     30%
Design-to-store     2-3 wk  6-9 mo  3-5 mo
New items/year      12,000  4,000   6,000


INVENTORY INVESTMENT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

For same revenue, inventory requirement:

Zara:     Revenue ÷ 11 turns = Low inventory
Gap:      Revenue ÷ 4 turns = 2.75× more inventory

Example at $10B revenue:
Zara inventory: ~$900M
Gap inventory:  ~$2.5B

Working capital savings: $1.6B
At 10% cost of capital: $160M/year advantage


MARKDOWN SAVINGS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Traditional retailer:
• 45% sold full price @ $50 = $22.50
• 55% sold at 40% off @ $30 = $16.50
• Blended revenue per unit: $38.70

Zara:
• 85% sold full price @ $50 = $42.50
• 15% sold at 30% off @ $35 = $5.25
• Blended revenue per unit: $47.75

Difference: $9.05 per unit (23% higher realized revenue)
```

## Discussion Questions

### Strategic Analysis

1. **Replicability**: Why haven't competitors successfully copied Zara's model? What barriers exist?

2. **Trade-offs**: Zara's model requires higher per-unit production costs (nearby manufacturing vs. Asia). How do they still achieve superior profitability?

3. **Scalability**: As Zara grows, what challenges might the centralized distribution model face? At what point might regional DCs make sense?

### Operational Questions

4. **Demand Sensing**: How does Zara's approach to demand forecasting differ from traditional retailers? What role does the store manager play?

5. **Supplier Relationships**: Zara uses a mix of in-house and external manufacturing. What determines which products are made where?

6. **Inventory Risk**: With limited safety stock and frequent replenishment, how does Zara handle demand spikes or supply disruptions?

### Future Challenges

7. **Sustainability vs. Speed**: Can Zara maintain its fast fashion model while meeting sustainability goals? What changes might be needed?

8. **Online Competition**: How should Zara adapt its supply chain for e-commerce, where customers expect Amazon-like speed?

## Key Takeaways

1. **Speed creates value** - Faster response means less markdown, more full-price sales
2. **Vertical integration enables control** - Own critical capabilities for competitive advantage
3. **Small batches reduce risk** - Test and learn beats big bets
4. **Information is the lifeblood** - Daily data flow enables rapid response
5. **Trade-offs are strategic choices** - Higher production cost for lower total system cost
6. **Customer proximity matters** - Store feedback is design input, not just sales
