## Service Characteristics

### Services vs. Manufacturing

```
SERVICE CHARACTERISTICS (IHIP):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

INTANGIBILITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Cannot touch, see, or store before purchase
• Difficult to evaluate quality beforehand
• Experience-based consumption
• Must tangibilize through physical evidence

HETEROGENEITY (Variability):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Each service encounter is different
• Depends on who provides, when, where
• Difficult to standardize
• Quality varies

INSEPARABILITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Production and consumption simultaneous
• Customer is part of the process
• Cannot separate provider from service
• Direct interaction required

PERISHABILITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Cannot store or inventory
• Unused capacity is lost forever
• Demand-supply matching critical
• Cannot "save" for later

IMPLICATIONS FOR OPERATIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Manufacturing              Service
───────────────────────────────────────────────────
Can inventory             Cannot inventory
Standardized              Variable
Quality before delivery   Quality during delivery
Customer not present      Customer is present
Production separated      Production = consumption
```

### Service Design Principles

```
SERVICE DESIGN FRAMEWORK:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. COMPLEXITY vs. DIVERGENCE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Complexity: Number of steps in process
Divergence: Variation allowed in each step

High Complexity + High Divergence:
    Custom consulting, haute cuisine
High Complexity + Low Divergence:
    Fast food, assembly line service
Low Complexity + High Divergence:
    Personal training, tutoring
Low Complexity + Low Divergence:
    ATM, vending machine

2. FRONT STAGE vs. BACK STAGE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Front Stage: Customer interaction
Back Stage: Support operations

Design decisions:
• What should customer see?
• What should be hidden?
• Where is the line of visibility?

3. CUSTOMER CONTACT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
High Contact: Customer is present throughout
              (haircut, surgery, consulting)

Low Contact:  Customer only at beginning/end
              (dry cleaning, repair)

High contact = more variability, harder to control
Low contact = easier to standardize, automate
```

## Service Quality

### SERVQUAL Model

```
SERVQUAL DIMENSIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. RELIABILITY
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ability to perform promised service dependably
• Deliver on time as promised
• Accurate information and billing
• Keep commitments

2. RESPONSIVENESS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Willingness to help and provide prompt service
• Quick response to requests
• Flexibility in meeting needs
• Attentiveness

3. ASSURANCE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Knowledge and courtesy of employees
• Competence and skill
• Credibility and trust
• Security and safety

4. EMPATHY
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Caring, individualized attention
• Understanding customer needs
• Personalized service
• Accessible and approachable

5. TANGIBLES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Physical facilities, equipment, appearance
• Modern equipment
• Attractive facilities
• Neat employee appearance
• Professional materials

SERVICE QUALITY GAP MODEL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gap 1: Not knowing what customers expect
Gap 2: Not selecting right service designs
Gap 3: Not delivering to service standards
Gap 4: Not matching performance to promises
Gap 5: Expected service vs. perceived service
```

### Service Recovery

```
SERVICE RECOVERY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE SERVICE RECOVERY PARADOX:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Customers who experience a failure followed by
excellent recovery may be MORE satisfied than
those who had no failure at all.

RECOVERY STEPS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Acknowledge: "I'm sorry this happened"
2. Empathize: "I understand your frustration"
3. Own: "I'll take care of this personally"
4. Fix: Solve the problem quickly
5. Compensate: Make it right
6. Follow up: Confirm resolution

EMPOWERMENT FOR RECOVERY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ritz-Carlton: $2,000 per employee per guest
              to resolve issues without approval

Nordstrom: "Use good judgment in all situations"
           No rule book needed

RECOVERY TAXONOMY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Type            Example
────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Apology         Sincere acknowledgment
Correction      Fix the actual problem
Empathy         Show understanding
Compensation    Discount, credit, free item
Follow-up       Check satisfaction later
```

## Service Capacity

### Managing Service Demand

```
DEMAND MANAGEMENT STRATEGIES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SHIFT DEMAND:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Off-peak pricing (happy hour, matinees)
• Promotions during slow periods
• Appointments vs. walk-ins
• Reservations

MANAGE PEAK DEMAND:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Queue management
• Virtual queues
• Overbooking
• Waitlist management

INVENTORY DEMAND:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Reservations = inventory of demand
• Prepayment/deposits
• Subscription services

EXPAND CAPACITY AT PEAK:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Cross-trained employees
• Part-time and seasonal workers
• Outsourcing
• Technology/self-service
```

### Waiting Line Management

```
PSYCHOLOGY OF WAITING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MAISTER'S LAWS OF WAITING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Unoccupied time feels longer than occupied
2. Pre-process waits feel longer than in-process
3. Anxiety makes waits feel longer
4. Uncertain waits feel longer than known
5. Unexplained waits feel longer than explained
6. Unfair waits feel longer than fair
7. Solo waits feel longer than group waits
8. Valuable services are worth waiting for

MANAGING PERCEPTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strategy              Implementation
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Occupy time           Mirrors, TV, reading material
Set expectations      "Wait time: 15 minutes"
Explain waits         "Due to high volume today"
Make it fair          Single queue, not multiple
Start the process     Take order in line
Manage anxiety        Progress indicators

QUEUE DESIGN OPTIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Single queue:         Fair, but can look long
Multiple queues:      Faster feeling, but unfair
Virtual queue:        Pager, text notification
Appointment:          Scheduled arrival
Take-a-number:        Wait anywhere, not in line
```

## Technology in Services

### Service Automation

```
SERVICE TECHNOLOGY SPECTRUM:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

HIGH TECH ←─────────────────────────→ HIGH TOUCH
     │                                     │
     ▼                                     ▼
Automated            Hybrid             Personal
     │                                     │
     ▼                                     ▼
• ATM              • Kiosk + help      • Private
• Self-checkout    • Online + phone      banking
• Chatbot          • Telemedicine      • Concierge
• Vending          • Hybrid learning   • Surgery

AUTOMATION CANDIDATES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Good for Automation:        Not Good for Automation:
• Routine transactions      • Complex problems
• Simple questions          • Emotional situations
• Off-hours access          • High-value interactions
• High volume               • Customization needs

CUSTOMER ACCEPTANCE FACTORS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Convenience
• Speed
• Control
• Privacy
• Cost savings

Barriers:
• Technology anxiety
• Preference for human contact
• Complexity of task
• Security concerns
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Services are different** - Intangible, variable, perishable
2. **Design for the customer** - Blueprint the experience
3. **Quality is perception** - Manage expectations and delivery
4. **Recovery matters** - Failures are opportunities
5. **Capacity is perishable** - Unused capacity is lost forever
6. **Balance tech and touch** - Right automation level
