# Week 51, Day 1: Laws Assessment — Understanding the 21 Laws of Leadership

> "Leadership is not about titles, positions, or flowcharts. It is about one life influencing another." — John Maxwell

## The Core Insight

John Maxwell's 21 Irrefutable Laws of Leadership represent decades of research, observation, and practice distilled into universal principles that govern leadership effectiveness. These laws operate whether we acknowledge them or not—like physical laws, they describe how leadership actually works rather than how we wish it worked. The CEO who understands and applies these laws leads with leverage; the CEO who ignores them struggles against forces they don't understand.

This week we'll examine these laws and their practical application to CEO leadership. Today begins with assessment—understanding where you stand in relation to each law.

---

## The 21 Laws Overview

### Laws of Personal Leadership

**1. The Law of the Lid**
Leadership ability determines a person's level of effectiveness. Your leadership is the lid on your organization's potential.

**2. The Law of Influence**
The true measure of leadership is influence—nothing more, nothing less. Title grants authority; influence grants leadership.

**3. The Law of Process**
Leadership develops daily, not in a day. Leadership is a journey of continuous growth, not a destination.

**4. The Law of Navigation**
Anyone can steer the ship, but it takes a leader to chart the course. Leaders see more, see farther, and see before others.

**5. The Law of Addition**
Leaders add value by serving others. The purpose of leadership is to lift others, not yourself.

**6. The Law of Solid Ground**
Trust is the foundation of leadership. Without trust, you cannot lead; with trust, almost anything is possible.

**7. The Law of Respect**
People naturally follow leaders stronger than themselves. Respect is earned through character, competence, and contribution.

### Laws of Team Leadership

**8. The Law of Intuition**
Leaders evaluate everything with a leadership bias. They instinctively know how to read situations, people, and trends.

**9. The Law of Magnetism**
Who you are is who you attract. You don't attract who you want; you attract who you are.

**10. The Law of Connection**
Leaders touch a heart before they ask for a hand. Connection precedes communication; relationship enables influence.

**11. The Law of the Inner Circle**
A leader's potential is determined by those closest to them. Your inner circle either extends or limits your effectiveness.

**12. The Law of Empowerment**
Only secure leaders give power to others. Insecure leaders hoard power; secure leaders multiply it.

**13. The Law of the Picture**
People do what people see. Modeling is more powerful than mentoring; demonstration trumps declaration.

### Laws of Organizational Leadership

**14. The Law of Buy-In**
People buy into the leader, then the vision. Trust the messenger before trusting the message.

**15. The Law of Victory**
Leaders find a way for the team to win. Accepting defeat is not an option for true leaders.

**16. The Law of the Big Mo**
Momentum is a leader's best friend. With momentum, problems seem smaller and people perform better.

**17. The Law of Priorities**
Leaders understand that activity is not necessarily accomplishment. Focus on what matters most.

**18. The Law of Sacrifice**
A leader must give up to go up. The higher you go, the more you must sacrifice.

**19. The Law of Timing**
When to lead is as important as what to do and where to go. The right action at the wrong time fails.

**20. The Law of Explosive Growth**
To add growth, lead followers. To multiply growth, lead leaders.

**21. The Law of Legacy**
A leader's lasting value is measured by succession. Legacy is not what you do but what you leave behind.

## The Self-Assessment Framework

### Rating Scale

For each law, honestly assess your current capability:

**1 - Unaware**: You don't understand this law or its implications
**2 - Aware but Struggling**: You understand the law but regularly violate it
**3 - Developing**: You apply this law inconsistently
**4 - Capable**: You apply this law consistently in most situations
**5 - Mastering**: You apply this law naturally and help others understand it

## Case Study Analysis

### Case Study 1: The Unaware Leader

**Context**: Kevin was a successful sales executive promoted to CEO. He had strong Law 2 (Influence) and Law 15 (Victory) scores from his sales career but had never assessed himself against leadership laws.

**The Assessment Shock**: When Kevin honestly completed the assessment, he discovered critical weaknesses:
- Law 11 (Inner Circle): 2/5—he had hired people who wouldn't challenge him
- Law 12 (Empowerment): 2/5—he made all significant decisions himself
- Law 20 (Explosive Growth): 1/5—he'd never developed a leader

**The Outcome**: Kevin's organization had grown through his sales prowess but plateaued when his personal capacity maxed out. The assessment revealed why: his leadership limitations were the organization's lid.

**The Lesson**: Assessment creates awareness. You cannot address weaknesses you don't see.

### Case Study 2: The Strategic Developer

**Context**: Maria took over as CEO with a clear understanding of leadership laws. She assessed herself honestly and created a development plan.

**The Strategic Approach**: Maria identified her three lowest scores:
- Law 8 (Intuition): 3/5
- Law 16 (Big Mo): 3/5
- Law 19 (Timing): 2/5

Rather than trying to improve everything, she focused her development energy on these three laws while maintaining her strengths.

**The Outcome**: Over two years, Maria raised her weakest law (Timing) from 2 to 4 and her other focus areas proportionally. Her overall leadership effectiveness improved dramatically because she addressed her constraints.

**The Lesson**: Strategic focus on weaknesses yields greater returns than broad improvement attempts.

### Case Study 3: The Honest Reassessment

**Context**: James had been CEO for five years and considered himself an excellent leader. At a board member's suggestion, he asked his team to rate him on the 21 laws.

**The Humbling Discovery**: James had rated himself 85/105. His team's average rating was 62/105. The largest gaps:
- Law 10 (Connection): He rated 5; team rated 2
- Law 12 (Empowerment): He rated 4; team rated 2
- Law 13 (The Picture): He rated 5; team rated 3

**The Insight**: James thought he connected well with people, but his team felt distant. He believed he empowered, but they felt micromanaged. He thought he modeled values, but they saw inconsistency.

**The Outcome**: The external assessment became the catalyst for genuine transformation. James couldn't improve what he didn't accurately see.

**The Lesson**: Self-assessment has blind spots. External feedback reveals reality.

## Practical Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Deep-Dive Assessment

Select your three lowest-scoring laws. For each one:
1. Write three specific examples where this law weakness hurt your leadership
2. Identify the root cause of your weakness (skill, will, belief, habit)
3. Research what excellence looks like for this law
4. Create a 30-day development plan

### Exercise 2: The 360 Assessment

Ask five people who know your leadership well to rate you on all 21 laws:
- Two direct reports
- Two peers
- One supervisor or board member

Compare their ratings to your self-assessment. Investigate significant gaps.

### Exercise 3: The Laws Journal

For the next 21 days, focus on one law per day:
- Morning: Read about the law and plan to observe it
- Throughout the day: Notice where this law applies
- Evening: Journal about what you learned

This immersion builds awareness and intuition about each law.

## Reflection Questions

3. **Strength Leverage**: How are you leveraging your strongest laws? Could you use them more deliberately?

4. **Blind Spots**: What blind spots might exist in your self-assessment? Who could help you see what you can't see yourself?

5. **Development Commitment**: Are you willing to commit sustained effort to improving your weakest laws? What would that commitment look like?

## Key Quote for the Day

> "The only thing a title can buy is a little time—either to increase your level of influence with others or to undermine it." — John Maxwell

Titles grant authority; laws describe how leadership actually works. The CEO title gives you a platform but doesn't guarantee leadership effectiveness. The 21 laws operate regardless of your title—you either work with them or against them. Assessment reveals where you're aligned with these laws and where you're fighting against them. This awareness is the starting point for all leadership development.
