# Week 46, Day 5: Hiring Above Your Lid

> "If you want to go fast, go alone. If you want to go far, go with people who can go further than you." — John Maxwell

## The Core Insight

While you work to raise your own lid, there's a powerful accelerant available: hiring leaders whose capabilities exceed your own. This strategy—hiring above your lid—can dramatically expand organizational capacity while you continue developing. But it requires psychological maturity, strategic intentionality, and management approaches that challenge conventional thinking.

Many CEOs unconsciously hire below their own level. They gravitate toward candidates they can easily evaluate, manage, and if necessary, replace. This feels safe but perpetuates the organizational lid. Hiring above your lid feels risky—what if they don't respect you? What if they outshine you? What if they leave for opportunities you can't provide?

These fears are real, but the alternative—constraining your organization to your current capability level—costs more than the discomfort of leading people who are better than you in certain dimensions. The best CEOs actively seek out leaders who make them feel slightly inadequate, knowing that collective capability matters more than personal superiority.

## The Psychology of Hiring Up

Understanding the psychology involved in hiring above your lid is essential to executing this strategy.

### The Ego Challenge

Your ego wants to be the smartest, most capable person in the room. It feels threatened when others demonstrate superior capability. This ego protection manifests subtly in hiring decisions:

- Finding reasons to reject obviously excellent candidates
- Preferring capable-but-not-threatening candidates over truly exceptional ones
- Rationalizing that "culture fit" excludes people who challenge you
- Unconsciously lowering standards to avoid threatening comparisons

Awareness of these dynamics is the first step. Every CEO experiences ego resistance when faced with hiring someone who might exceed them. The mature response is to acknowledge the resistance and hire anyway.

### The Security Question

Hiring people more capable than yourself raises questions: Will they respect my authority? Will they try to take my job? Will they stay, or leave once they realize they could run their own show?

These concerns aren't irrational, but they're manageable:

**Respect through authenticity**: Leaders respect CEOs who acknowledge what they don't know and value what others bring. Pretending to be more capable than you are earns less respect than honest acknowledgment of your learning edge.

**Security through value-add**: Your job as CEO isn't to be the most capable in every function. It's to integrate, align, and create conditions where capable people thrive. This CEO-specific value isn't threatened by functional excellence.

**Retention through growth**: High-capability hires stay when they're growing, challenged, and valued. Create those conditions, and capability asymmetry becomes an asset rather than a flight risk.

### The Impostor Dynamic

Hiring above your lid can intensify impostor feelings. Surrounded by people who know more than you in their domains, you may feel you don't belong at the top. This is a feature, not a bug.

The CEO's job increasingly becomes learning from the expertise around you, making integrated decisions, and maintaining organizational coherence. These are legitimate, valuable functions—not a consolation prize for not being the smartest.

## Strategic Hiring Above the Lid

Hiring above your lid works best when it's strategic rather than opportunistic.

### Identifying Lid-Raising Hires

Not every hire needs to exceed your capability. Focus on strategic positions where:

**Your Gap is Greatest**: Where is the largest delta between what the role requires and what you could personally deliver? That's where hiring above your lid has the highest impact.

**Organizational Constraint is Clearest**: What functional area most constrains organizational growth? Bringing in exceptional capability there unlocks disproportionate value.

**Learning Opportunity is Richest**: Where could you learn most from a more capable leader? The best above-lid hires teach you, accelerating your own development.

### Defining "Above Your Lid"

What does it mean to hire someone above your lid? It's not about credentials or pedigree. It's about:

- **Functional depth**: Deeper expertise in their domain than you possess or could develop
- **Scale experience**: Has successfully operated at a scale you haven't yet reached
- **Specific capabilities**: Demonstrates particular skills that are underdeveloped in you
- **Network access**: Brings relationships and connections that expand organizational reach
- **Perspective breadth**: Sees dimensions of problems you tend to miss

### The Evaluation Challenge

How do you evaluate candidates who exceed your capability? You can't fully assess expertise that exceeds your own. Strategies that help:

**Reference Triangulation**: Speak with people who can evaluate capability you can't. Their assessments supplement your limited evaluation.

**Results Analysis**: Past results provide evidence even when you can't assess methodology. What did they actually achieve?

**Expert Panels**: Include people with domain expertise in the evaluation process. Their technical assessment complements your leadership assessment.

**Problem Immersion**: Present real organizational challenges and discuss approaches. You can evaluate thinking process even when you can't evaluate technical conclusions.

**Character Assessment**: Even if you can't fully assess capability, you can assess character, values, and interpersonal dynamics. Exceptional capability with poor character creates more problems than it solves.

## Managing Above-Lid Hires

Hiring exceptional talent is only half the challenge. Managing them effectively requires adapting your leadership approach.

### The Directive-to-Delegative Shift

With less capable reports, you might direct specific actions. With above-lid hires, you set direction and desired outcomes while deferring to their expertise on execution. Your job becomes defining what success looks like, not how to achieve it.

### The Question-Based Approach

When you're not the expert, your primary leadership tools are questions:

- "Help me understand your thinking on this approach?"
- "What alternatives did you consider?"
- "What could go wrong, and how would we know?"
- "What do you need from me to succeed?"
- "What am I missing that you see?"

Questions engage expertise without requiring that you possess it.

### Creating Challenge and Growth

High-capability hires need challenge to stay engaged. Your job includes ensuring they face problems worthy of their capability:

- Expand their scope as they prove themselves
- Connect them with stretch opportunities
- Give them organizational authority commensurate with their capability
- Create visibility for their contributions
- Ensure compensation reflects their value

### Building Honest Relationships

Above-lid relationships work best with explicit acknowledgment of the dynamic:

"I hired you because you know more about [domain] than I do. I'll set direction and context. I'll ask questions to understand. I'll make integrated decisions where needed. But I'm counting on your expertise, and I'll rarely override you in your domain."

This transparency establishes appropriate expectations and shows respect for their capability.

## The Organizational Dynamics

Hiring above your lid creates ripple effects throughout the organization.

### Team Dynamics

Other team members observe above-lid hires. Their reactions vary:

- Some feel threatened by new capability in the room
- Some feel inspired to elevate their own performance
- Some feel the implicit message about performance expectations

Manage these dynamics proactively. Frame above-lid hires as organizational strengthening, not individual criticism.

### Authority Questions

When a hire knows more than the CEO in their domain, others notice. Questions arise: Who's really in charge? Does the CEO have credibility to lead?

Address this by being clear about decision rights. The above-lid hire has domain authority. You retain integrative, strategic, and organizational authority. These aren't competing—they're complementary.

### Culture Implications

Above-lid hires often bring different experiences and expectations. They may have worked in more sophisticated environments. Their standards may exceed current organizational norms. This creates productive tension—pressure to elevate—but can also create friction with existing culture.

Help them adapt enough to be effective while helping the organization learn from what they bring.

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: The Insecure Founder

**Background**: Kevin Park founded a successful consumer app company and grew it to 100 employees. His board urged him to hire a COO with operational scale experience—someone who'd operated at 500+ employee companies. Kevin resisted, citing cultural fit concerns.

**The Real Issue**: Kevin's cultural fit objection masked his real concern: hiring someone who'd make him feel inadequate. His strength was product vision, not operations. A strong COO would highlight this gap.

**The Intervention**: A trusted board member helped Kevin see his resistance pattern. Kevin acknowledged that his ego was protecting him from discomfort at the expense of organizational capability.

**The Outcome**: Kevin hired an exceptional COO with experience at much larger companies. Initially, Kevin felt exactly as he'd feared—outclassed in operational discussions. But over time, he recognized that his product vision and the COO's operational excellence combined to create something neither could achieve alone.

**The Lesson**: Hiring above your lid requires acknowledging ego resistance and proceeding anyway. The discomfort is real but temporary. The capability gain is permanent.

### Case Study 2: The Learning CEO

**Background**: Maria Fernandez led a manufacturing company into technology-enabled transformation. She knew little about technology but recognized its strategic importance. Rather than hire someone "manageable," she sought the strongest technology leader she could find.

**The Hire**: She recruited a CTO from a leading tech company—someone who initially questioned whether he could work for a non-technical CEO. Maria's pitch: "I know what we need to achieve. You know how to achieve it. I'll learn as much as I can, but I'm hiring you to be better at this than I'll ever be."

**The Partnership**: Maria became an avid learner—asking questions, reading what her CTO recommended, attending technical discussions. She never pretended to expertise she didn't have, but she developed enough literacy to engage meaningfully.

**The Result**: The technology transformation succeeded beyond expectations. Maria credited her CTO publicly and genuinely. The CTO stayed because Maria created an environment where he could do his best work. Maria's own lid rose through proximity to excellence.

**The Lesson**: Hiring above your lid creates learning opportunities. Proximity to superior capability accelerates your own development if you approach it with humility and curiosity.

### Case Study 3: The Failed Above-Lid Hire

**Background**: Thomas Chen hired a high-capability CFO from a much larger company. On paper, it was a lid-raising hire. In practice, it became a disaster.

**The Problems**: Thomas struggled to adjust his management approach. He continued to question and second-guess decisions in ways that undermined his CFO's authority. He couldn't tolerate his CFO's different style—more formal, more process-oriented. He felt threatened when his CFO's board presentations were stronger than his own.

**The Departure**: After 11 months, the CFO left for another company. Exit interview feedback was direct: "Thomas wants above-lid talent but manages for below-lid comfort."

**The Reflection**: Thomas had intellectually embraced hiring above his lid but hadn't done the psychological work required to make it succeed. His ego resistance manifested in management behaviors that drove exceptional talent away.

**The Lesson**: Hiring above your lid requires changing how you manage, not just who you hire. Without adaptive management, above-lid hires become expensive failures.

## Practical Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Hire You Fear (30 minutes)

Imagine the hire that would most intimidate you—someone whose capability in a key area would clearly exceed yours. Describe this person in detail:

- What is their background?
- What have they accomplished?
- Why would they threaten your ego?
- What would they contribute that you can't?
- What would it feel like to manage them?
- What would your organization gain from their presence?

Now ask: Is this fear a reason not to hire them, or evidence that you should?

### Exercise 2: The Reference Question Design (25 minutes)

When evaluating candidates who may exceed your capability, you need questions that access information beyond your evaluation ability. Design 10 reference check questions specifically for above-lid hiring:

Questions that reveal:
- Capability level relative to industry benchmarks
- How they've worked with less-expert managers
- Whether they elevate those around them
- How they handle capability asymmetry
- What they need to stay engaged and committed

These questions supplement your direct evaluation with perspectives that can assess what you can't.

### Exercise 3: The Management Mode Experiment (ongoing)

If you have any current team members who approach or exceed your lid in their domains, experiment with management mode shifts:

For the next month:
- Ask questions instead of giving opinions
- Set outcomes, not methods
- Explicitly acknowledge their expertise
- Request their teaching on topics they know better

Track what changes in the relationship, their performance, and your learning.

## Reflection Questions

1. **Ego Honesty**: How does your ego respond to people who are clearly more capable than you in important areas? What does that response cost your organization?

2. **Historical Pattern**: Looking at your hiring history, have you consistently hired at, above, or below your lid? What does this pattern reveal? What has it cost you?

3. **Evaluation Gap**: How do you evaluate candidates in areas where you lack expertise? What's your track record with these evaluations? Where do you need help?

4. **Management Flexibility**: How adaptable is your management style? Can you genuinely shift from directive to delegative, from telling to asking, from expert to learner?

5. **Security Foundation**: What's your security as CEO based on? If it's based on being the most capable, above-lid hiring will always threaten you. What would a more secure foundation look like?

## Key Quote for the Day

> "A leader who produces other leaders multiplies their influence. A leader who produces followers limits their impact to what they can personally touch." — John Maxwell

**Analysis**: This quote reframes above-lid hiring from threatening to essential. When you hire people more capable than yourself, you're producing leaders who extend your impact beyond your personal reach. Your influence multiplies through their capability.

The alternative—hiring people whose capability you comfortably exceed—means your organization's impact is limited to what you personally touch. This might feel safer, but it caps organizational potential at your individual level.
