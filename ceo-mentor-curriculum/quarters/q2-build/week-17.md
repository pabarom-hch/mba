# Week 17: Vertical Integration

**Quarter:** Q2 — BUILD
**Mentor:** Andrew Carnegie
**Theme:** Controlling your value chain

> "Put all your eggs in one basket, and then watch that basket." — Andrew Carnegie

---

## Overview

Carnegie didn't just own steel mills—he owned the iron ore, the coal mines, the railroads, and the ships that connected them. This vertical integration gave him cost advantages and supply security that competitors couldn't match.

This week examines when and how to control more of your value chain, and when integration is a distraction.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Value chain control creates advantage.** Know your chain. Know your vulnerabilities.

2. **Integration has benefits.** Cost, quality, speed, reliability, and security.

3. **Integration has costs.** Capital, complexity, and distraction from core competence.

4. **Strategic inputs differ from commodities.** Focus integration on strategic advantage.

5. **Modern integration goes beyond assets.** Data, relationships, and platforms matter.

---

## The Carnegie Integration Framework

Carnegie integrated when:
1. The step was critical to his competitive advantage (cost/quality)
2. Suppliers had power that threatened his business
3. He had or could develop the competence to run it well
4. The capital investment was justified by returns

He stayed focused when:
1. The step was commodity (anyone could supply)
2. Integration would distract from steel excellence
3. Partners could do it better than he could

---

## Mentor's Advice for CEOs

*"Concentrate; put all your eggs in one basket, and watch that basket."* — Andrew Carnegie

Carnegie was not a diversified conglomerate—he was a focused vertical integrator. Every acquisition strengthened his steel business. Every investment deepened his moat.

The lesson isn't that integration is always right, but that strategic focus beats scattered expansion. Know your core. Strengthen it. Don't chase every opportunity.
