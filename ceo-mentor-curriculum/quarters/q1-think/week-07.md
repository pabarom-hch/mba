# Week 7: Long-Term Thinking

**Quarter:** Q1 — THINK
**Mentor:** Warren Buffett
**Theme:** The power of thinking in decades, not days

> "Someone's sitting in the shade today because someone planted a tree a long time ago." — Warren Buffett

---

## Overview

This week shifts focus from Munger's psychology to Buffett's orientation—specifically, his extraordinary long-term time horizon. While most CEOs think in quarters, Buffett thinks in decades. This simple shift in perspective changes everything about which decisions look good.

Long-term thinking is Buffett's primary competitive advantage. Most of his edge isn't analytical—it's patience.

## Daily Practice

### Day 1 — The Time Horizon Advantage
**Morning Meditation:**
> "Our favorite holding period is forever."

**Reflection Prompt:**
What's your actual time horizon for major decisions? Be honest. When you're stressed, does it shrink to weeks or days?

**CEO Application:**
Take your most significant current decision. How would your analysis change if you had to live with the consequences for 30 years?

### Day 3 — The Institutional Imperative
**Morning Meditation:**
> "Once combatants run out of ammunition, their nuclear response is to merge."

**Reflection Prompt:**
Buffett warns of the "institutional imperative"—the tendency of organizations to imitate peers, resist change, and justify current direction. Where do you see this in your organization?

**CEO Application:**
What is your organization doing because competitors do it, not because it's right? What would you stop if you could start fresh?

**Reflection Prompt:**
Where in your business are you being too active (making changes, pursuing opportunities) when patience would yield better results?

**CEO Application:**

**CEO Application:**

## Key Takeaways

1. **Time horizon is a competitive advantage.** Most people can't think beyond next quarter. You can.

2. **Short-term pressure is constant.** Recognize it. Name it. Resist it where appropriate.

3. **Wonderful businesses improve with time.** Is yours one of them?

4. **Patience looks like inaction.** Sometimes the best decision is to wait.

5. **Align incentives with time horizons.** Your systems reinforce or undermine long-term thinking.

---

## The Buffett Time Horizon Test

Before major decisions, ask:
- Would I be comfortable with this decision appearing in tomorrow's newspaper?
- Would I be comfortable with this decision appearing in a biography written in 30 years?
- Am I optimizing for this quarter or this decade?
- If I had to hold this position forever, would I still take it?

---

## Mentor's Advice for CEOs

*"I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."* — Warren Buffett

The CEO with a long-term orientation makes different decisions than the CEO optimizing for the next board meeting. They invest in culture, systems, and capability. They build moats. They make decisions they'd be proud of in 20 years.

This isn't about ignoring short-term reality—you have to survive to thrive. But within the constraints of survival, orient toward the long term.
