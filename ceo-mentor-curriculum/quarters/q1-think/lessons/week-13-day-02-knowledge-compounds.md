# Day 2: Knowledge Compounds

**Week 13 | Q1 — THINK | Mentor: Warren Buffett**

> "Read 500 pages every day. That's how knowledge works. It builds up, like compound interest."

---

## The Core Insight

Knowledge compounds like money—each piece of learning builds on what came before. The more you know, the faster you can learn. The broader your base, the more connections you can make. Over decades, this compounding creates enormous intellectual advantage.

Buffett reads 5-6 hours per day. This isn't because he has nothing else to do—it's because he understands that knowledge is his greatest compounding asset.

---

## How Knowledge Compounds

### The Accumulation Effect

**Knowledge builds on knowledge:**
- Each book adds to prior books
- Each concept connects to prior concepts
- Each mental model links to prior models
- Understanding deepens exponentially

### The Connection Effect

**More knowledge = more connections:**
- Two ideas can connect in one way
- Three ideas can connect in three ways
- Ten ideas can connect in 45 ways
- Hundred ideas: nearly 5,000 connections

**The more you know, the more patterns you see.**

### The Speed Effect

**Knowledge accelerates learning:**
- Familiarity speeds reading
- Context aids comprehension
- Mental frameworks enable categorization
- Prior learning reduces new learning time

### The Insight Effect

**Critical mass enables breakthroughs:**
- Enough knowledge creates insight
- Patterns become visible
- Novel connections emerge
- Wisdom develops

## The Latticework of Knowledge

### Munger's Mental Models

Munger advocates for "a latticework of mental models":

**The idea:**
- Learn models from multiple disciplines
- Create a framework that connects them
- Use the latticework to analyze situations
- Better thinking through better models

### Building the Latticework

**Collect models from:**
- Physics (leverage, critical mass, momentum)
- Biology (evolution, ecosystems, adaptation)
- Psychology (biases, motivation, behavior)
- Economics (incentives, supply/demand, opportunity cost)
- History (patterns, cycles, human nature)
- Mathematics (probability, compounding, scale)

### Using the Latticework

**Apply multiple models to each situation:**
- What does physics say?
- What does psychology say?
- What does economics say?
- What does history suggest?

**The more models, the better your analysis.**

- Decisions without context
- Patterns not recognized
- Opportunities missed
- Declining relative advantage
