## The Complete Framework: Biases 9-16

### The Relationship Biases

**Reciprocation** and **Mere Association** shape how we relate to people and information:
- Reciprocation creates obligation cycles—both helpful and harmful
- Association transfers our feelings to connected things, killing honest feedback

**Application:** Build systems that reward messengers of bad news. Evaluate requests independently of favors received.

### The Self-Perception Biases

**Denial**, **Excessive Self-Regard**, and **Over-Optimism** distort how we see ourselves and reality:
- Denial protects us from painful truths
- Self-regard makes us overestimate our abilities
- Over-optimism makes us underestimate challenges

**Application:** Seek external calibration. Apply adjustment factors to plans. Ask what you're not seeing.

### The Evaluation Biases

**Deprival Super-Reaction**, **Social Proof**, and **Contrast Misreaction** distort how we evaluate situations:
- Loss aversion makes us irrationally attached to what we have
- Social proof makes us follow crowds even when wrong
- Contrast makes things look good or bad based on comparison

**Application:** Evaluate absolutely, not relatively. Think independently before checking consensus. Ask: Would I start this today?

## The Bias Interaction Map

### How This Week's Biases Compound

**Denial + Over-Optimism:**
We don't see problems (denial) AND expect better outcomes than warranted (optimism). Double distortion.

**Social Proof + Contrast:**
We follow what others are doing (social proof) AND those actions seem reasonable compared to alternatives (contrast). Cascading conformity.

**Loss Aversion + Denial:**
We can't face the pain of loss (loss aversion) SO we refuse to see that we've lost (denial). Sunk cost escalation.

**Association + Self-Regard:**
We assume our success reflects our ability (self-regard) AND credit ourselves for associated positive outcomes (association). Unearned confidence.

### Breaking the Compound Patterns

Each interaction point is a leverage opportunity:
- External input breaks self-regard-denial-optimism loops
- Absolute evaluation breaks social proof-contrast loops
- Fresh-start thinking breaks loss aversion-denial loops
- Source-blind evaluation breaks association-self-regard loops

## Building Systematic Protection

### Personal Practices

**Daily:**
- Notice when bias might be operating
- Ask "What am I not seeing?"
- Pause before reacting to bad news

**Weekly:**
- Review major decisions for bias influence
- Seek disconfirming information
- Check consensus views for independent validity

**Monthly:**
- External calibration (feedback, data, outside views)
- Audit ongoing investments for sunk cost traps
- Review prediction accuracy to calibrate optimism

### Team Practices

**In meetings:**
- Collect opinions before discussion (prevents cascades)
- Assign devil's advocates
- Separate message from messenger

**In decisions:**
- Evaluate options absolutely, not just comparatively
- Check for social proof driving alignment
- Build in reconsideration triggers

**In culture:**
- Reward bad news delivery
- Celebrate course corrections
- Model admitting mistakes

### Organizational Practices

**Information flow:**
- Anonymous feedback channels
- Regular external reviews
- Metrics that can't be filtered

**Decision processes:**
- Pre-mortems on major initiatives
- Kill criteria set before commitment
- Staged commitments with checkpoints

**Accountability:**
- Track predictions vs. outcomes
- Learn from both success and failure
- Attribute luck and skill appropriately

## Week 5 Summary

Biases 9-16 complete the picture of major cognitive distortions:

**The core insight:** We're wired to seek reciprocity, shoot messengers, deny pain, overestimate ourselves, feel losses intensely, follow crowds, and misjudge by comparison.

**The practical application:**
- Build systems that counteract these tendencies
- Seek external calibration regularly
- Evaluate independently before checking consensus
- Compare to the right baseline (absolute value, not relative comparison)

**The cumulative effect:** Understanding 16 biases provides a comprehensive framework for better judgment. But knowing isn't enough—you must build practices and systems that protect against what you know.
