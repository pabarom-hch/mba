# Product Strategy

## Learning Objectives

- Understand product levels and the total product concept
- Manage products through the lifecycle
- Apply product portfolio analysis
- Design effective product development processes
- Make strategic product line decisions

---

## Introduction

Product is the first "P" of the marketing mix and the foundation of value creation. Product strategy encompasses decisions about what to offer, how to design it, when to launch it, and how to manage it over time. This chapter covers the frameworks for strategic product management.

## The Total Product Concept

### Product Levels

```
LEVELS OF PRODUCT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

              ┌─────────────────────────┐
              │   POTENTIAL PRODUCT     │
              │   Future improvements   │
              │   ┌─────────────────┐   │
              │   │ AUGMENTED       │   │
              │   │ Additional      │   │
              │   │ services        │   │
              │   │ ┌───────────┐   │   │
              │   │ │ EXPECTED  │   │   │
              │   │ │ Normal    │   │   │
              │   │ │ features  │   │   │
              │   │ │┌─────────┐│   │   │
              │   │ ││  CORE   ││   │   │
              │   │ ││ Benefit ││   │   │
              │   │ │└─────────┘│   │   │
              │   │ └───────────┘   │   │
              │   └─────────────────┘   │
              └─────────────────────────┘

LEVEL           DEFINITION              EXAMPLE (Hotel)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Core            Basic benefit sought    Rest/sleep
Expected        Minimum requirements    Bed, bathroom, towels
Augmented       Exceeds expectations    Spa, concierge, bar
Potential       Future possibilities    AI butler, VR tours

IMPLICATION:
Competition typically occurs at augmented level
Augmented becomes expected over time → innovation required
```

### Product Classifications

```
CONSUMER PRODUCTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONVENIENCE PRODUCTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Frequent purchase, low effort
• Low price, wide distribution
• Example: Groceries, magazines, gum

SHOPPING PRODUCTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Compare on price, quality, style
• Less frequent purchase
• Selective distribution
• Example: Furniture, appliances, clothing

SPECIALTY PRODUCTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Unique characteristics, brand loyalty
• Special purchase effort
• Exclusive distribution
• Example: Luxury cars, designer goods

UNSOUGHT PRODUCTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Not actively seeking
• Requires aggressive promotion
• Example: Insurance, cemetery plots

B2B PRODUCTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Type              Examples
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Materials         Raw materials, components
Capital items     Installations, equipment
Supplies          Operating supplies, MRO
Services          Maintenance, consulting
```

## Product Lifecycle

### Lifecycle Stages

```
PRODUCT LIFECYCLE STAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

       Sales                      Maturity
         │                         ___/\___
         │                      __/        \__
         │              Growth /              \
         │                 ___/                \__ Decline
         │              __/                       \___
         │           __/
         │       ___/ Introduction
         │    __/
         │___/
         └───────────────────────────────────────────→ Time

STAGE CHARACTERISTICS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

INTRODUCTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sales:          Low, slow growth
Costs:          High (development, launch)
Profits:        Negative or low
Customers:      Innovators, early adopters
Competition:    Few competitors
Marketing:      Build awareness, trial
Strategy:       Market development

GROWTH:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sales:          Rapid growth
Costs:          Declining (scale effects)
Profits:        Rising, peak margins
Customers:      Early majority
Competition:    Increasing entrants
Marketing:      Maximize share, differentiate
Strategy:       Penetration, extend product line

MATURITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sales:          Peak, then plateau
Costs:          Low
Profits:        Peak volume, declining margins
Customers:      Late majority
Competition:    Stable, consolidating
Marketing:      Defend share, efficiency
Strategy:       Market modification, product upgrade

DECLINE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sales:          Declining
Costs:          Low but increasing per unit
Profits:        Declining
Customers:      Laggards
Competition:    Exiting
Marketing:      Reduce spending, harvest
Strategy:       Harvest or divest
```

```
ADOPTION CURVE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

               __/                           \__

 2.5%    13.5%     34%       34%       16%

Innovators  Early    Early     Late    Laggards
           Adopters  Majority  Majority

THE CHASM (Geoffrey Moore):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gap between Early Adopters and Early Majority

Early Adopters:        Want revolutionary change
Early Majority:        Want evolutionary improvement

To cross the chasm:
• Target specific niche (bowling pin strategy)
• Provide whole product solution
• Establish market leadership in niche
• Use niche as beachhead for broader market
```

## Product Portfolio Management

### BCG Matrix for Products

```
BCG PRODUCT PORTFOLIO:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

              RELATIVE MARKET SHARE
              High            Low
         ┌─────────────┬─────────────┐
Market   │   STARS     │  QUESTION   │
Growth   │   ★         │   MARKS     │
   High  │             │     ?       │
         │ Invest to   │ Invest or   │
         │ maintain    │ divest      │
         ├─────────────┼─────────────┤
   Low   │   CASH      │    DOGS     │
         │   COWS      │     ✗       │
         │   $$$       │             │
         │ Harvest     │ Divest      │
         │ profits     │             │
         └─────────────┴─────────────┘

PORTFOLIO FLOW:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
? → ★ → $$$ → ✗

Question marks need cash to become stars
Stars become cash cows as market matures
Cash cows fund new question marks
Dogs should typically be divested
```

### Product Line Decisions

```
PRODUCT LINE STRATEGIES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

LINE LENGTH DECISIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Line stretching:
• Downward: Add lower-end products
  - Example: Mercedes adding A-Class
  - Risk: Cannibalization, brand dilution

• Upward: Add higher-end products
  - Example: Toyota creating Lexus
  - Risk: Credibility challenge

• Both: Stretch in both directions
  - Example: Marriott brands

Line filling:
• Add items within current range
• Satisfy more customer needs
• Block competitors
• Risk: Over-complexity, confusion

LINE PRUNING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
When to prune:
• Product unprofitable
• Cannibalizing better products
• Consuming disproportionate resources
• No strategic purpose

How to prune:
• Analyze profitability by SKU
• Consider impact on line
• Manage customer transition
• Monitor competitor response

PRODUCT LINE ANALYSIS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Product     Sales    Margin   % of Line   Strategy
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Core A      40%      25%      45%         Maintain
Core B      25%      30%      27%         Grow
Value C     20%      15%      12%         Review
Niche D     10%      45%      11%         Protect
Legacy E     5%      10%       5%         Prune
```

## New Product Development

### NPD Process

```
NEW PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT STAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. IDEA GENERATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sources:
• Customers (needs, complaints)
• Employees (suggestions)
• Competitors (reverse engineering)
• R&D (technology push)
• Suppliers (materials, components)

Methods:
• Brainstorming
• Focus groups
• Lead user analysis
• Open innovation

2. IDEA SCREENING
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Criteria:
• Market attractiveness
• Technical feasibility
• Strategic fit
• Resource requirements

Kill poor ideas early (save resources)
Pass good ideas forward

3. CONCEPT DEVELOPMENT & TESTING
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Develop product concept statement
• Create prototype or description
• Test with target customers
• Gauge purchase intent

4. MARKETING STRATEGY DEVELOPMENT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Define target market
• Plan positioning
• Set sales/profit goals
• Develop marketing mix

5. BUSINESS ANALYSIS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Estimate sales volume
• Project costs
• Calculate profitability
• Go/no-go decision

6. PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Build actual product
• R&D and engineering
• Create prototypes
• Test functionality

7. TEST MARKETING
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Options:
• Simulated test market
• Controlled test market
• Full test market
• Online A/B testing

8. COMMERCIALIZATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• When: Timing decision
• Where: Geographic rollout
• To whom: Target sequencing
• How: Launch strategy
```

### Stage-Gate Process

```
STAGE-GATE MODEL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

   Stage 1    Stage 2    Stage 3    Stage 4    Stage 5
   Scoping    Build      Develop    Testing    Launch
              Case
      │          │          │          │          │
      ▼          ▼          ▼          ▼          ▼
   ┌─────┐   ┌─────┐   ┌─────┐   ┌─────┐   ┌─────┐
───│Gate │───│Gate │───│Gate │───│Gate │───│Gate │───►
   │  1  │   │  2  │   │  3  │   │  4  │   │  5  │
   └─────┘   └─────┘   └─────┘   └─────┘   └─────┘
      │          │          │          │          │
      ▼          ▼          ▼          ▼          ▼
   GO/KILL   GO/KILL   GO/KILL   GO/KILL   GO/KILL

GATE DECISIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Go:       Project passes, move to next stage
Kill:     Project terminated
Hold:     Wait for more information
Recycle:  Redo previous stage

GATE CRITERIA:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Must meet: Mandatory criteria (knockout questions)
• Should meet: Highly desirable (scoring questions)
• Financial hurdles: ROI, NPV thresholds
```

## Brand and Product Architecture

### Branding Strategies

```
BRAND ARCHITECTURE OPTIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

BRANDED HOUSE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
One master brand across all products

Example: Google
• Google Search
• Google Maps
• Google Drive
• Google Cloud

Pros: Leverage, efficiency, clarity
Cons: Risk concentration, flexibility limits

HOUSE OF BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Different brands for different products

Example: P&G
• Tide, Pampers, Gillette, Crest
• (P&G hidden from consumer)

Pros: Target different segments, risk isolation
Cons: Expensive, no leverage

ENDORSED BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sub-brands linked to parent

Example: Marriott
• Courtyard by Marriott
• Residence Inn by Marriott
• W Hotels (Marriott)

Pros: Balance leverage and flexibility
Cons: Complex architecture

SUB-BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Parent brand with descriptive modifier

Example: Apple
• Apple iPhone
• Apple Mac
• Apple Watch

Pros: Clear connection, product identity
Cons: Everything affects master brand
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Think in product levels** - Core to potential, compete at augmented
2. **Manage the lifecycle** - Adapt strategy to stage
3. **Balance the portfolio** - Stars, cash cows, question marks
4. **Innovate systematically** - Stage-gate discipline
5. **Prune ruthlessly** - Complexity is costly
6. **Extend carefully** - Protect brand equity
