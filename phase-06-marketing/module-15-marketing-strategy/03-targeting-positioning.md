# Targeting and Positioning

## Learning Objectives

- Evaluate and select target market segments
- Develop compelling positioning strategies
- Create effective positioning statements
- Use perceptual mapping for competitive analysis
- Avoid common positioning mistakes

---

## Introduction

After segmenting the market, marketers must decide which segments to target and how to position their offerings within those segments. Targeting determines where to compete; positioning determines how to win. Together, they form the strategic foundation for all marketing mix decisions.

---

## Target Market Selection

### Targeting Strategies

```
TARGETING APPROACH OPTIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

UNDIFFERENTIATED (Mass Marketing):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                 TOTAL MARKET                     │
│                 One offer for all                │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Example: Commodities, utilities
Advantage: Scale economies
Disadvantage: Vulnerable to segmented competitors

DIFFERENTIATED (Multi-segment):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
┌──────────────┐ ┌──────────────┐ ┌──────────────┐
│  Segment A   │ │  Segment B   │ │  Segment C   │
│  Offer A     │ │  Offer B     │ │  Offer C     │
└──────────────┘ └──────────────┘ └──────────────┘

Example: P&G (multiple detergent brands)
Advantage: More total sales, stronger positions
Disadvantage: Higher costs, complexity

CONCENTRATED (Niche):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
┌──────────────┐ ┌──────────────┐ ┌──────────────┐
│              │ │  Segment B   │ │              │
│  (ignored)   │ │  All focus   │ │  (ignored)   │
└──────────────┘ └──────────────┘ └──────────────┘

Example: Rolex (luxury watches only)
Advantage: Deep expertise, strong position
Disadvantage: Risk of segment decline

MICRO-MARKETING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Local marketing:    Customize to local markets
Individual:         One-to-one customization

Example: Amazon recommendations, Nike ID
Advantage: Perfect fit
Disadvantage: Complexity, cost
```

### Target Segment Selection Process

```
TARGETING DECISION FRAMEWORK:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STEP 1: SCREEN FOR BASIC CRITERIA
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Eliminate segments that:
□ Are too small to serve profitably
□ Are declining without turnaround potential
□ Cannot be reached with current resources
□ Conflict with company values/strategy

STEP 2: ASSESS ATTRACTIVENESS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rate each segment on:
□ Market size and growth
□ Competitive intensity
□ Profit potential
□ Accessibility

STEP 3: ASSESS CAPABILITY FIT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rate your ability to:
□ Meet segment needs better than competition
□ Reach segment cost-effectively
□ Defend position over time

STEP 4: SELECT STRATEGY
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Based on resources and segments:
□ Single segment: Focus on best opportunity
□ Selective specialization: 2-3 unrelated segments
□ Product specialization: One product, many segments
□ Market specialization: One segment, many products
□ Full coverage: All segments with different offers
```

## Positioning Strategy

### What Is Positioning?

```
POSITIONING DEFINITION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

"The act of designing a company's offering and
image to occupy a distinctive place in the mind
of the target market."
                        — Philip Kotler

KEY PRINCIPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Positioning happens in the customer's mind
• It's relative to competition
• Must be believable and deliverable
• Should be sustainable over time
• Expressed through all marketing mix elements

POSITIONING VS. BRAND:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Positioning: The intended perception
Brand: The actual perception and relationship

THE POSITIONING GOAL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
               GENERIC                   UNIQUE
                  │                         │
                  ▼                         ▼
    "They're all the same"    "This is different and
                               better for me"
                                     │
                                     ▼
                              PREFERENCE
                                     │
                                     ▼
                               PURCHASE
```

### Positioning Dimensions

```
POSITIONING BASIS OPTIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PRODUCT ATTRIBUTES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on specific features
Example: Gillette (closest shave)
Risk: Can be copied, narrow focus

BENEFITS DELIVERED:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on what customer gets
Example: Volvo (safety)
Risk: Must deliver consistently

PRICE/QUALITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on value equation
Example: Walmart (always low prices)
Risk: Commoditization pressure

USAGE OCCASION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on when/where product used
Example: Gatorade (during exercise)
Risk: Limited occasions

USER TYPE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on who uses product
Example: Apple (creative professionals)
Risk: Excludes other potential users

COMPETITOR COMPARISON:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on vs. specific rival
Example: Avis (We try harder)
Risk: Gives competitor attention

PRODUCT CATEGORY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on category membership
Example: 7-Up (the uncola)
Risk: Confusing if overdone

CULTURAL SYMBOL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on imagery and associations
Example: Marlboro (cowboy, freedom)
Risk: Can seem inauthentic
```

### Positioning Statement

```
POSITIONING STATEMENT TEMPLATE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

For [target segment]
Who [needs/wants statement]
[Brand] is the [competitive frame of reference]
That [key benefit/point of difference]
Because [reason to believe]

EXAMPLE - VOLVO:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For families with children
Who value safety above all
Volvo is the premium automobile brand
That provides the safest driving experience
Because Volvo invented key safety features and
has the lowest accident fatality rate

EXAMPLE - SLACK:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For knowledge workers in teams
Who need to communicate and collaborate efficiently
Slack is the workplace communication platform
That replaces email with organized, searchable channels
Because it integrates with 2,000+ apps and reduces
email volume by 48%

POSITIONING STATEMENT COMPONENTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Component               Question Answered
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Target                  Who are we talking to?
Need                    What problem do they have?
Brand                   What is our offering?
Frame                   What category are we in?
Benefit                 Why should they choose us?
RTB                     Why should they believe us?
```

### Points of Parity and Difference

```
POP AND POD FRAMEWORK:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

POINTS OF PARITY (POP):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Associations not unique to brand but necessary for
legitimate category membership

Category POPs:
Things you must have to be considered
Example: For a luxury car—leather, performance, quality

Competitive POPs:
Things that neutralize competitor advantages
Example: Apple Watch adding fitness = neutralizes Fitbit

POINTS OF DIFFERENCE (POD):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unique attributes/benefits that customers associate
strongly with brand and believe they cannot find
elsewhere

POD Requirements:
• Desirable: Customers want it
• Deliverable: You can provide it
• Differentiated: Competitors don't offer it

THE POP/POD BALANCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
           ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
           │         POINTS OF PARITY        │
           │    (Category membership)        │
           │                                 │
           │  ┌───────────────────────────┐  │
           │  │   POINTS OF DIFFERENCE   │  │
           │  │   (Competitive advantage) │  │
           │  │                           │  │
           │  │      UNIQUE VALUE         │  │
           │  │      PROPOSITION          │  │
           │  │                           │  │
           │  └───────────────────────────┘  │
           │                                 │
           └─────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Perceptual Mapping

### Creating Perceptual Maps

```
PERCEPTUAL MAP PROCESS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STEP 1: IDENTIFY KEY DIMENSIONS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Choose 2 attributes most important to customers
• Usually from customer research
• Dimensions should be independent
• Common: price-quality, modern-traditional

STEP 2: RATE COMPETITORS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Survey customers or use expert judgment
Rate each competitor on each dimension (1-10 scale)

STEP 3: PLOT POSITIONS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Create 2D map with dimensions as axes
Plot each competitor's position

STEP 4: ANALYZE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Identify clusters (competitive groups)
• Find white space (potential opportunities)
• Assess your position vs. competitors
• Determine ideal customer preferences

EXAMPLE - AUTOMOBILE MARKET:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
               LUXURY
                  │
       Mercedes ★│    ★ BMW
                  │         ★ Lexus
                  │
    PRACTICAL ────┼──────────────── SPORTY
                  │
         ★ Honda │  ★ Volkswagen
                  │
      ★ Toyota   │        ★ Mazda
                  │
                VALUE
```

### Repositioning

```
REPOSITIONING STRATEGIES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHEN TO REPOSITION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Current position no longer differentiated
• Market preferences have shifted
• New segment more attractive
• Competitive position untenable
• Brand has become stale/irrelevant

REPOSITIONING OPTIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Change product to match position
   Example: Domino's improving pizza quality

2. Change position to match product
   Example: Old Spice → younger, edgy

3. Change customer perception of product
   Example: Arm & Hammer (baking → deodorizer)

4. Change customer beliefs about competitors
   Example: Comparative advertising

5. Find new position dimension
   Example: Method (design-forward cleaning)

REPOSITIONING RISKS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Alienate existing customers
• Confuse the market
• Fail to establish new position
• Competitor claims vacated position
• Implementation inconsistency
```

## Positioning in Practice

### Positioning Development Process

```
POSITIONING DEVELOPMENT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PHASE 1: DISCOVER
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Customer research:
• What do they value?
• How do they perceive us vs. competitors?
• What unmet needs exist?

Competitive analysis:
• How are competitors positioned?
• What positions are crowded vs. open?
• What are competitors' vulnerabilities?

Internal assessment:
• What can we credibly claim?
• What are our genuine strengths?
• What can we sustain over time?

PHASE 2: DEVELOP
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Generate positioning options:
• Multiple positioning statements
• Different frames of reference
• Various benefits/reasons to believe

Evaluate options:
• Differentiation: Is it unique?
• Relevance: Do customers care?
• Credibility: Can we deliver?
• Sustainability: Can we maintain?

PHASE 3: DEPLOY
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Translate to marketing mix:
• Product: Features that deliver position
• Price: Consistent with position
• Place: Channels that reinforce position
• Promotion: Messages that communicate position

Execute consistently:
• Internal alignment
• Customer touchpoint consistency
• Long-term commitment
```

### Common Positioning Mistakes

```
POSITIONING PITFALLS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

UNDER-POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Problem: Vague, unclear positioning
Symptom: "We're a technology company"
Fix: Be specific and distinctive

OVER-POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Problem: Too narrow, limiting
Symptom: Known for one thing, can't extend
Fix: Find position with room to grow

CONFUSED POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Problem: Inconsistent claims
Symptom: Mixed messages across touchpoints
Fix: Single-minded focus, integrated execution

IRRELEVANT POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Problem: Position customers don't care about
Symptom: Differentiated but not preferred
Fix: Research what actually matters

IMPLAUSIBLE POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Problem: Claims customers don't believe
Symptom: Skepticism, distrust
Fix: Build credibility before claiming

POSITIONING CHECKLIST:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
□ Is it relevant to target customers?
□ Is it differentiated from competition?
□ Is it credible (can we deliver)?
□ Is it sustainable (hard to copy)?
□ Is it consistent across all touchpoints?
□ Is it motivating (drives preference)?
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Choose targeting carefully** - Match resources to segment attractiveness
2. **Positioning is mental** - It happens in the customer's mind
3. **Be distinctive** - Parity gets you in; difference makes you win
4. **Use positioning statements** - Crisp articulation guides execution
5. **Map perceptions** - Visualize competitive positions and opportunities
6. **Stay consistent** - Position must be maintained across all touchpoints
