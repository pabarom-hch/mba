# Market Analysis

## Learning Objectives

- Conduct comprehensive market analysis
- Size markets using multiple approaches
- Analyze customer needs and behavior
- Assess competitive dynamics
- Identify market opportunities and threats

---

## Introduction

Market analysis forms the foundation of marketing strategy. Before developing products, setting prices, or designing promotions, marketers must understand the market landscape: Who are the customers? What do they need? How big is the opportunity? What are competitors doing? This chapter covers the frameworks and methods for rigorous market analysis.

## Market Sizing

### Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up

```
MARKET SIZING APPROACHES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TOP-DOWN (TAM → SAM → SOM):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Start with total market, narrow to addressable

TAM (Total Addressable Market)
│   Total global market for product category
│   Example: $500B global software market
│
├── SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)
│   │   Segment you can reach (geography, capability)
│   │   Example: $50B US enterprise software
│   │
│   └── SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)
│       │   Realistic share you can capture
│       │   Example: $500M (1% of SAM)
│
PROS: Quick, uses available data
CONS: May miss local dynamics, often overstates

BOTTOM-UP:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Build from unit economics upward

Number of potential customers
× Adoption rate
× Average purchase value
× Purchase frequency
= Market size

Example:
10,000 potential enterprise customers in target
× 30% likely to adopt this software category
× $100,000 average annual contract
× 1 purchase per year
= $300M addressable market

PROS: More accurate, grounded in specifics
CONS: Requires primary research, time-consuming
```

### Market Sizing Framework

```
MARKET SIZING PROCESS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STEP 1: DEFINE THE MARKET
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• What problem does the product solve?
• Who has this problem?
• What alternatives exist?
• Geographic scope?
• Time horizon?

STEP 2: SEGMENT THE MARKET
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Break into logical segments:
• By customer type (consumer, business, government)
• By use case (enterprise, SMB, individual)
• By geography
• By price point

STEP 3: SIZE EACH SEGMENT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For each segment, estimate:
• Number of potential buyers
• Average purchase amount
• Purchase frequency
• Growth rate

STEP 4: VALIDATE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cross-check with:
• Industry reports
• Competitor revenues
• Analyst estimates
• Customer research
```

### Example: SaaS Market Sizing

```
EXAMPLE: PROJECT MANAGEMENT SOFTWARE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TOP-DOWN:
Global productivity software market:          $50B
Project management share:                     × 15%
                                             ─────
Total market:                                $7.5B
North America share:                         × 40%
                                             ─────
North America market:                        $3.0B
SMB segment (target):                        × 35%
                                             ─────
Target market:                               $1.05B

BOTTOM-UP:
SMB companies in North America:              2,000,000
Have 10+ employees (need PM tools):          × 40%
                                             ─────
Potential customers:                         800,000
Likely to use paid software:                 × 50%
                                             ─────
Addressable customers:                       400,000
Average annual spend:                        × $2,500
                                             ─────
Total addressable market:                    $1.0B

TRIANGULATION:
Both approaches suggest ~$1B market
Provides confidence in estimate
```

## Customer Analysis

### Understanding Customer Needs

```
CUSTOMER NEEDS FRAMEWORK:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

JOBS TO BE DONE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What job is the customer hiring the product to do?

Types of jobs:
• Functional: Get something done (drill hole)
• Emotional: Feel a certain way (confident)
• Social: Be perceived by others (successful)

Example - Hiring a consultant:
• Functional: Solve a business problem
• Emotional: Reduce anxiety about decision
• Social: Show board you're taking action

CUSTOMER VALUE HIERARCHY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                    ┌─────────────┐
                    │   Delight   │ Unexpected benefits
                    └──────┬──────┘ (create loyalty)
              ┌────────────▼────────────┐
              │       Desired          │ Expected benefits
              └────────────┬───────────┘ (differentiate)
         ┌─────────────────▼─────────────────┐
         │           Required               │ Must have
         └─────────────────┬────────────────┘ (table stakes)
    ┌──────────────────────▼──────────────────────┐
    │                  Basic                      │ Assumed
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘ (invisible)

NEEDS VS. WANTS VS. DEMANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Needs:    Basic requirements (transportation)
Wants:    Specific form of need (a car)
Demands:  Wants backed by purchasing power (Tesla Model 3)
```

```
RESEARCH METHODS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

RESEARCH PROCESS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

2. Choose methodology
3. Design instruments
4. Collect data
5. Analyze findings
6. Generate insights
7. Make recommendations
```

## Competitive Analysis

### Competitor Identification

```
COMPETITOR TYPES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DIRECT COMPETITORS:
Same product, same customer segment
Example: Coca-Cola vs. Pepsi

INDIRECT COMPETITORS:
Different product, same need
Example: Coca-Cola vs. Coffee

POTENTIAL COMPETITORS:
Could enter your market
• Adjacent market players
• Vertically integrating suppliers/customers
• New entrants with capital

SUBSTITUTE COMPETITORS:
Different category, same job-to-be-done
Example: Coca-Cola vs. Water vs. Energy drinks

COMPETITOR SET MAPPING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                    MOST DIRECT
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Size markets both ways** - Top-down and bottom-up triangulation
2. **Understand jobs to be done** - Functional, emotional, social
3. **Map the customer journey** - Identify pain points and opportunities
4. **Know your competitors** - Direct, indirect, and potential
5. **Assess opportunities rigorously** - Attractiveness × capability fit
6. **Track trends systematically** - Impact and timing matter
