# Pricing Strategy

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the economics and psychology of pricing
- Apply different pricing methods and strategies
- Design pricing architecture for product lines
- Execute price changes effectively
- Manage competitive pricing dynamics

---

## Introduction

Price is the only marketing mix element that generates revenue—all others represent costs. Pricing decisions directly affect profitability and must balance value capture, competitive positioning, and customer perception. This chapter covers pricing frameworks, from cost-based fundamentals to advanced value-based approaches.

```

                    CUSTOMER VALUE

                          ▲
                         ╱ ╲
                        ╱   ╲
                       ╱     ╲

                    ╱   ZONE    ╲

PRICING ZONE DETERMINANTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Floor:    Variable costs (break-even minimum)
```

### Pricing Objectives

```
PRICING OBJECTIVES HIERARCHY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SURVIVAL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Short-term objective
• Cover variable costs
• Stay in business during crisis
• Price at or below cost

PROFIT MAXIMIZATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Maximize current profits
• May sacrifice long-term position
• Optimal when margin > share goals

MARKET SHARE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Penetration pricing
• Build scale and experience
• Drive down costs
• May sacrifice short-term profits

MARKET SKIMMING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• High initial price
• Capture value from early adopters
• Gradually reduce price
• Works when quality signals value

QUALITY LEADERSHIP:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Price signals premium quality
• Maintain image and margins
• Supports R&D investment

OBJECTIVE ALIGNMENT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Objective           Price Strategy
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Survival            Low, cover costs
Max profit          Optimize margin × volume
Max revenue         Volume priority
Market share        Penetration, aggressive
Quality leader      Premium pricing
```

## Pricing Methods

### Cost-Based Pricing

```
COST-BASED PRICING APPROACHES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

COST-PLUS (MARKUP) PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Price = Unit Cost × (1 + Markup %)

Example:
Unit cost:          $50
Desired markup:     40%
Price:              $50 × 1.40 = $70

Pros: Simple, ensures margin
Cons: Ignores demand, competition

TARGET RETURN PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Price = Unit Cost + (Desired Return × Invested Capital)
                    ──────────────────────────────────
                         Unit Sales

Example:
Unit cost:          $50
Invested capital:   $1,000,000
Target return:      20%
Expected units:     50,000

Price = $50 + ($200,000 / 50,000) = $54

BREAK-EVEN ANALYSIS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Break-even volume = Fixed Costs / (Price - Variable Cost)

Example:
Fixed costs:        $500,000
Variable cost:      $30
Price:              $50
Break-even:         $500,000 / ($50-$30) = 25,000 units

BREAK-EVEN CHART:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
$                Total Revenue
│                    ╱
│                   ╱    Profit
│                  ╱     Zone
│                 ╱─────────────
│                ╱ Break-even
│               ╱  point
│              ╱
│        Loss ╱  Total Cost
│        Zone╱───────────────
│           ╱    Fixed Costs
│──────────/─────────────────
└─────────────────────────────→ Volume
```

### Competition-Based Pricing

```
COMPETITIVE PRICING APPROACHES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

GOING-RATE PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Price at or near competitor levels
• At market: Same as competitors
• Above market: Premium position
• Below market: Value position

When to use:
• Commodity markets
• High price transparency
• Similar offerings

COMPETITIVE BIDDING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Price to win contracts (B2B)

Expected profit = Profit if win × Probability of winning

Bid lower:  Higher win probability, lower profit
Bid higher: Lower win probability, higher profit

Optimal bid: Balance probability and profit

PRICE LEADERSHIP:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Leader sets price, others follow
• Barometric: One firm moves first
• Dominant: Largest firm sets price
• Collusive: Implicit coordination (often illegal)

COMPETITIVE PRICE MONITORING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Track competitor prices:
• Direct observation
• Mystery shopping
• Price tracking services
• Customer feedback
```

### Value-Based Pricing

```
VALUE-BASED PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CORE PRINCIPLE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Price based on customer perceived value, not cost

Value-based:  What is it worth to customer?
Cost-based:   What did it cost us?

VALUE PRICING PROCESS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Understand customer needs
2. Quantify value delivered
3. Set price to capture share of value
4. Communicate value to justify price

ECONOMIC VALUE ANALYSIS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total Economic Value = Reference Value + Differentiation Value

Reference value:     Price of next best alternative
Differentiation:     Value of differences (+/-)

Example - Industrial Equipment:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Reference product price:                    $100,000

Positive differentiators:
• 20% energy savings ($10,000/year × 5 years)  +$50,000
• Lower maintenance ($3,000/year × 5 years)    +$15,000
• Higher output (5% more production)           +$25,000

Negative differentiators:
• Training required                            -$5,000

Total Economic Value:                          $185,000

Price capture (60% of differentiation value):  $154,000

VALUE COMMUNICATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Don't just set value price—communicate it
• Quantify benefits in customer terms
• Show total cost of ownership
• Provide ROI calculator
• Offer risk-reduction guarantees
```

## Pricing Psychology

### Psychological Pricing Tactics

```
PSYCHOLOGICAL PRICING EFFECTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CHARM PRICING (9-ENDING):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
$9.99 perceived as significantly less than $10.00
Left-digit effect: Focus on leftmost digit
Works best for low-involvement purchases

PRESTIGE PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Round numbers ($100 vs. $99) signal quality
Used for luxury, premium goods
Avoid 9-endings for prestige products

REFERENCE PRICE EFFECTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• External reference: "Compare at $100"
• Anchor high, discount to target
• Show original price crossed out

PRICE-QUALITY INFERENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Higher price → assumed higher quality
Especially when:
• Quality hard to evaluate before purchase
• Limited product knowledge
• Risk of poor choice is high

FAIRNESS PERCEPTIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unfair pricing triggers strong negative reaction
Perceived unfair:
• Price increases without cost justification
• Surge pricing during emergencies
• Different prices for same customer

DECOY EFFECT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Add inferior option to make target look better

Example:
• Small coffee: $2.50
• Medium coffee: $4.00  ← Target (looks good vs. large)
• Large coffee: $4.25   ← Decoy (slightly more, not worth it)
```

### Framing and Presentation

```
PRICE FRAMING TECHNIQUES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

BUNDLING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Combine products at single price
• Obscures individual prices
• Captures more consumer surplus
• Increases perceived value

Types:
• Pure bundle: Only available together
• Mixed bundle: Available separately or together

UNBUNDLING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Separate components for separate pricing
• Base product cheaper (attracts)
• Add-ons provide margin
• Example: Airline seat + baggage + food

TEMPORAL FRAMING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"Just $1 per day" vs. "$365 per year"
Smaller numbers feel more affordable
Match framing to purchase psychology

PERCENTAGE VS. DOLLAR:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rule of 100:
• Under $100: Use percentage ("20% off!")
• Over $100: Use dollars ("Save $50!")
Larger-looking number has more impact

PAYMENT TIMING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pain of paying is real
• Prepayment: Decouple pain from consumption
• Subscription: Reduce transaction frequency
• Financing: Spread pain over time
```

## Pricing Strategies

### New Product Pricing

```
NEW PRODUCT PRICING STRATEGIES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MARKET SKIMMING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Start high, reduce over time

When to use:
• Sufficient demand at high price
• High price signals quality
• Competitors cannot enter quickly
• Costs don't decline with volume

Example: New iPhone launch pricing
$1,200 → $999 → $799 (as new model launches)

MARKET PENETRATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Start low to build market share

When to use:
• Price-sensitive market
• Strong economies of scale
• Keeps competitors out
• Market share drives long-term profit

Example: Netflix early pricing
$8/month → acquire subscribers → raise prices later

STRATEGY COMPARISON:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                Skimming        Penetration
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Initial price   High            Low
Profit timing   Front-loaded    Back-loaded
Market share    Lower           Higher
Competition     Attracts entry  Deters entry
Risk profile    Lower           Higher
Best for        Innovation      Commodities
```

### Competitive Pricing Strategies

```
COMPETITIVE PRICING POSITIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PREMIUM PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Price above market
• Requires genuine differentiation
• Communicates quality
• Higher margins
• Smaller volume

ECONOMY PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Price well below market
• Minimal marketing/features
• High volume focus
• Cost leadership required
• Vulnerable to cost disruption

VALUE PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Good quality at fair price
• Middle market position
• Efficiency in value delivery
• Broad appeal

GOOD-BETTER-BEST:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tiered pricing at different levels

Example - SaaS:
Good:   Basic     $10/month   Core features
Better: Pro       $25/month   Advanced features
Best:   Enterprise Custom     Full platform

Benefits:
• Captures different willingness to pay
• Anchoring effects (best makes better look good)
• Self-selection by customers
```

### Dynamic Pricing

```
DYNAMIC PRICING APPROACHES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TIME-BASED PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vary price by time period
• Peak vs. off-peak (utilities, transport)
• Happy hour discounts
• Seasonal pricing

DEMAND-BASED PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Adjust price to demand level
• Yield management (airlines, hotels)
• Real-time adjustments
• Algorithms optimize revenue

SURGE PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Increase price during high demand
• Uber/Lyft model
• Balances supply and demand
• Risk: Customer backlash

PERSONALIZED PRICING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Different prices for different customers
• Based on purchase history
• Willingness to pay signals
• Ethical and legal considerations

DYNAMIC PRICING CONSIDERATIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pro:  Maximize revenue, balance demand
Con:  Fairness concerns, trust erosion
Key:  Transparency, clear rationale
```

## Price Implementation

### Price Changes

```
PRICE INCREASE IMPLEMENTATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHEN TO INCREASE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Costs have increased
• Demand exceeds supply
• Inflation environment
• Value has increased
• Competitor prices risen

HOW TO INCREASE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Reduce discounting
• Unbundle and charge separately
• Reduce package size (shrinkflation)
• Substitute lower-cost ingredients
• Direct price increase (last resort)

COMMUNICATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Announce in advance
• Justify with cost/value reasons
• Emphasize value, not just price
• Offer grandfathering for loyal customers

PRICE DECREASE IMPLEMENTATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHEN TO DECREASE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Excess capacity
• Competitive pressure
• Market share goals
• Cost reductions enable

RISKS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Price war escalation
• Quality perception damage
• Profit erosion
• Difficult to reverse
```

### Discount Management

```
DISCOUNT TYPES AND PURPOSES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FUNCTIONAL DISCOUNTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trade discounts:     For channel members
Promotional:         For advertising/display
Service:             For inventory/delivery

QUANTITY DISCOUNTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Volume:              More units = lower price
Cumulative:          Based on total purchases over time
Non-cumulative:      Single order based

CASH DISCOUNTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Early payment:       2/10, net 30
Reduces receivables risk

SEASONAL DISCOUNTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Off-peak:            Smooth demand
Pre-season:          Shift timing

DISCOUNT WATERFALL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
List price                           $100.00
- Standard discount (10%)             -$10.00
Invoice price                         $90.00
- Volume rebate (5%)                   -$4.50
- Early payment (2%)                   -$1.71
- Promotional support                  -$3.00
Pocket price                          $80.79
Pocket margin                          19.2%

DISCOUNT DISCIPLINE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Track pocket price, not list price
• Limit discount authority
• Tie discounts to behaviors (volume, early pay)
• Review and prune regularly
```

## Price Wars and Competition

### Managing Price Competition

```
PRICE WAR DYNAMICS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PRICE WAR TRIGGERS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Excess capacity
• New entrant disruption
• Market share ambitions
• Cost breakthrough
• Desperation moves

PRICE WAR CONSEQUENCES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Margin destruction
• Customer expectations reset
• Brand value erosion
• Winner's curse

AVOIDING PRICE WARS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Differentiate: Make price less relevant
• Signal: Communicate commitment to margins
• Selective response: Match only where you must
• Add value: Bundle services with products
• Focus: Compete on value, not price

RESPONDING TO PRICE ATTACKS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Decision tree:
1. Is competitor committed or testing?
   → Testing: Don't respond

2. Will it significantly impact us?
   → No: Ignore

3. Can we match profitably?
   → No: Add value instead

4. Will matching escalate?
   → Yes: Respond selectively
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Price affects profit directly** - 1% price increase = 8-11% profit increase
2. **Value-based beats cost-based** - Capture customer willingness to pay
3. **Psychology matters** - Reference prices, framing, fairness
4. **Match strategy to situation** - Skim vs. penetrate, premium vs. value
5. **Discount discipline** - Watch pocket price, not list price
6. **Avoid price wars** - Compete on value, not just price
