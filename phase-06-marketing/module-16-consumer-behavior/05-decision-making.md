# Consumer Decision Making

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the consumer decision-making process
- Apply behavioral economics to marketing
- Recognize cognitive biases and heuristics
- Design effective choice architecture
- Influence decisions ethically

---

## Introduction

Consumer decision-making ranges from automatic, habitual choices to complex, deliberative processes. Understanding how consumers actually make decisions—often irrationally—enables marketers to design products, prices, and communications that align with real human psychology rather than idealized rational models.

---

## Decision-Making Models

### Rational vs. Behavioral Model

```
TRADITIONAL RATIONAL MODEL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Assumptions:
• Consumers have perfect information
• Unlimited cognitive capacity
• Well-defined preferences
• Maximize utility

Reality:
• Information is limited
• Cognitive capacity is bounded
• Preferences are constructed
• Satisficing, not maximizing

BEHAVIORAL MODEL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
              ┌─────────────────┐
              │    SYSTEM 1     │ Fast, automatic
              │   (Intuitive)   │ Emotional
              │                 │ Effortless
              └────────┬────────┘
                       │
                       ▼ (Sometimes triggers)
              ┌─────────────────┐
              │    SYSTEM 2     │ Slow, deliberate
              │   (Rational)    │ Logical
              │                 │ Effortful
              └─────────────────┘

KAHNEMAN'S DUAL SYSTEM:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
System 1:    Handles 95%+ of decisions
             Fast, intuitive, emotional
             Uses heuristics (mental shortcuts)
             Prone to biases

System 2:    Handles complex decisions
             Slow, deliberate, rational
             Effortful, depletes energy
             Called upon when needed
```

### Decision Types

```
DECISION TYPE SPECTRUM:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

HABITUAL DECISIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Characteristics:
• Low involvement
• Repeat purchase
• Little search
• Automatic

Examples:
• Regular grocery items
• Morning coffee
• Gas station

Marketing:
• Maintain availability
• Reinforce habit
• Defend against switching

LIMITED PROBLEM SOLVING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Characteristics:
• Moderate involvement
• Some search
• Known category
• Compare few options

Examples:
• Clothing
• Restaurant choice
• Electronics

Marketing:
• Easy comparison
• Clear differentiation
• Point of purchase influence

EXTENDED PROBLEM SOLVING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Characteristics:
• High involvement
• Extensive search
• Unknown category
• Many options evaluated

Examples:
• Car purchase
• Home buying
• College selection

Marketing:
• Information provision
• Reduce perceived risk
• Build consideration
```

## Heuristics and Biases

### Common Heuristics

```
DECISION HEURISTICS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AVAILABILITY HEURISTIC:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Judge probability by ease of recall
• Recent events seem more likely
• Vivid events more influential
• Familiar brands preferred

Marketing use:
• Stay top-of-mind
• Memorable advertising
• Frequent exposure

REPRESENTATIVENESS HEURISTIC:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Judge based on similarity to prototype
• Stereotyping
• Category expectations
• Packaging signals

Marketing use:
• Meet category expectations
• Or deliberately violate for differentiation
• Use appropriate symbols

ANCHORING AND ADJUSTMENT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Start from initial value, adjust insufficiently
• First price sets expectations
• Reference points matter
• "Was $100, now $60"

Marketing use:
• Set high anchors
• Show "regular" price
• MSRP as reference

AFFECT HEURISTIC:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Use current feelings as basis for judgment
• "How do I feel about this?"
• Mood affects decisions
• Emotional associations

Marketing use:
• Create positive feelings
• Emotional advertising
• Pleasant experiences
```

### Cognitive Biases

```
KEY COGNITIVE BIASES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

LOSS AVERSION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Losses feel ~2x worse than equivalent gains

Implications:
• "Don't miss out" > "Save now"
• Framing matters (gain vs. loss)
• Endowment effect (overvalue owned items)

STATUS QUO BIAS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Preference for current state

Implications:
• Default options powerful
• Switching costs not just financial
• Inertia is real

CONFIRMATION BIAS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Seek information confirming beliefs

Implications:
• Hard to change attitudes
• Echo chambers
• Provide confirming evidence

OPTIMISM BIAS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Overestimate positive outcomes for self

Implications:
• "It won't happen to me"
• Underestimate risks
• Insurance, prevention hard sells

PRESENT BIAS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Prefer immediate over future rewards

Implications:
• Instant gratification wins
• Saving is hard
• Immediate benefits emphasized

SUNK COST FALLACY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Continue because of past investment

Implications:
• Loyalty from past purchases
• Difficult to abandon
• "I've come this far"
```

## Choice Architecture

### Designing Choices

```
CHOICE ARCHITECTURE PRINCIPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEFAULTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pre-selected options are usually kept
• Opt-out > Opt-in
• 401k enrollment
• Email subscriptions

REDUCE FRICTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Make desired behavior easy
• Fewer clicks to buy
• Pre-filled forms
• One-click ordering

INCREASE FRICTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Make undesired behavior hard
• Multiple confirmations to cancel
• Cooling-off periods
• "Are you sure?"

SIMPLIFY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Reduce complexity in choices
• Fewer options
• Clear comparisons
• Highlight key differences

MAKE IT SOCIAL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Show what others are doing
• "Most popular choice"
• Reviews and ratings
• Social proof

TIMELY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Prompt at decision moment
• Checkout suggestions
• Post-purchase recommendations
• Contextual offers
```

### Nudge Theory

```
NUDGE FRAMEWORK (THALER & SUNSTEIN):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEFINITION:
"Any aspect of the choice architecture that alters
behavior in a predictable way without forbidding
any options or significantly changing their
economic incentives."

LIBERTARIAN PATERNALISM:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Guide good decisions while preserving choice

NUDGE EXAMPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Context             Nudge
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Healthy eating      Fruit at eye level
Savings            Auto-enrollment
Energy             Neighbor comparison
Organ donation     Opt-out default
Retirement         Escalating contributions

ETHICAL NUDGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
✓ Transparent (not hidden)
✓ Easy to opt-out
✓ In consumer's interest
✓ Don't exploit vulnerabilities

DARK PATTERNS (Avoid):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
✗ Hidden costs
✗ Forced continuity
✗ Roach motel (easy in, hard out)
✗ Misdirection
✗ Shame ("Are you sure you don't care?")
```

### Choice Overload

```
PARADOX OF CHOICE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE PROBLEM:
More choice can lead to:
• Decision paralysis
• Lower satisfaction
• Regret and doubt
• Choice deferral

FAMOUS STUDY (IYENGAR):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
24 jams displayed:  60% stopped, 3% purchased
6 jams displayed:   40% stopped, 30% purchased

Too many options → Analysis paralysis

MANAGING CHOICE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strategy            Application
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Reduce options      Curated selection
Categorize          Group similar options
Recommend           "Our pick" or "Best seller"
Customize           Show relevant options
Sequential          Step-by-step choices
Undo capability     Reduce decision anxiety

OPTIMAL NUMBER OF OPTIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Studies suggest: 3-7 options often optimal
• Enough for choice
• Not overwhelming
• Easy to process
```

## Decision Context

### Situational Factors

```
CONTEXT EFFECTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DECOY EFFECT (ASYMMETRIC DOMINANCE):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Adding inferior option changes preferences

Without decoy:
• Small: $50
• Large: $80
Choice: Mixed

With decoy:
• Small: $50
• Medium: $75 (decoy - dominated by Large)
• Large: $80
Choice: Large becomes preferred

COMPROMISE EFFECT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Middle option seems safest

• Budget: $500
• Standard: $800 ← Most chosen
• Premium: $1,200

FRAMING EFFECTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Same information, different presentation

Gain frame: "90% fat-free"
Loss frame: "10% fat"

Risk-averse for gains → Prefer "90% fat-free"
Risk-seeking for losses → Prefer avoiding "10% fat"

TEMPORAL CONTEXT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Time pressure → More heuristic use
• Morning → More self-control
• Decision fatigue → Poor choices later
```

### Post-Decision Behavior

```
POST-PURCHASE PSYCHOLOGY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

COGNITIVE DISSONANCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mental discomfort from conflicting beliefs
• "Did I make the right choice?"
• Especially for expensive purchases
• Seek reassurance

Marketing response:
• Post-purchase reassurance
• Welcome communications
• Community inclusion
• Usage support

SATISFACTION VS. DELIGHT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Satisfaction = Performance - Expectations

Below expectations → Dissatisfaction
Meets expectations → Satisfaction
Exceeds expectations → Delight

Marketing strategy:
• Set appropriate expectations
• Exceed them
• Manage disappointment proactively

REGRET AND COUNTERFACTUAL THINKING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"What if I had chosen differently?"

Regret types:
• Action regret (did something wrong)
• Inaction regret (missed opportunity)

Marketing response:
• "Right choice" messaging
• Easy returns/exchanges
• Future opportunity framing
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **System 1 dominates** - Most decisions are intuitive
2. **Heuristics are shortcuts** - Useful but biased
3. **Biases are predictable** - Design around them
4. **Choice architecture matters** - Defaults are powerful
5. **Less can be more** - Avoid choice overload
6. **Context shapes choice** - Framing and situation matter
