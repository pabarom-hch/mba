# Culture and Consumer Behavior

## Learning Objectives

- Understand how culture shapes consumption
- Apply cultural frameworks to marketing
- Navigate cross-cultural marketing challenges
- Recognize subcultures and their influence
- Adapt marketing to cultural contexts

---

## Introduction

Culture is the lens through which consumers view the world. It shapes values, perceptions, wants, and behaviors in ways that are often invisible to those within the culture. For global marketers, understanding cultural differences is essential for success; for domestic marketers, understanding subcultures enables targeted, relevant marketing.

## Culture Fundamentals

### What Is Culture?

```
CULTURE DEFINITION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"The collective programming of the mind that
distinguishes members of one group from another."
                              — Geert Hofstede

CULTURE CHARACTERISTICS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Learned (not inherited)
• Shared (collective phenomenon)
• Symbolic (expressed through language, rituals)
• Integrated (elements interconnect)
• Dynamic (evolves over time)

CULTURAL ELEMENTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Values:       Shared beliefs about what's important
Norms:        Rules for appropriate behavior
Rituals:      Symbolic activities with meaning
Myths:        Stories expressing cultural values
Material culture: Physical objects with meaning
```

### Cultural Dimensions

```
HOFSTEDE'S CULTURAL DIMENSIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

POWER DISTANCE (PDI):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Acceptance of unequal power distribution

High PDI (Malaysia, Mexico):
• Hierarchy respected
• Status symbols matter
• Authority-based ads

Low PDI (Denmark, Israel):
• Equality valued
• Status symbols less important
• Peer-based messaging

INDIVIDUALISM VS. COLLECTIVISM (IDV):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Priority of individual vs. group

Individualist (USA, UK):
• "I" identity
• Self-expression valued
• Personal achievement ads

Collectivist (China, Japan):
• "We" identity
• Group harmony valued
• Family/community ads

MASCULINITY VS. FEMININITY (MAS):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Achievement vs. relationship orientation

Masculine (Japan, Germany):
• Competition valued
• Success/status focus
• Achievement advertising

Feminine (Sweden, Norway):
• Quality of life valued
• Caring/relationships focus
• Balance messaging

UNCERTAINTY AVOIDANCE (UAI):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tolerance for ambiguity

High UAI (Greece, Portugal):
• Seek structure, rules
• Expert endorsements
• Detailed information

Low UAI (Singapore, Jamaica):
• Comfortable with ambiguity
• Innovation appeals
• Fewer guarantees needed

LONG-TERM ORIENTATION (LTO):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Future vs. present/past orientation

Long-term (China, Japan):
• Persistence valued
• Savings emphasized
• Future benefits

Short-term (USA, Nigeria):
• Immediate results
• Spending/consumption
• Quick payoffs

INDULGENCE VS. RESTRAINT (IND):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gratification vs. control

Indulgent (USA, Mexico):
• Enjoying life
• Fun and leisure
• Emotional appeals

Restrained (Russia, India):
• Suppressing gratification
• Duty and social norms
• Practical appeals
```

### Cross-Cultural Marketing

```
STANDARDIZATION VS. ADAPTATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STANDARDIZATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Same marketing across markets

Advantages:
• Cost efficiency
• Consistent brand image
• Global brand building

Disadvantages:
• May not resonate locally
• Miss local preferences
• Cultural missteps

ADAPTATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Customize for local markets

Advantages:
• Cultural relevance
• Better local fit
• Competitive locally

Disadvantages:
• Higher costs
• Brand inconsistency
• Complexity

GLOCALIZATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"Think global, act local"

Standardize:
• Brand positioning
• Core values
• Visual identity

Adapt:
• Language
• Models and imagery
• Cultural references
• Product features
• Promotion tactics
```

### Cultural Marketing Mistakes

```
CROSS-CULTURAL ERRORS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

LANGUAGE BLUNDERS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• "Nova" car in Spanish markets ("no go")
• KFC's "Finger-lickin' good" became
  "Eat your fingers off" in Chinese
• "Got Milk?" became "Are you lactating?"

SYMBOLIC MISTAKES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• White = Mourning in Asia (not purity)
• Number 4 = Death in China
• Left hand offensive in Middle East
• Owl = Wisdom (West) vs. Bad luck (India)

RELIGIOUS INSENSITIVITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Religious imagery in secular contexts
• Ignoring fasting periods
• Inappropriate product associations

CULTURAL CONTEXT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
High context (Asia, Middle East):
• Implicit communication
• Relationships matter
• Face and honor important

Low context (USA, Germany):
• Explicit communication
• Task-focused
• Direct feedback
```

## Subcultures

### Subculture Types

```
MAJOR SUBCULTURE CATEGORIES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ETHNIC SUBCULTURES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shared heritage, language, customs

US Examples:
• Hispanic/Latino
• African American
• Asian American
• Native American

Marketing approach:
• Authentic representation
• Cultural relevance
• Language considerations
• Community engagement

AGE SUBCULTURES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Generational cohorts with shared experiences

Generation       Birth Years    Characteristics
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Silent           1928-1945      Traditional, loyal
Baby Boomers     1946-1964      Affluent, idealistic
Gen X            1965-1980      Independent, skeptical
Millennials      1981-1996      Digital native, values
Gen Z            1997-2012      Mobile-first, diverse
Gen Alpha        2013+          AI native, global

RELIGIOUS SUBCULTURES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Faith-based consumption patterns
• Dietary restrictions (Kosher, Halal)
• Holiday observances
• Modesty requirements
• Ethical constraints

GEOGRAPHIC SUBCULTURES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Regional differences
• Urban vs. rural
• Regional preferences
• Climate-based needs
• Local traditions
```

### Generational Marketing

```
GENERATIONAL DIFFERENCES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

BABY BOOMERS (1946-1964):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Values: Hard work, success, optimism
Media: TV, print, increasingly digital
Marketing: Quality, value, anti-aging
Size: 70M (US), high spending power

GEN X (1965-1980):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Values: Independence, work-life balance
Media: TV, email, early internet
Marketing: Convenience, nostalgia, family
Size: 65M (US), peak earning years

MILLENNIALS (1981-1996):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Values: Experiences, authenticity, social impact
Media: Social media, mobile, streaming
Marketing: Experiences, values, peer reviews
Size: 72M (US), largest generation

GEN Z (1997-2012):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Values: Diversity, activism, individuality
Media: TikTok, YouTube, Instagram
Marketing: Authenticity, visual, snackable
Size: 68M (US), growing buying power

MULTIGENERATIONAL APPROACH:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Common ground:
• Core human needs
• Quality expectations
• Value for money

Adapt by generation:
• Channel selection
• Message format
• Cultural references
• Technology use
```

### Cultural Creep

```
CULTURAL APPROPRIATION VS. APPRECIATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

APPROPRIATION (Avoid):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Taking without understanding
• Stereotyping
• Profiting from sacred elements
• Lacking authentic connection

APPRECIATION (Positive):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Genuine respect
• Collaboration with community
• Giving credit
• Authentic representation

GUIDELINES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Consult cultural experts
• Include diverse perspectives in teams
• Research meaning and context
• Obtain permission where appropriate
• Give back to community
• Accept feedback gracefully
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Culture shapes perception** - Same stimulus, different interpretation
2. **Dimensions vary** - Hofstede's framework guides adaptation
3. **Rituals are opportunities** - Connect with cultural practices
4. **Subcultures are distinct** - Don't treat as monolithic
5. **Generations differ** - Media, values, and expectations vary
6. **Culture evolves** - Stay current with trends
7. **Respect is essential** - Avoid appropriation and stereotypes
