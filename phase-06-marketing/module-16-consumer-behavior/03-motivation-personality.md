# Motivation and Personality

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the role of motivation in consumer behavior
- Apply motivation theories to marketing strategy
- Recognize how personality influences consumption
- Use psychographic profiling effectively
- Design motivationally-targeted marketing

---

## Introduction

Motivation is the driving force that causes people to act. Understanding what motivates consumers—their needs, wants, and goals—is essential for designing products and messages that resonate. Personality shapes how motivation is expressed, creating distinct patterns of consumer behavior.

---

## Motivation Fundamentals

### The Motivation Process

```
MOTIVATION MODEL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NEED ──► DRIVE ──► BEHAVIOR ──► GOAL ──► REDUCTION
(Tension)  (Arousal)  (Action)    (Satisfaction)
    │                                      │
    └──────── FEEDBACK ────────────────────┘

NEED:
Internal state of deprivation
• Biogenic (food, water, shelter)
• Psychogenic (status, belonging)

DRIVE:
Arousal to reduce the need
• Intensity varies
• Direction toward goal

BEHAVIOR:
Action taken to satisfy need
• Goal-oriented
• Learned patterns

GOAL:
Object or state that satisfies need
• Positive goals (approach)
• Negative goals (avoidance)
```

### Other Motivation Theories

```
ALTERNATIVE FRAMEWORKS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

HERZBERG'S TWO-FACTOR THEORY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Hygiene factors:     Must be present or dissatisfied
                     (Price, availability, basic quality)

Motivators:          Create satisfaction when present
                     (Design, status, experience)

Implication:
Hygiene = Table stakes
Motivators = Differentiation

McCLELLAND'S ACQUIRED NEEDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Need for Achievement:
• Set challenging goals
• Seek feedback
• Take calculated risks
Products: Self-improvement, education, productivity

Need for Affiliation:
• Seek social approval
• Avoid conflict
• Want to belong
Products: Social platforms, group activities

Need for Power:
• Influence others
• Control environment
• Seek status
Products: Luxury goods, leadership programs

FREUDIAN MOTIVATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unconscious motivation drives behavior
• Id: Pleasure principle
• Ego: Reality principle
• Superego: Moral principle

Marketing use:
• Symbolic meaning of products
• Deep emotional appeals
• Projective techniques in research
```

### Motivation Types

```
MOTIVATION DIMENSIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

INTRINSIC VS. EXTRINSIC:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Intrinsic:    Internal satisfaction from activity
              Example: Joy of learning, creativity

Extrinsic:    External rewards drive behavior
              Example: Points, prizes, money

Marketing implication:
Extrinsic rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation
Use external rewards carefully

APPROACH VS. AVOIDANCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Approach:     Moving toward positive goal
              "Get the new iPhone"

Avoidance:    Moving away from negative outcome
              "Don't miss out"

Marketing implication:
Loss framing often more powerful than gain
But depends on context and personality

HIGH VS. LOW INVOLVEMENT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
High involvement:
• Personal relevance
• Risk perceived
• Extensive search
• Central processing

Low involvement:
• Little relevance
• Low risk
• Minimal search
• Peripheral processing

Marketing implication:
Match message strategy to involvement level
```

## Goal-Setting and Consumer Behavior

### Goal Pursuit

```
CONSUMER GOALS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

GOAL HIERARCHY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
            Life values
          (Be successful)
                │
          Life themes
        (Build career)
                │
          Current concerns
        (Get promotion)
                │
          Consumption goals
        (Look professional)
                │
          Product choices
        (Buy business attire)

GOAL TYPES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Prevention goals:    Avoid negative outcomes
                     Security, safety, responsibility

Promotion goals:     Achieve positive outcomes
                     Growth, advancement, aspiration

Marketing implication:
Prevention: "Don't let bad things happen"
Promotion: "Achieve your dreams"

GOAL CONFLICT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Approach-approach:   Two positive options
                     (Vacation vs. new car)

Approach-avoidance:  Positive and negative in one
                     (Delicious but unhealthy)

Avoidance-avoidance: Two negative options
                     (Pay now or pay more later)

Marketing role: Help resolve conflicts
```

### Self-Regulation

```
SELF-REGULATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEFINITION:
Process of guiding behavior toward goals

COMPONENTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Goal setting    → What do I want?
Self-monitoring → How am I doing?
Self-evaluation → Am I on track?
Self-reaction   → Adjust behavior

SELF-CONTROL CHALLENGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Immediate gratification bias
• Ego depletion (willpower fatigue)
• Emotional triggers
• Environmental cues

MARKETING APPLICATIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Help consumers achieve goals:
• Tracking apps (fitness, finance)
• Commitment devices
• Progress visualization
• Social accountability

Exploit self-control failures:
• Impulse purchase positioning
• Immediate rewards
• Easy options (be careful: ethics)
```

## Personality

### Personality and Consumption

```
PERSONALITY DEFINITION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"Consistent patterns of thinking, feeling, and
behaving that distinguish individuals"

THE BIG FIVE (OCEAN):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trait               High Score Characteristics
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Openness            Creative, curious, adventurous
                    → Try new products, variety-seeking

Conscientiousness   Organized, disciplined, reliable
                    → Research purchases, quality focus

Extraversion        Outgoing, energetic, talkative
                    → Social products, brand visibility

Agreeableness       Cooperative, trusting, helpful
                    → Ethical brands, social causes

Neuroticism         Anxious, emotional, sensitive
                    → Risk-averse, seek reassurance

MARKETING APPLICATIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Personality Segment     Product Appeals
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
High Openness           Novel, innovative, artistic
High Conscientiousness  Quality, reliability, organization
High Extraversion       Social, bold, attention-getting
High Agreeableness      Sustainable, ethical, community
High Neuroticism        Safe, guaranteed, reassuring
```

### Brand Personality

```
BRAND PERSONALITY FRAMEWORK:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AAKER'S BRAND PERSONALITY DIMENSIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sincerity:      Honest, genuine, wholesome
                Brands: Hallmark, Coca-Cola

Excitement:     Daring, spirited, imaginative
                Brands: Red Bull, Tesla

Competence:     Reliable, intelligent, successful
                Brands: IBM, McKinsey

Sophistication: Upper class, charming, glamorous
                Brands: Chanel, Mercedes

Ruggedness:     Outdoorsy, tough, strong
                Brands: Jeep, Timberland

BRAND-CONSUMER CONGRUENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Consumers prefer brands matching:
• Actual self (who I am)
• Ideal self (who I want to be)
• Social self (how others see me)

Self-congruity → Preference → Purchase → Loyalty

CREATING BRAND PERSONALITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Through:
• Visual identity
• Tone of voice
• Spokesperson choice
• User imagery
• Advertising style
• Product design
• Customer experience
```

## Motivation and Marketing Strategy

### Motivational Research

```
UNCOVERING MOTIVATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PROJECTIVE TECHNIQUES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Word association:    What comes to mind?
Sentence completion: "When I use Brand X, I feel..."
Story completion:    What happens next?
Collage/mood boards: Express feelings visually
Personification:     If brand were a person...

DEPTH INTERVIEWS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Probing beyond surface answers
• "Why is that important to you?"
• "Tell me about a time when..."
• "What does that mean to you?"

LADDERING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Surface attribute → Benefit → Value

Example:
"Why do you like that the phone is thin?"
→ "It fits in my pocket"
→ "I can take it anywhere"
→ "I stay connected"
→ "I don't miss opportunities"
→ "I'm successful"

Surface → Functional → Emotional → Value
```

### Motivational Messaging

```
MOTIVATIONAL APPEALS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

APPEAL TYPE         EXAMPLE MESSAGE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Achievement         "Reach your full potential"
Affiliation         "Join millions of users"
Power               "Take control of your life"
Security            "Protect what matters most"
Novelty             "Discover something new"
Self-expression     "Be yourself"
Value               "Get more for less"
Pleasure            "Enjoy life's moments"

MATCHING APPEALS TO SEGMENTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
High achievement need → Challenge, success messages
High affiliation need → Social, belonging messages
High power need       → Control, status messages

EMOTIONAL VS. RATIONAL APPEALS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Emotional:  Connect with underlying motivation
            Better for low-involvement, hedonic products

Rational:   Address conscious evaluation
            Better for high-involvement, utilitarian products

Best:       Combine both (emotional connection + rational justification)
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Understand the "why"** - Motivation explains behavior
2. **Needs are hierarchical** - Address appropriate level
3. **Goals drive behavior** - Help consumers achieve theirs
4. **Personality varies** - Different appeals for different traits
5. **Brand personality matters** - Create congruence with target
6. **Dig deeper** - Surface preferences mask deeper motivations
