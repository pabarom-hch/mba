# Module 16: Consumer Behavior

## Overview

Consumer behavior studies how individuals, groups, and organizations select, purchase, use, and dispose of products and services. Understanding the "why" behind consumer decisions enables marketers to design more effective strategies. This module covers psychological, social, and cultural influences on consumer choice, from perception and motivation to decision-making processes.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1. Analyze psychological influences on consumer behavior
2. Understand the consumer decision-making process
3. Apply motivation and perception theories to marketing
4. Assess social and cultural influences on consumption
5. Design marketing strategies based on behavioral insights
6. Apply behavioral economics principles to influence choice
7. Predict and influence consumer behavior ethically

## Consumer Decision Process

```
THE FIVE-STAGE MODEL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. PROBLEM RECOGNITION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Triggered by:
• Need recognition (internal)
• Opportunity recognition (external)
• Gap between actual and desired state

Marketing implications:
• Create awareness of problems
• Highlight dissatisfaction
• Introduce new possibilities

2. INFORMATION SEARCH
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sources:
• Internal (memory, experience)
• External (ads, reviews, friends)

Search intensity depends on:
• Involvement level
• Perceived risk
• Time pressure
• Knowledge level

3. EVALUATION OF ALTERNATIVES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Consideration set:
• All brands → Awareness set → Consideration set

Evaluation criteria:
• Attributes and features
• Benefits sought
• Trade-offs and priorities

4. PURCHASE DECISION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Influenced by:
• Attitudes toward alternative
• Social influence
• Situational factors
• Unexpected factors

5. POST-PURCHASE BEHAVIOR
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Outcomes:
• Satisfaction/Dissatisfaction
• Cognitive dissonance
• Loyalty or switching
• Word of mouth
```

## Behavioral Economics Principles

```
KEY BEHAVIORAL BIASES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ANCHORING:
First piece of information disproportionately influences
Example: "Was $100, now $50" (anchor on $100)

LOSS AVERSION:
Losses feel ~2x worse than equivalent gains
Example: "Don't miss out" more effective than "Save now"

SOCIAL PROOF:
Looking to others for guidance on behavior
Example: "Most popular choice" labels

SCARCITY:
Limited availability increases perceived value
Example: "Only 3 left in stock"

DEFAULT EFFECT:
People stick with pre-selected options
Example: Opt-out vs. opt-in for email lists

FRAMING:
How information is presented affects decisions
Example: "90% fat-free" vs. "10% fat"

PRESENT BIAS:
Preference for immediate over future rewards
Example: Why people don't save for retirement

ENDOWMENT EFFECT:
Overvaluing things we own
Example: Free trial converts better than discount
```
