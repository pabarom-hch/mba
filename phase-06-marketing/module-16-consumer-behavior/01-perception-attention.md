# Perception and Attention

## Learning Objectives

- Understand how consumers process sensory information
- Apply principles of attention to marketing
- Design effective sensory marketing strategies
- Recognize perceptual biases and their marketing implications
- Create messages that break through attention barriers

---

## Introduction

Perception is the process by which consumers select, organize, and interpret sensory stimuli into a meaningful picture of the world. Before any message can influence behavior, it must first be perceived. Understanding perception is essential for designing marketing that captures attention and creates intended impressions.

---

### The Perception Process Model

```
PERCEPTION STAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXPOSURE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Stimulus comes within range of sensory receptors
• Active: Seeking information
• Passive: Incidental contact

Thresholds:
• Absolute threshold: Minimum detectable level
• Differential threshold (JND): Minimum detectable change

ATTENTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mental resources allocated to stimulus
• Limited capacity
• Selective process
• Voluntary or involuntary

Factors affecting attention:
• Stimulus factors (size, color, novelty)
• Personal factors (needs, interests)
• Situational factors (context, clutter)

INTERPRETATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Meaning assigned to stimulus
• Based on prior experience, expectations
• Influenced by context
• Subject to bias

Interpretation principles:
• Categorization
• Inference
• Schema application
```

### Selective Perception

```
SELECTIVE PROCESSES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SELECTIVE EXPOSURE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People choose what stimuli to expose themselves to
• Seek confirming information
• Avoid dissonant information

Implication:
Reaching non-customers is harder than reaching customers

SELECTIVE ATTENTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People choose what stimuli to notice
• Filter most information
• Notice what's relevant

Implication:
Must break through clutter to be noticed

SELECTIVE DISTORTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People interpret information consistent with beliefs
• Hear what they want to hear
• Twist meaning to fit expectations

Implication:
Message may not be received as intended

SELECTIVE RETENTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People remember information that supports attitudes
• Forget inconvenient facts
• Remember confirming evidence

Implication:
Repetition needed; consistency important
```

### Perceptual Organization

```
GESTALT PRINCIPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FIGURE-GROUND:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
We perceive objects as distinct from background
Marketing use: Make key elements stand out

PROXIMITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Objects near each other seem related
Marketing use: Group related products/info

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Similar objects seem related

CLOSURE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Marketing use: Engaging, memorable logos (IBM, WWF)

CONTINUITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
We perceive smooth, continuous lines
Marketing use: Guide eye through layout
```

## Perceptual Thresholds

### Weber's Law

```
JUST NOTICEABLE DIFFERENCE (JND):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WEBER'S LAW:
The stronger the initial stimulus, the greater the
change needed for it to be noticed.

ΔI/I = k (constant)

Where:
ΔI = Change in stimulus intensity
I = Initial stimulus intensity
k = Constant for that sense

APPLICATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PRICE CHANGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
$1 price reduction means more to:
$10 item (10% off) than $100 item (1% off)

PRODUCT CHANGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Small changes: Keep below JND (cost savings)
Major improvements: Ensure above JND (perceived value)

Example:
Reducing candy bar size by 5%: Below JND (won't notice)
New feature in software: Must be obvious to matter

MARKETING APPLICATIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
When to stay below JND:
• Price increases
• Package size reductions
• Quality reductions

When to exceed JND:
• Product improvements
• Price discounts
• New features
```

### Subliminal Perception

```
SUBLIMINAL PERCEPTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEFINITION:
Perception of stimuli below the threshold of
conscious awareness

THE EVIDENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Very weak effects if any
• Cannot make people do things they don't want
• Unethical if used
• Largely a myth

WHAT DOES WORK (Supraliminal):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Brief exposures that ARE consciously perceived
• Priming effects
• Mere exposure effect

MERE EXPOSURE EFFECT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Repeated exposure increases liking
• Even without conscious recall
• Works for brands, music, faces
• Foundation of advertising repetition
```

## Sensory Marketing

### Multi-Sensory Branding

```
SENSORY BRAND EXPERIENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CREATING SENSORY SIGNATURES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sense           Brand Example
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Visual          Apple (minimalist, white)
Audio           Intel (5-note chime)
Scent           Singapore Airlines (Stefan Floridian)
Touch           Moleskine (leather feel)
Taste           Coca-Cola (secret formula)

MULTI-SENSORY CONGRUENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
All senses should tell the same story
• Luxury brand: Heavy, scented, soft, warm colors
• Health brand: Light, fresh scent, cool colors
• Tech brand: Smooth, minimal, white/silver

CROSS-MODAL EFFECTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Senses influence each other:
• Music tempo affects eating speed
• Scent affects time perception
• Color affects taste perception
• Touch affects judgment of quality
```

### Retail Atmospherics

```
STORE ATMOSPHERE EFFECTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MUSIC:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tempo:
• Slow tempo → More time in store, more spending
• Fast tempo → Faster movement, turnover

Genre:
• Classical → Premium perceptions, higher spending
• Pop → Younger perception

Volume:
• Louder → Less time in store
• Softer → More browsing

SCENT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Pleasant scent → More positive evaluations
• Thematic scent → Congruent with product works best
• Signature scent → Creates memory, identity

LIGHTING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Brighter → More arousal, faster movement
• Dimmer → More relaxed, intimate

COLOR:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Warm colors → Approach behavior, urgency
• Cool colors → Relaxation, trust

CROWDING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Some crowding → Social proof, excitement
• Too much → Stress, avoidance
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Perception is subjective** - What matters is perceived reality
2. **Attention is scarce** - Break through clutter strategically
3. **Selection happens** - People filter, distort, and forget
4. **Interpretation varies** - Context and expectations matter
5. **Thresholds exist** - Use Weber's Law strategically
6. **Senses combine** - Multi-sensory marketing is powerful
