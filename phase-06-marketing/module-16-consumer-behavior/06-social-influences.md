# Social Influences on Consumer Behavior

## Learning Objectives

- Understand how social factors influence consumption
- Apply reference group theory to marketing
- Leverage social proof and word-of-mouth
- Analyze family decision-making dynamics
- Design social marketing strategies

---

## Introduction

Humans are inherently social beings, and consumption is often a social act. We buy to belong, to signal identity, and because others recommend products. Understanding social influence enables marketers to leverage networks, communities, and social dynamics to drive brand adoption and advocacy.

## Reference Groups

### Types of Reference Groups

```
REFERENCE GROUP CATEGORIES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MEMBERSHIP GROUPS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Groups you belong to

Primary groups:
• Face-to-face, informal
• Family, close friends, coworkers
• High influence, frequent contact

Secondary groups:
• More formal, less contact
• Professional associations, clubs
• Moderate influence

ASPIRATIONAL GROUPS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Groups you want to belong to
• Admire from outside
• Adopt behaviors/products to gain membership
• Strong influence on visible consumption

Examples:
• Country club members
• Luxury brand communities
• Influencer followings

DISSOCIATIVE GROUPS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Groups you want to avoid association with
• Reject their consumption patterns
• "I would never be like them"
• Defines what NOT to buy

VIRTUAL COMMUNITIES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Online groups and networks
• Brand communities
• Social media groups
• Forums and subreddits
• Influencer audiences
```

### Reference Group Influence

```
TYPES OF INFLUENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

INFORMATIONAL INFLUENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Accept information from others as evidence
• Rely on expert knowledge
• Observe what others do
• Seek recommendations

Conditions:
• Uncertainty about best choice
• Others have relevant expertise
• Low risk of asking

VALUE-EXPRESSIVE INFLUENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Want to be associated with group identity
• Adopt group's attitudes/behaviors
• Express identification with group
• Signal belonging

Conditions:
• Group membership matters
• Consumption is visible
• Identity-relevant products

UTILITARIAN INFLUENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Conform to group norms for rewards/avoid punishment
• Comply to gain approval
• Avoid social sanctions
• Fit in with expectations

Conditions:
• Rewards/punishments meaningful
• Behavior is observable
• Group can enforce norms
```

## Word of Mouth

### WOM Dynamics

```
WORD-OF-MOUTH POWER:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHY WOM MATTERS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 92% trust recommendations from friends
• 70% trust online reviews
• WOM drives 13% of consumer sales
• 5x more impactful than paid advertising

WOM CHARACTERISTICS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Valence:      Positive or negative
              (Negative spreads faster, wider)

Volume:       How much conversation
              (More is better, within limits)

Dispersion:   Spread across or within networks
              (Broad reach vs. deep penetration)

WHAT GETS SHARED (STEPPS):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Social currency:  Makes sharer look good
Triggers:         Reminded by environment
Emotion:          High arousal (awe, anger, anxiety)
Public:           Visible, observable
Practical value:  Useful information
Stories:          Narrative container

NEGATIVE WOM:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Causes:
• Disappointment vs. expectations
• Product/service failure
• Perceived unfairness

Response:
• Acknowledge and apologize
• Resolve quickly
• Service recovery paradox
```

### Online Reviews

```
ONLINE REVIEW DYNAMICS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

REVIEW INFLUENCE FACTORS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rating level:     Higher ratings drive purchase
Review volume:    More reviews = more trust
Recency:          Recent reviews weighted more
Reviewer credibility: Verified, detailed reviews matter

THE J-CURVE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Review distribution typically J-shaped

Count
  │
  │*
  │*
  │*
  │*
  │ *            **
  │  *          *  *
  │   *        *    **
  │    ********      **
  └─────────────────────→
   1  2  3  4  5   Stars

Extreme opinions more likely to post

MANAGING REVIEWS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Encourage satisfied customers to review
• Respond to all negative reviews
• Don't fake (illegal, obvious, damaging)
• Highlight reviews in marketing
• Act on feedback patterns
```

## Family Decision Making

### Family Roles

```
FAMILY DECISION ROLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ROLE              DEFINITION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Initiator         First suggests/considers product
Influencer        Affects evaluation of alternatives
Gatekeeper        Controls information flow
Decider           Makes final purchase decision
Buyer             Actually makes the purchase
User              Consumes the product

EXAMPLE - FAMILY CAR:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Initiator:    Child notices friend's new car
Influencer:   Spouse suggests features needed
Gatekeeper:   Parent researches options
Decider:      Both spouses jointly decide
Buyer:        One spouse handles transaction
User:         Whole family uses

IMPLICATIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Target the right family member(s)
• Message to each role differently
• Recognize multiple decision-makers
```

### Family Lifecycle

```
FAMILY LIFECYCLE STAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

YOUNG SINGLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Few financial burdens
• Fashion, entertainment, tech
• Early adopters
• Forming preferences

NEWLY MARRIED:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Highest purchase rate
• Durables, home furnishing
• Dual income often
• Setting up household

FULL NEST I (Young children):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Home purchase peak
• Liquid assets low
• Child-oriented purchases
• Price sensitive

FULL NEST II (Older children):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Financial position better
• Education expenses
• Children influence more
• Larger home, cars

FULL NEST III (Adult children at home):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Good financial position
• Adult children still influence
• Durables replacement
• Saving for future

EMPTY NEST:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Home ownership peak
• Most satisfied financially
• Travel, recreation
• Health products

SOLITARY SURVIVOR:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Income decline
• Medical, security products
• Affection, attention needs
• Downsizing
```

### Household Decision Dynamics

```
DECISION PATTERNS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

HUSBAND-DOMINANT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Traditional in: Cars, insurance, electronics
Declining pattern in Western markets

WIFE-DOMINANT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Traditional in: Food, cleaning, childcare
Also shifting with changing roles

JOINT DECISIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Common for: Major appliances, vacation, housing
Increasing for previously gendered categories

AUTONOMIC (Individual):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Personal items, small purchases
Increasing with dual incomes

CONFLICT RESOLUTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Consensus (compromise)
• Accommodation (give in)
• One partner decides
• Budget allocation
• Expert within household

CHILDREN'S INFLUENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Expressive products (cereals, toys, restaurants)
• Technology products
• Family purchases (vacation destinations)
• Increasing with age
• "Pester power"
```

## Social Class

### Class and Consumption

```
SOCIAL CLASS DIMENSIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEFINING FACTORS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Occupation
• Education
• Income
• Wealth
• Social prestige

CLASS CATEGORIES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Upper-upper:    Old money, inherited wealth
Lower-upper:    New money, earned wealth
Upper-middle:   Professionals, managers
Middle:         White collar, service
Working:        Blue collar, trade
Lower:          Unskilled, unemployed

CONSUMPTION PATTERNS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Class           Consumption Characteristics
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Upper           Understated quality, experiences
Middle          Brand-conscious, aspirational
Working         Value-oriented, practical

STATUS CONSUMPTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Visible products signal status
• Luxury goods
• "Right" brands
• Prestige locations

Veblen goods: Demand increases with price
(Status value from exclusivity)
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Groups shape behavior** - We consume to belong and differentiate
2. **WOM is powerful** - Earned recommendations beat advertising
3. **Influence is multi-directional** - Influencers and influenced both matter
4. **Family is a unit** - Multiple roles in purchase decisions
5. **Social class affects consumption** - Patterns vary by class
6. **Online amplifies social** - Reviews, sharing, influencers at scale
