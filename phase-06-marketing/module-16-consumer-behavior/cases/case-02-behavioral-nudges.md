# Case Study: Behavioral Nudges in Practice

## Executive Summary

This case explores how organizations apply behavioral economics principles to influence consumer decisions. From retirement savings to organ donation to reducing energy consumption, nudges have proven remarkably effective at changing behavior without restricting choice. The case examines successful implementations, ethical considerations, and design principles for effective choice architecture.

**Key Question:** How can organizations ethically apply behavioral insights to improve decision-making while preserving consumer autonomy?

---

## Introduction to Nudge Theory

### Background

```
NUDGE DEFINED (THALER & SUNSTEIN):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"Any aspect of the choice architecture that alters
people's behavior in a predictable way without
forbidding any options or significantly changing
their economic incentives."

LIBERTARIAN PATERNALISM:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Paternalist: Steer toward beneficial outcomes
• Libertarian: Maintain freedom of choice

Examples of nudges:
• Default options
• Simplification
• Social norms
• Disclosure
• Reminders
• Feedback

Not nudges:
• Mandates
• Bans
• Significant financial incentives
```

## Case A: Retirement Savings

### The Problem

```
TRADITIONAL 401(k) ENROLLMENT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TRADITIONAL APPROACH:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Opt-in enrollment
• Employee must actively enroll
• Must choose contribution rate
• Must select investments

RESULTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Participation rates: 60-70%
Average contribution: 5-6% of salary
Many poor investment choices
Procrastination widespread

WHY PEOPLE FAIL TO ENROLL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Present bias: "I'll do it later"
• Status quo bias: "I'll stick with current"
• Choice overload: "Too many funds to choose"
• Complexity: "I don't understand this"
• Loss aversion: "I'll miss that money now"
```

### The Nudge Solution

```
AUTO-ENROLLMENT + AUTO-ESCALATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SAVE MORE TOMORROW (SHLOMO BENARTZI):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Key innovations:
1. Auto-enrollment at 3% of salary
2. Auto-escalation: Increase 1% each year
3. Default to target-date funds
4. Time contribution increase to raises

BEHAVIORAL PRINCIPLES APPLIED:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Default effect: Enrolled unless opt-out
• Status quo bias: Now works in favor
• Present bias: Future commitment is easy
• Loss aversion: Never feel the "loss"
• Simplification: Fewer decisions needed

RESULTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Before auto-enrollment: 60% participation
After auto-enrollment: 90%+ participation

Contribution rates increased 3x over time
Better investment choices (defaults)
Opt-out rates very low (<10%)
```

## Case B: Organ Donation

### The Problem

```
ORGAN DONATION GAP:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE CHALLENGE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• More people need organs than available
• Many would donate but don't register
• Family decisions often don't match wishes
• Thousands die on waiting lists

OPT-IN SYSTEMS (US, UK, Germany):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Requires active registration
Participation: 15-35%
Many potential donors never register

BEHAVIORAL BARRIERS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Requires action (status quo bias)
• Thinking about death uncomfortable
• Procrastination
• Assumes can be done "later"
```

### The Nudge Solution

```
OPT-OUT (PRESUMED CONSENT) SYSTEMS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

COUNTRIES WITH OPT-OUT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Spain, Austria, Belgium, France, UK (2020)

RESULTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Country         System      Consent Rate
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Germany         Opt-in      12%
Austria         Opt-out     99.98%
US              Opt-in      ~35%
Spain           Opt-out     90%+
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE POWER OF DEFAULTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Same choice, different default
• Dramatically different outcomes
• Inaction now means donation
• Status quo bias works for good

ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Does presumed consent equal actual consent?
• Family override provisions
• Cultural and religious concerns
• Ease of opting out important
```

## Case C: Energy Conservation

### The Problem

```
RESIDENTIAL ENERGY USE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE CHALLENGE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Households overuse energy
• Cost and environmental impact
• Information campaigns ineffective
• Behavior change difficult

TRADITIONAL APPROACHES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Financial incentives (rebates)
• Information campaigns
• Technology (smart thermostats)

LIMITED SUCCESS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Information alone rarely changes behavior
Financial incentives wear off
```

### The Nudge Solution

```
OPOWER SOCIAL COMPARISONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE INTERVENTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Home Energy Reports comparing usage to neighbors

Report includes:
• Your usage vs. similar homes
• Neighbor comparison charts
• Efficiency ranking
• Energy-saving tips

BEHAVIORAL PRINCIPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Social norms: "Most neighbors use less"
• Social proof: "Efficient is normal"
• Loss aversion: "You're using more"
• Feedback: "See your impact"

RESULTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 2-3% reduction in energy usage
• Persists over time
• Cost-effective vs. rebates
• Now used by 100+ utilities
• Saved $1 billion+ in energy

THE SMILEY FACE STUDY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Added :) for below-average users
Added :( for above-average users

Prevented boomerang effect
(Above-average users didn't increase usage)
```

## Case D: Healthy Eating

### The Problem

```
FOOD CHOICE ENVIRONMENT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE CHALLENGE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Unhealthy foods often most accessible
• Portion sizes have grown
• Choice architecture favors unhealthy
• Information labels insufficient

CAFETERIA DESIGN ISSUES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Desserts at front
• Healthy options hidden
• Large portion defaults
• Unhealthy at eye level
```

### The Nudge Solution

```
CAFETERIA CHOICE ARCHITECTURE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WANSINK'S SMARTER LUNCHROOMS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Placement interventions:
• Fruit at front, near cashier
• Vegetables at eye level
• Healthy options more accessible
• Water before soda

Default changes:
• Smaller plates available
• Healthy default (opt into dessert)
• Half-portions available
• Water default (ask for soda)

RESULTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 35% increase in fruit selection
• 20% increase in vegetables
• 30% reduction in desserts
• No complaints from students
• No restriction of choice

GOOGLE CAFETERIA EXPERIMENTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Smaller plates (reduced consumption 5%)
• Drinks in opaque containers (reduced sugary drinks)
• Healthy options first (increased selection)
• Signage highlighting healthy options
```

## Case E: Financial Services

### Default Investment Options

```
SWEDISH PENSION REFORM:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE CHOICE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 456 funds to choose from
• Or accept default fund
• Massive advertising campaign to choose

RESULTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Initially: 66% made active choice
10 years later: 92% in default fund

Default fund outperformed average active choice!
• Lower fees
• Better diversification
• Less behavioral error

LESSON:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Good defaults + choice = Best outcome
Forcing choice doesn't mean better choice
```

### Overdraft Protection

```
OVERDRAFT OPT-IN:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

BEFORE 2010 (US):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Overdraft protection automatic default
• High fees ($35/transaction)
• Many consumers unaware
• "Courtesy" protection costly

REGULATORY NUDGE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Required banks to make overdraft opt-in
Consumers must actively choose overdraft

RESULTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Opt-in rate: ~16%
• $5 billion annual fee reduction
• No increase in bounced checks
• Consumer protection through defaults
```

## Ethical Framework

### Evaluating Nudges

```
ETHICAL NUDGE CRITERIA:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TRANSPARENCY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
✓ Nudge is visible, not hidden
✓ Motivation is clear
✓ Easy to understand
✗ Secret manipulation

EASE OF OPT-OUT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
✓ Simple to choose otherwise
✓ No barriers to opting out
✓ Truly voluntary
✗ Difficult to escape

WELFARE-ENHANCING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
✓ In person's own interest
✓ Person would agree if informed
✓ Consistent with expressed preferences
✗ Exploiting vulnerabilities

REVERSIBILITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
✓ Can change mind later
✓ Not a permanent commitment
✓ Regular choice points
✗ Lock-in without exit

THE SLUDGE PROBLEM:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"Sludge" = Friction that impedes good outcomes

Examples of sludge:
• Complex forms to get benefits
• Difficult cancellation processes
• Hidden information
• Excessive paperwork

Ethical nudging includes removing sludge
```

### Dark Patterns

```
UNETHICAL NUDGES TO AVOID:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DARK PATTERN EXAMPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Forced continuity (hidden auto-renewal)
• Roach motel (easy in, hard out)
• Misdirection (visual distraction)
• Hidden costs (revealed late)
• Confirmshaming ("No, I don't want to save")
• Trick questions (confusing opt-in/out)

REGULATORY RESPONSE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• FTC crackdowns on dark patterns
• Consumer protection laws
• EU Digital Services Act
• State-level legislation

BRAND RISK:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Consumer backlash
• Social media exposure
• Regulatory fines
• Long-term trust damage
```

## Teaching Note

### Learning Objectives

This case teaches students to:
- Design effective nudges using behavioral principles
- Evaluate ethical dimensions of choice architecture
- Apply nudge thinking to business problems
- Recognize dark patterns and avoid them

### Key Principles

**Effective Nudge Design:**
1. Make desired behavior easy (reduce friction)
2. Use defaults wisely (harness inertia)
3. Leverage social norms (show what others do)
4. Provide timely feedback (make impact visible)
5. Keep it simple (reduce cognitive load)

**Ethical Guidelines:**
1. Transparent (visible and understood)
2. Easy to opt-out (preserve autonomy)
3. Welfare-enhancing (benefits the nudged)
4. Aligned with preferences (what they'd choose if informed)

### Discussion Questions

1. Should governments nudge citizens? What limits should exist?
2. Is "libertarian paternalism" a coherent philosophy?
3. How do you balance business goals with consumer welfare?
4. Can competitors differentiate on ethical choice architecture?
