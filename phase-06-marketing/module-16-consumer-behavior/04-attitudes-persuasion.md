# Attitudes and Persuasion

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the structure and function of attitudes
- Apply attitude formation and change theories
- Design persuasive marketing communications
- Recognize the routes to persuasion
- Overcome resistance to attitude change

---

## Introduction

Attitudes are learned predispositions to respond favorably or unfavorably to an object, person, or idea. Consumer attitudes toward brands strongly predict purchase behavior, making attitude formation and change central to marketing strategy. Understanding persuasion enables marketers to design communications that shape and shift attitudes effectively.

## Attitude Fundamentals

### Attitude Structure

```
THE ABC MODEL OF ATTITUDES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

                    ATTITUDE
                        │
        ┌───────────────┼───────────────┐
        │               │               │
   ┌────▼────┐     ┌────▼────┐     ┌────▼────┐
   │ AFFECT  │     │BEHAVIOR │     │COGNITION│
   │(Feelings)│     │(Actions) │     │(Beliefs) │
   └─────────┘     └─────────┘     └─────────┘

AFFECT (Emotional):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Feelings about the object
• Like/dislike
• Emotions evoked
Example: "I love Apple products"

BEHAVIOR (Conative):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Tendency to act
• Past behavior
• Intentions
Example: "I always buy Apple"

COGNITION (Beliefs):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Beliefs about attributes
• Knowledge
• Perceptions
Example: "Apple products are well-designed"

ATTITUDE HIERARCHY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Standard learning:    Cognition → Affect → Behavior
Low-involvement:      Cognition → Behavior → Affect
Experiential:         Affect → Behavior → Cognition
```

### Attitude Functions

```
FUNCTIONAL THEORY OF ATTITUDES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

UTILITARIAN FUNCTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Attitudes based on rewards/punishments
"This product gives me value"
Marketing: Emphasize benefits, value proposition

KNOWLEDGE FUNCTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Attitudes help organize and simplify
"I understand this category"
Marketing: Reduce complexity, provide frameworks

EGO-DEFENSIVE FUNCTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Attitudes protect self-esteem
"I deserve the best"
Marketing: Reassure, protect self-image

VALUE-EXPRESSIVE FUNCTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Attitudes express values and identity
"This brand reflects who I am"
Marketing: Connect to identity, values

MATCHING STRATEGY TO FUNCTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Function          Message Strategy
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Utilitarian       Benefits, features, price
Knowledge         Simple, clear, educational
Ego-defensive     Reassurance, social proof
Value-expressive  Identity, lifestyle, values
```

## Attitude Formation

### How Attitudes Form

```
ATTITUDE FORMATION SOURCES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DIRECT EXPERIENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Using the product creates attitudes
• Strongest predictor of behavior
• Resistant to change
• Marketing: Sampling, trials, demos

INDIRECT EXPERIENCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Observing others or receiving information
• Advertising
• Word of mouth
• Social media
• Reviews

CONDITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Classical: Association with positive stimuli
Operant: Reinforcement of attitudes/behaviors

SOCIALIZATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Family, peers, culture transmit attitudes
• Childhood brand experiences
• Social influence
• Cultural norms
```

### Multi-Attribute Attitude Models

```
FISHBEIN MODEL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Attitude = Σ (Belief × Importance)
           i=1

Ao = Σ bi × ei

Where:
Ao = Attitude toward object
bi = Belief about attribute i
ei = Evaluation (importance) of attribute i

EXAMPLE - SMARTPHONE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Attribute     Belief (Brand X)  Importance  Product
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Battery life     7/10              9        63
Camera quality   8/10              8        64
Price            5/10              7        35
Design           9/10              6        54
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Attitude Score:                             216

STRATEGIES TO CHANGE ATTITUDES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Change beliefs (bi):
   "Our battery now lasts 20% longer"

2. Change importance (ei):
   "Battery life matters more than you think"

3. Add new attribute:
   "Introducing AI photography features"

4. Change competitor beliefs:
   "Brand Y's battery degrades faster"
```

## Attitude Change

### The Elaboration Likelihood Model

```
ELM (PETTY & CACIOPPO):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

              MESSAGE RECEIVED
                     │
                     ▼
           ┌─────────────────┐
           │   Motivation    │
           │   and Ability   │
           │   to Process?   │
           └────────┬────────┘
                    │
         ┌──────────┴──────────┐
         │                     │
    High │                     │ Low
         ▼                     ▼
   ┌───────────┐         ┌───────────┐
   │  CENTRAL  │         │PERIPHERAL │
   │   ROUTE   │         │   ROUTE   │
   └─────┬─────┘         └─────┬─────┘
         │                     │
    Argument                Cues
    Quality              (Source,
         │               Emotion)
         ▼                     ▼
   ┌───────────┐         ┌───────────┐
   │  Strong,  │         │  Weak,    │
   │  Lasting  │         │ Temporary │
   │  Change   │         │  Change   │
   └───────────┘         └───────────┘

CENTRAL ROUTE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
When: High involvement, high ability
Process: Careful evaluation of arguments
Result: Enduring attitude change
Strategy: Strong arguments, evidence, logic

PERIPHERAL ROUTE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
When: Low involvement or low ability
Process: Use of mental shortcuts, cues
Result: Temporary attitude change
Strategy: Attractive source, emotion, simplicity

CUES THAT WORK PERIPHERALLY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Source attractiveness
• Source credibility/expertise
• Number of arguments (not quality)
• Endorsements
• Emotional appeals
• Social proof
```

### Resistance to Persuasion

```
WHY ATTITUDES RESIST CHANGE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ATTITUDE STRENGTH:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strong attitudes resist change
Indicators of strength:
• Importance to the person
• Knowledge/certainty
• Consistency over time
• Accessibility (quickly comes to mind)
• Direct experience

PSYCHOLOGICAL REACTANCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Resistance to perceived persuasion attempts
• "Don't tell me what to do"
• Can backfire (boomerang effect)

Solution:
• Indirect approaches
• Choice and autonomy
• Don't appear manipulative

INOCULATION THEORY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Weak attacks make attitudes stronger
Like vaccination for attitudes

Marketing use:
• "Some people say X, but..."
• Prepare customers for competitor claims
• Strengthen loyalty

OVERCOMING RESISTANCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strategy              Application
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Reduce counter-arguing  Use distraction, music
Build credibility first  Then persuade
Use two-sided messages   Acknowledge negatives
Social proof             Others have changed
Foot-in-the-door         Small request first
```

## Principles of Persuasion

### Cialdini's Six Principles

```
CIALDINI'S INFLUENCE PRINCIPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. RECIPROCITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People feel obligated to return favors
• Free samples
• Gifts with purchase
• Valuable content
• Unexpected kindness

"Give before you ask"

2. COMMITMENT & CONSISTENCY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People want to be consistent with prior actions
• Foot-in-the-door technique
• Public commitments
• Small asks lead to big asks
• Loyalty programs

"Start small"

3. SOCIAL PROOF:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People follow what others do
• Testimonials
• Reviews and ratings
• "Most popular" labels
• Usage statistics

"Join the crowd"

4. AUTHORITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People defer to experts
• Expert endorsements
• Credentials displayed
• Professional appearance
• Awards and recognition

"Trust the expert"

5. LIKING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People say yes to those they like
• Attractive sources
• Similarity
• Compliments
• Cooperation

"Make friends first"

6. SCARCITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People want what's limited
• Limited time offers
• Low stock warnings
• Exclusive access
• Rare editions

"Don't miss out"
```

### Message Design

```
PERSUASIVE MESSAGE ELEMENTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SOURCE CHARACTERISTICS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Credibility:      Expertise + Trustworthiness
Attractiveness:   Physical + Similarity + Likability

Match source to product:
• Expert for technical products
• Attractive for beauty products
• Similar for lifestyle products

MESSAGE STRUCTURE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
One-sided vs. Two-sided:
• One-sided: Only positive (low involvement)
• Two-sided: Acknowledge negatives (high involvement,
              sophisticated audience)

Order effects:
• Primacy: First arguments strongest
• Recency: Last arguments remembered

Explicit vs. Implicit:
• Explicit: State conclusion clearly
• Implicit: Let audience conclude (engaged audience)

EMOTIONAL APPEALS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fear appeals:
• Moderate fear most effective
• Must provide solution
• Too much fear = avoidance

Humor:
• Attracts attention
• Can distract from message
• Works best for low-involvement

Warmth/Nostalgia:
• Creates positive associations
• Builds emotional connection
• Effective for mature brands
```

## Measuring Attitudes

### Attitude Measurement Methods

```
ATTITUDE SCALES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

LIKERT SCALE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"This brand is high quality"
Strongly Disagree  1  2  3  4  5  Strongly Agree

SEMANTIC DIFFERENTIAL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cheap    ───┬───┬───┬───┬───┬───┬───   Expensive
            1   2   3   4   5   6   7

Low quality ───┬───┬───┬───┬───┬───┬─── High quality
                1   2   3   4   5   6   7

ATTITUDE TOWARD AD (Aad):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"Rate this ad on the following:"
Good/Bad, Likeable/Unlikeable, Interesting/Boring

PURCHASE INTENT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"How likely are you to buy?"
Definitely not  1  2  3  4  5  6  7  Definitely will

NET PROMOTER SCORE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"How likely to recommend (0-10)?"
Detractors (0-6) - Promoters (9-10) = NPS

IMPLICIT MEASURES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Implicit Association Test (IAT)
• Response latency
• Physiological measures
• Eye tracking
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Attitudes have three components** - Affect, behavior, cognition
2. **Functions differ** - Match strategy to attitude function
3. **Routes matter** - Central for involved, peripheral for not
4. **Use Cialdini wisely** - Reciprocity, social proof, scarcity
5. **Resistance is real** - Build trust before persuading
6. **Measure and track** - Attitudes predict behavior
