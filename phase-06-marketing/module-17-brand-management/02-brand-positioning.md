# Brand Positioning

## Learning Objectives

- Develop compelling brand positioning strategies
- Create effective positioning statements
- Differentiate brands in crowded markets
- Apply positioning frameworks
- Execute repositioning when needed

---

## Brand Positioning Fundamentals

### What Is Positioning?

```
POSITIONING DEFINED:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"The act of designing the company's offering and
image to occupy a distinctive place in the mind
of the target market."
                                        — Kotler

"Positioning is not what you do to a product.
Positioning is what you do to the mind of the
prospect."
                                — Ries & Trout

POSITIONING COMPONENTS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Target:       Who are we for?
Frame:        What category are we in?
Point of Difference:  Why choose us?
Reason to Believe:    Why believe us?
```

### The Positioning Statement

```
POSITIONING STATEMENT FORMAT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For [TARGET CUSTOMER]
Who [STATEMENT OF NEED/OPPORTUNITY]
[BRAND NAME] is the [FRAME OF REFERENCE]
That [POINT OF DIFFERENCE]
Because [REASON TO BELIEVE]

EXAMPLE - VOLVO:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For upscale families
Who desire a stylish car that meets their
active lifestyle
Volvo is the premium automobile
That offers maximum safety with
Scandinavian design
Because of its heritage of safety innovations
and engineering excellence

EXAMPLE - APPLE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For creative individuals and professionals
Who want technology that inspires
Apple is the technology company
That delivers beautifully designed products
that just work
Because of its relentless focus on user
experience and integration
```

## Points of Parity and Difference

### POP and POD Framework

```
POINTS OF PARITY (POP):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Associations not unique to brand but necessary
for credibility

CATEGORY POPs:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Essential to be considered in category
• For airlines: safety, routes
• For fast food: speed, cleanliness
• For banks: security, convenience

COMPETITIVE POPs:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Negate competitor's point of difference
• Match competitor strengths
• Neutralize advantages
• Level playing field

POINTS OF DIFFERENCE (POD):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strong, favorable, unique associations

POD CRITERIA:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Desirable: Customers want it
• Deliverable: Company can provide
• Differentiating: Competitors don't have

COMMON POD BASES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Product performance
• Customer service
• Innovation
• Heritage/authenticity
• Social responsibility
• Price/value
```

### Positioning Strategies

```
POSITIONING STRATEGY OPTIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ATTRIBUTE POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on specific product attribute
Example: Crest (cavity prevention)
Risk: Narrow, can be copied

BENEFIT POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on benefit delivered
Example: Volvo (safety)
Risk: Benefit must be relevant

USER POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Focus on type of user
Example: Apple (creative professionals)
Risk: Limits market

COMPETITOR POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Position against competitor
Example: Avis (#2, we try harder)
Risk: Gives competitor attention

CATEGORY POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Position as category leader/definer
Example: Red Bull (energy drinks)
Risk: Category ownership challenges

VALUE POSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Price-quality positioning
Example: Walmart (everyday low prices)
Risk: Commoditization
```

## Differentiation

### Sources of Differentiation

```
DIFFERENTIATION DIMENSIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PRODUCT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Features and functionality
• Performance and quality
• Design and aesthetics
• Durability and reliability
• Innovation and technology

SERVICE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Customer service excellence
• Speed and convenience
• Customization
• Support and training
• Ease of doing business

CHANNEL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Distribution exclusivity
• Channel experience
• Availability and access
• Partner expertise

PEOPLE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Employee expertise
• Customer relationships
• Culture and values
• Leadership

IMAGE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Brand personality
• Symbols and imagery
• Heritage and authenticity
• Aspirational associations
```

### Sustainable Differentiation

```
DIFFERENTIATION SUSTAINABILITY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EASY TO COPY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Product features
• Price points
• Advertising messages
• Promotional offers

HARDER TO COPY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Service excellence
• Company culture
• Customer relationships
• Brand associations

VERY HARD TO COPY:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Brand equity (built over time)
• Innovation culture
• Network effects
• Ecosystem lock-in

DEFENSIVE STRATEGIES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Patents and IP
• Continuous innovation
• Customer switching costs
• Strong brand relationships
• First-mover advantages
```

## Repositioning

### When to Reposition

```
REPOSITIONING TRIGGERS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MARKET CHANGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Consumer preferences shift
• New competitors enter
• Technology disruption
• Demographic changes

COMPETITIVE CHANGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Competitor repositions
• Market share loss
• Differentiation erodes
• Price pressure

COMPANY CHANGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• New capabilities
• Strategic shift
• M&A activity
• Leadership change

BRAND PROBLEMS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Negative associations
• Aging brand
• Crisis damage
• Relevance decline
```

### Repositioning Strategies

```
REPOSITIONING OPTIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

REAL REPOSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Change the actual product/service
• New features or quality
• Different target market
• Enhanced experience

PSYCHOLOGICAL REPOSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Change perceptions without product change
• New messaging
• Updated brand identity
• Different associations

COMPETITIVE REPOSITIONING:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Reframe the competition
• Change frame of reference
• Attack competitor position
• Create new category

EXAMPLE - OLD SPICE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Before: Old-fashioned, father's brand
After: Confident, humorous, masculine

Real changes:
• New product formulations
• Modern packaging

Perceptual changes:
• "The Man Your Man Could Smell Like"
• Humorous, edgy advertising
• Social media engagement
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Positioning is mental** - Happens in customer's mind
2. **POP enables, POD wins** - Parity qualifies, difference wins
3. **Simple is powerful** - One clear idea
4. **Consistency builds** - Stay the course
5. **Repositioning is risky** - Lose current while building new
6. **Differentiation must sustain** - Easy to copy = no advantage
