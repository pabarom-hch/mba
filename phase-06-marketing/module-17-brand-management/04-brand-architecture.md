# Brand Architecture

## Learning Objectives

- Design effective brand portfolio strategies
- Choose appropriate brand architecture models
- Make strategic naming decisions
- Balance leverage and flexibility
- Manage multi-brand portfolios

## Brand Architecture Models

### Architecture Spectrum

```
BRAND ARCHITECTURE SPECTRUM:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

BRANDED HOUSE ←─────────────────────→ HOUSE OF BRANDS
    │                                        │
    │                                        │
    ▼                                        ▼
One master brand                   Independent brands
across all products                for each product

Google          Marriott          P&G
├─Google Search ├─Courtyard       ├─Tide
├─Google Maps   ├─Residence Inn   ├─Pampers
├─Google Drive  ├─W Hotels        ├─Gillette
├─Google Cloud  ├─Ritz-Carlton    └─Crest
└─Google Pixel  └─St. Regis

ENDORSED BRANDS            SUB-BRANDS
Marriott endorses          Apple + Product
but distinct brands        (Apple iPhone,
                          Apple Watch)
```

### Branded House

```
BRANDED HOUSE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Single master brand used across all offerings

EXAMPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Google (Search, Maps, Cloud, Pixel)
• Virgin (Atlantic, Mobile, Hotels)
• FedEx (Express, Ground, Freight)

ADVANTAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Maximum leverage of brand equity
• Marketing efficiency (one brand to build)
• Clear brand message
• Synergies across products
• Customer trust transfers

DISADVANTAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Risk concentration (problems affect all)
• Limited targeting flexibility
• Difficult to stretch to new categories
• One failure can damage all

WHEN TO USE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Products share common positioning
• Target markets overlap
• Brand has broad permission
• Synergies outweigh risks
```

### House of Brands

```
HOUSE OF BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Separate brands for each product, parent hidden

EXAMPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• P&G (Tide, Pampers, Gillette, Crest)
• Unilever (Dove, Axe, Ben & Jerry's)
• Marriott (Ritz-Carlton, W, Courtyard)

ADVANTAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Target different segments precisely
• Isolate brand risk
• Maximum flexibility
• Acquisition integration easier
• Position against each other

DISADVANTAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Expensive (multiple brands to build)
• No leverage across portfolio
• Consumer confusion possible
• Resource-intensive

WHEN TO USE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Distinct target segments
• Risk isolation important
• Acquired brands have equity
• Products unrelated
```

### Hybrid Approaches

```
ENDORSED BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sub-brands linked to master brand

Structure: [Brand] by [Parent]
           [Brand] + [Parent logo]

Examples:
• Courtyard by Marriott
• Polo by Ralph Lauren
• iPhone by Apple (implied)

ADVANTAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Leverage parent credibility
• Maintain distinct positioning
• Flexibility with security

SUB-BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Parent brand + descriptor

Structure: [Parent] [Descriptor]

Examples:
• Apple iPhone, Apple Watch, Apple TV
• BMW 3 Series, 5 Series, X5
• Microsoft Office, Windows, Azure

ADVANTAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Clear connection to parent
• Product differentiation
• Shared marketing efficiency
```

## Portfolio Strategy

### Portfolio Roles

```
BRAND PORTFOLIO ROLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRATEGIC BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Future growth engines
• High investment
• Market development
• Long-term focus

FLAGSHIP BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Represent the company
• Identity anchor
• Premium positioning
• Quality standard

CASH COW BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Generate profits
• Maintain position
• Harvest value
• Fund other brands

FLANKER BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Protect flagship
• Block competitor space
• Capture segments
• Fight brands

ENTRY-LEVEL BRANDS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Attract new customers
• Lower price point
• Gateway to portfolio
• Trade-up potential
```

### Brand Hierarchy

```
BRAND HIERARCHY LEVELS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Level 1: CORPORATE BRAND
         (General Electric)
              │
Level 2: FAMILY BRAND
         (GE Appliances)
              │
Level 3: INDIVIDUAL BRAND
         (Profile Series)
              │
Level 4: MODIFIER
         (Profile 27" Dishwasher)

NAMING DECISIONS AT EACH LEVEL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Use corporate name? (adds credibility, adds risk)
• Create family brand? (groups products)
• Individual brand? (distinct positioning)
• What modifiers? (descriptors, numbers)
```

## Naming Strategy

### Naming Approaches

```
BRAND NAME TYPES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FOUNDER NAMES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ford, Dell, Ralph Lauren, Versace
+ Authenticity, heritage
- Tied to one person

DESCRIPTIVE NAMES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
General Electric, American Airlines
+ Clear meaning
- Limited flexibility

ABSTRACT NAMES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Apple, Amazon, Nike
+ Flexible, ownable
- Requires investment to build meaning

INVENTED NAMES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Xerox, Kodak, Häagen-Dazs
+ Unique, protectable
- No inherent meaning

ACRONYMS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IBM, BMW, UPS
+ Short, memorable
- Can be cold, meaningless

NAMING CRITERIA:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
□ Memorable (easy to remember)
□ Meaningful (conveys something)
□ Likeable (positive associations)
□ Protectable (can trademark)
□ Adaptable (works globally)
□ Timeless (won't date)
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Architecture is strategy** - Reflects business strategy
2. **Spectrum of options** - Branded house to house of brands
3. **Balance leverage and risk** - More leverage = more risk
4. **Roles drive investment** - Strategic brands get resources
5. **Naming matters** - Names carry meaning and limit flexibility
6. **Evolve thoughtfully** - Changes are costly and confusing
