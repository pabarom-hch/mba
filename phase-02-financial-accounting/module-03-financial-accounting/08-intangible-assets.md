# Intangible Assets

## Learning Objectives

- Understand accounting for acquired vs. internally developed intangibles
- Master goodwill accounting and impairment testing
- Analyze R&D capitalization decisions
- Identify intangible asset quality issues
- Evaluate companies with significant intangible value

---

## Introduction

In the modern economy, intangible assets often drive more value than physical assets. Think of software companies, pharmaceutical patents, or brand value. Yet accounting rules often understate intangible assets, creating a gap between book value and market value that every CEO must understand.

---

## Core Concepts

### Types of Intangible Assets

**Identifiable Intangibles:**

| Type | Examples | Useful Life |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Patents | Technology patents | Legal life (often 20 years) |
| Trademarks | Brands, logos | Indefinite (if actively used) |
| Copyrights | Software, content | Legal life |
| Customer relationships | Customer lists | Estimated life |
| Licenses | Broadcasting, franchise | Contract term |
| Software | Developed or purchased | Estimated life (3-7 years) |
| Non-compete agreements | Employment contracts | Contract term |

**Goodwill:**
- Not separately identifiable
- Arises only from acquisitions
- Excess of purchase price over net identifiable assets

### Acquired vs. Internally Developed

**Critical Distinction:**

| Intangible | Acquired | Internally Developed |
|------------|----------|---------------------|
| Patent | Capitalize at cost | Expense R&D costs |
| Brand | Capitalize at fair value | Cannot capitalize |
| Customer relationships | Capitalize at fair value | Cannot capitalize |
| Software | Capitalize at cost | Capitalize development (after feasibility) |

**Why This Matters:**
- Acquiring a company → Intangibles on balance sheet
- Building same capabilities internally → Expenses, nothing on balance sheet
- Creates inconsistency in financial statements

### Goodwill Deep Dive

**How Goodwill Arises:**

```
Purchase Price                    $100M
Less: Fair Value of Net Assets    ($70M)
= Goodwill                         $30M
```

**What Goodwill Represents:**
- Expected synergies
- Workforce value
- Market position
- Expected future growth
- "Overpayment" (sometimes)

**Goodwill Accounting:**
- No amortization (indefinite life)
- Annual impairment testing required
- Impairment = one-way trip (cannot reverse)

### Impairment Testing

**Goodwill Impairment (Two-Step Test Simplified):**

```
Step 1: Compare fair value of reporting unit to carrying value
- If FV > CV → No impairment
- If FV < CV → Impairment exists

Step 2: Measure impairment
- Impairment = CV of Goodwill - Implied FV of Goodwill
```

**Triggers for Impairment:**
- Market cap below book value
- Significant industry/economic downturn
- Loss of key customers or contracts
- Regulatory changes
- Failed integration

**Impairment Entry:**
```
Debit: Goodwill Impairment Loss    $XX
Credit: Goodwill                   $XX
```

### R&D Accounting

**US GAAP Treatment:**
- Research: Always expense
- Development: Usually expense (some software capitalization)

**Software Development (ASC 985-20):**
- Expense until technological feasibility established
- Capitalize from feasibility to general release
- Amortize over sales life

**IFRS Difference:**
- More development costs can be capitalized
- If criteria met: Capitalize development
- More judgment involved

## Intangible Asset Analysis

### Key Metrics

**Intangibles as % of Total Assets:**
```
Intangibles / Total Assets

High % = potential quality concern (overpaid for acquisitions?)
```

**Goodwill as % of Equity:**
```
Goodwill / Shareholders' Equity

> 100% = Equity would be negative without goodwill (risky)
```

**Amortization Rate:**
```
Amortization Expense / Beginning Intangible Assets

Higher = shorter assumed lives = conservative
```

### Quality Assessment

**High Quality Intangibles:**
- Well-established brands with pricing power
- Patents with remaining useful life
- Long-term customer relationships
- Proven synergies from acquisitions

**Concerning Intangibles:**
- Large goodwill from recent acquisitions (unproven)
- Goodwill from acquisitions at premium valuations
- Intangibles in declining industries
- Companies with history of impairments

### Red Flags

| Warning Sign | Potential Issue |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Goodwill > 50% of equity | High acquisition risk |
| Recent large goodwill additions | Integration risk |
| Industry peers taking impairments | Your company may be next |
| Qualitative impairment indicators | Likely impairment coming |
| Declining ROIC despite goodwill | Acquisitions not delivering |

## Consulting Application

### M&A Due Diligence

**Intangible Asset Allocation:**
When acquiring a company, purchase price must be allocated:

```
Purchase Price                    $500M
Allocate to:
- Tangible assets (FV)            $150M
- Identifiable intangibles (FV)   $200M
  - Customer relationships          $80M
  - Technology                      $50M
  - Brand                          $40M
  - Non-compete                    $30M
- Goodwill (residual)             $150M
```

**Key Questions:**
1. What's the useful life of each intangible?
2. What discount rate was used for valuation?
3. How sensitive are values to assumptions?
4. What's the amortization impact on earnings?

### Goodwill Sustainability

**When Analyzing Acquisitive Companies:**

| Check | What to Assess |
|-------|---------------|
| Acquisition history | Track record of deals |
| Goodwill/total assets | Concentration risk |
| Historical impairments | Pattern of overpaying |
| Post-deal performance | Did synergies materialize? |
| Integration capability | Execution track record |

## CEO Application

### Managing Intangible Assets

**Strategic Considerations:**

| Asset Type | Management Focus |
|------------|-----------------|
| Patents | Maintain, extend, enforce |
| Brands | Invest in building value |
| Customer relationships | Retention, expansion |
| Technology | Continue investment, avoid obsolescence |

### Acquisition Decisions

**Before Acquiring:**
1. What intangibles are you really buying?
2. What's the appropriate purchase price?
3. What synergies justify premium?
4. Can you integrate effectively?
5. What's the risk of impairment?

**After Acquiring:**
1. Is the business performing to plan?
2. Are synergies materializing?
3. Any impairment indicators?
4. How do results compare to acquisition thesis?

### Questions for Your CFO

1. "What's our goodwill as a percentage of equity?"
2. "What assumptions drive our intangible valuations?"
3. "Are there any impairment indicators we should discuss?"
4. "How much of our intangible amortization is from acquisitions?"
5. "What's our R&D capitalization policy?"

## Worked Example

### Goodwill Impairment Analysis

**Situation:**
TechAcquire Inc. bought DataCorp for $200M three years ago:
- Net tangible assets: $50M
- Identifiable intangibles: $80M
- Goodwill: $70M

**Current State:**
- DataCorp revenue flat (was expected to grow 15%/year)
- Key customers lost to competitor
- Technology becoming obsolete
- Management estimates fair value of unit: $120M

**Impairment Test:**

```
Carrying Value of DataCorp:
  Tangible assets (now)       $40M
  Identifiable intangibles    $60M (after amortization)
  Goodwill                    $70M
  Total Carrying Value       $170M

Fair Value of Unit           $120M

CV ($170M) > FV ($120M) → Impairment exists
```

**Measuring Impairment:**

```
Fair Value of Unit           $120M
Less: FV of Tangible Assets  ($40M)
Less: FV of Identifiable Int.($50M)
Implied Goodwill FV           $30M

Goodwill CV                   $70M
Implied GW FV                ($30M)
Goodwill Impairment           $40M
```

**Journal Entry:**
```
Debit: Goodwill Impairment Loss    $40M
Credit: Goodwill                   $40M
```

**Financial Statement Impact:**
- One-time charge of $40M (non-cash)
- Goodwill reduced from $70M to $30M
- Equity reduced by $40M (after tax effect)

## Common Mistakes

### 1. Ignoring Intangible Asset Quality
**Mistake:** Treating all intangibles as equally valuable.
**Reality:** Some intangibles depreciate rapidly; others gain value.

### 2. Dismissing Goodwill Impairment
**Mistake:** Viewing impairment as "non-cash" and irrelevant.
**Reality:** Impairment reveals that acquisition value was destroyed.

### 3. Underestimating Amortization Impact
**Mistake:** Focusing on adjusted EBITDA, ignoring amortization.
**Reality:** Amortization represents real cost of acquisitions.

### 4. Not Adjusting for R&D
**Mistake:** Comparing companies without adjusting for R&D policy.
**Reality:** R&D expensing creates hidden assets for some companies.

### 5. Overvaluing Indefinite-Life Intangibles
**Mistake:** Assuming indefinite means forever.
**Reality:** Even strong brands can decline; requires ongoing investment.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Acquired vs. internal creates inconsistency** - Same asset treated differently
2. **Goodwill is the premium paid** - Represents expected synergies/growth
3. **Goodwill impairment is one-way** - Once written down, doesn't come back
4. **R&D is mostly expensed** - Creates "hidden" assets for R&D-heavy companies
5. **Quality varies enormously** - Not all intangibles are valuable
6. **Large goodwill = acquisition risk** - May indicate overpayment
7. **Watch impairment indicators** - Early warning of value destruction
