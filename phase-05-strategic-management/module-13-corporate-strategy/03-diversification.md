# Diversification Strategy

## Learning Objectives

- Distinguish types of diversification and their performance implications
- Apply frameworks to evaluate diversification decisions
- Understand when diversification creates vs. destroys value
- Analyze corporate coherence and relatedness
- Design effective diversification strategies

---

## Introduction

Should Amazon have entered cloud computing? Should Disney have acquired Fox? Diversification—expanding into new products, markets, or businesses—is among the highest-stakes decisions a corporation makes. The evidence shows most diversification fails to create value. Yet some diversified companies are spectacularly successful. Understanding when and how to diversify separates strategic excellence from expensive mistakes.

---

## Types of Diversification

### The Diversification Continuum

```
DIVERSIFICATION SPECTRUM:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SINGLE        DOMINANT    RELATED      RELATED-      UNRELATED
BUSINESS      BUSINESS    CONSTRAINED  LINKED        (CONGLOMERATE)
    |------------|------------|------------|------------|
   High                                               Low
                    RELATEDNESS

DEFINITIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Single Business:
• >95% revenue from one business
• Example: Wrigley (pre-Mars acquisition)

Dominant Business:
• 70-95% revenue from one business
• Example: McDonald's

Related Constrained:
• <70% from single business
• All businesses share common link
• Example: Johnson & Johnson (healthcare)

Related Linked:
• <70% from single business
• Businesses linked but not all share common resource
• Example: Amazon (tech platforms)

Unrelated (Conglomerate):
• <70% from single business
• No common link across businesses
• Example: Berkshire Hathaway
```

### Related vs. Unrelated

```
RELATED DIVERSIFICATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Businesses connected through:
• Shared customers
• Similar technologies
• Common capabilities
• Linked value chains

Value creation through:
• Economies of scope
• Knowledge transfer
• Cross-selling
• Shared services

Examples:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Disney:
├── Movies (Animation, Marvel, Lucasfilm)
├── Theme Parks
├── Streaming (Disney+)
├── Merchandise
└── Cruises/Resorts

All leverage same IP/content/brand

UNRELATED DIVERSIFICATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Businesses with no operational connection

Value creation through:
• Superior capital allocation
• Turnaround expertise
• Financial synergies
• Governance advantages

Examples:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Berkshire Hathaway:
├── Insurance (GEICO, reinsurance)
├── Railroads (BNSF)
├── Utilities (Berkshire Energy)
├── Consumer brands (Dairy Queen, See's)
└── Manufacturing (Precision Castparts)

No operational synergy—capital allocation skill
```

## The Performance Evidence

### What the Research Shows

```
DIVERSIFICATION AND PERFORMANCE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
META-ANALYSIS FINDINGS:

1. Related outperforms unrelated
   • Related: +5-10% vs. undiversified
   • Unrelated: -5-10% vs. undiversified

2. Moderate diversification optimal
   • Inverted-U relationship
   • Too much diversification hurts

3. Conglomerate discount
   • 10-20% below sum of parts
   • Market penalizes complexity

4. Focus premium
   • Breakups often create value
   • "Return to core" works

TYPICAL FINDINGS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
│ Diversification Type     │ Performance vs. Focused │
├──────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┤
│ Related constrained      │ +5 to +10%              │
│ Related linked           │ 0 to +5%                │
│ Unrelated               │ -5 to -15%              │
└──────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────┘
```

### Why Diversification Fails

```
COMMON FAILURE MODES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. SYNERGY ILLUSION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Claimed synergies don't materialize:
• "Cross-selling" doesn't happen
• "Shared services" cost more than savings
• Integration harder than expected
• Cultures clash

"Synergy" becomes excuse for empire-building

2. ACQUISITION PREMIUM DESTRUCTION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Overpaying for acquisitions:
• Average premium: 30-40%
• Most synergies go to seller
• Winner's curse
• Integration costs not included

Acquirers' stock drops on announcement (usually)

3. MANAGEMENT DISTRACTION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Complexity overwhelms management:
• Attention divided across businesses
• Core business neglected
• Decision-making slows
• Best talent spread thin

4. AGENCY PROBLEMS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Manager interests ≠ shareholder interests:
• Empire building (bigger = more pay)
• Reduced personal risk
• Avoiding hard decisions
• Growth as ego metric
```

## Relatedness and Value Creation

### Sources of Relatedness

```
TYPES OF RELATEDNESS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. MARKET RELATEDNESS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shared customers or channels:
• Same customer base
• Common distribution
• Shared sales force
• Cross-selling opportunities

Example: Apple
• iPhone → Mac → iPad → Watch
• Same customers buy multiple products
• Ecosystem lock-in

2. OPERATING RELATEDNESS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shared activities or resources:
• Manufacturing capabilities
• Technology platforms
• Supply chain
• R&D facilities

Example: Procter & Gamble
• Consumer goods capabilities
• Retail relationships
• Marketing expertise
• Distribution efficiency

3. STRATEGIC RELATEDNESS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Similar strategic logic/capabilities:
• Comparable success factors
• Transferable skills
• Common management approach
• Strategic alignment

Example: Danaher
• Lean manufacturing expertise
• Continuous improvement culture
• Acquisition integration skill
```

### Measuring Relatedness

```
RELATEDNESS ASSESSMENT MATRIX:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
             Business A  Business B  Business C
Business A      -         High        Medium
Business B    High          -         Low
Business C   Medium        Low          -

RELATEDNESS DIMENSIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Dimension           Weight    A-B    B-C    A-C
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shared customers     25%      High   Low    Medium
Shared technology    25%      High   Low    High
Shared capabilities  25%      High   Low    Medium
Shared resources     25%      Medium Low    Low
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Weighted average             High   Low    Medium

RULE OF THUMB:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
High relatedness: Share 3+ major dimensions
Medium relatedness: Share 1-2 dimensions
Low relatedness: No meaningful sharing
```

## Diversification Modes

### Internal Development

```
INTERNAL DEVELOPMENT (Organic Growth):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Build new business using internal resources

ADVANTAGES:
• Leverage existing capabilities
• Maintain cultural consistency
• Spread investment over time
• Learning captured internally
• No premium paid

DISADVANTAGES:
• Slow
• High failure rate
• Requires new capabilities
• Opportunity cost during build

BEST WHEN:
• Related to existing businesses
• Time not critical
• Internal capabilities strong
• Acquisition targets overpriced

EXAMPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Amazon → AWS (built from internal capability)
• Google → Chrome (internal browser development)
• Apple → Services (leveraged device base)
```

### Acquisitions

```
ACQUISITIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Buy existing business

ADVANTAGES:
• Speed to market
• Acquire capabilities
• Eliminate competitor
• Immediate revenue/profit
• Proven business model

DISADVANTAGES:
• Premium (30-40% typical)
• Integration challenges
• Culture clash
• Hidden problems
• 70%+ fail to create value

BEST WHEN:
• Speed critical
• Capabilities hard to build
• Target has unique assets
• Integration manageable
• Price reasonable

ACQUISITION TYPES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Horizontal: Same industry (market share)
Vertical: Up/down value chain (integration)
Related: Adjacent industry (synergy)
Unrelated: Different industry (diversification)
```

### Joint Ventures and Alliances

```
STRATEGIC ALLIANCES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Collaborate with partner rather than own

ADVANTAGES:
• Shared investment/risk
• Access partner capabilities
• Flexibility
• Option to acquire later
• Learn before committing

DISADVANTAGES:
• Shared control
• Partner conflicts
• Knowledge leakage
• Coordination costs
• Unstable (50%+ fail)

BEST WHEN:
• High uncertainty
• Need partner capabilities
• Risk too high alone
• Market/regulatory requirements
• Testing before full entry

EXAMPLES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Starbucks + Pepsi (ready-to-drink coffee)
• GM + Honda (fuel cell development)
• Boeing + multiple suppliers (787)
```

## Portfolio Coherence

### Evaluating Corporate Portfolios

```
COHERENCE FRAMEWORK:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                   RELATEDNESS
                   High              Low
              ┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
         High │   COHERENT      │   INCOHERENT    │
   SYNERGY    │                 │   HIGH VALUE    │
   REALIZED   │ Disney, Apple   │   Rare cases    │
              ├─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
         Low  │  UNREALIZED     │   INCOHERENT    │
              │  POTENTIAL      │   LOW VALUE     │
              │  Integration    │                 │
              │  needed         │   Conglomerates │
              └─────────────────┴─────────────────┘

COHERENCE AUDIT QUESTIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Do businesses share customers?
2. Do businesses share capabilities?
3. Are synergies being actively captured?
4. Does management understand all businesses?
5. Is portfolio logic clear to outsiders?
6. Does corporate add value to each unit?
```

### The "Minimum Efficient Scale of Management"

```
CONCEPT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There's a limit to managerial attention
Diversification spreads management thin
Beyond threshold, value destroyed

INDICATORS OF OVER-DIVERSIFICATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• CEO can't explain all businesses coherently
• Corporate staff can't add value to all units
• Resource allocation becomes political
• Best talent doesn't flow to best opportunities
• Some businesses neglected

OPTIMAL DIVERSIFICATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• All businesses benefit from corporate
• Management can add value to each
• Resource allocation is strategic
• Synergies are actively managed
• Portfolio logic is clear
```

## Diversification Strategies

### Growth Diversification

```
WHEN TO DIVERSIFY FOR GROWTH:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
✓ Core market mature or declining
✓ Core market limited in size
✓ Excess capabilities to leverage
✓ Attractive adjacencies available
✓ Strong balance sheet

GROWTH PATHS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Market development (same products, new geographies)
2. Product development (new products, same customers)
3. Related diversification (new products, new customers)
4. Unrelated diversification (purely new)

RISK LADDER:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                    High
                      ↑
            Unrelated │
                      │
            Related   │
                      │
         New products │
                      │
         New markets  │
                      ↓
                    Low
```

### Value Diversification

```
DIVERSIFICATION TO CREATE VALUE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Not growth for growth's sake—value creation

VALUE CREATION MECHANISMS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Economies of scope
   • Shared resources reduce costs
   • Combined > Sum of parts

2. Market power
   • Cross-selling
   • Bundling
   • Competitive position

3. Resource leverage
   • Under-utilized assets
   • Excess capabilities
   • Brand extension

4. Learning/capability building
   • New skills acquired
   • Competitive improvement
   • Future options created
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Related usually beats unrelated** - Synergy-based diversification outperforms
2. **Most diversification destroys value** - 70% of acquisitions fail
3. **Synergies must be real and captured** - Not just claimed
4. **Mode matters** - Build, buy, or ally based on circumstances
5. **Relatedness has multiple dimensions** - Market, operating, strategic
6. **Management attention is finite** - Over-diversification spreads it thin
7. **Portfolio coherence is key** - Clear logic, captured synergies
8. **Corporate must add value** - Or divest
