# Competitive Positioning

## Learning Objectives

- Distinguish between the three generic competitive strategies
- Analyze competitive positioning through strategic trade-offs
- Evaluate positioning decisions using the value chain
- Identify the "stuck in the middle" trap
- Apply positioning frameworks to real companies

---

## Introduction

Competitive positioning answers the question: "How will we compete?" While industry analysis reveals the average profitability of an industry, positioning determines which firms earn above-average returns within that industry. Superior positioning comes from making different choices than competitors—choices that create unique value for customers.

```
FUNDAMENTAL QUESTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. Lower costs than competitors (cost leadership)
2. Differentiated offering commanding premium (differentiation)

TWO SCOPES:

2. Narrow focus (segment)
```

## Cost Leadership

### Strategic Logic

```
GOAL: Become the lowest-cost producer in the industry
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Price at or near industry average
Earn above-average profits due to cost advantage
OR price below to gain share (while still profitable)

VALUE CREATION:
Customer WTP: Industry average
Cost: Below industry average
Value captured: Above industry average
```

### Sources of Cost Advantage

```
1. ECONOMIES OF SCALE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fixed costs spread over more units
Purchasing power with suppliers
Specialization of labor and equipment

Example: Walmart's distribution
• 150 distribution centers
• Cross-docking efficiency
• 2-3% cost advantage in logistics

2. LEARNING CURVE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Costs decline with cumulative experience
Process improvements
Labor efficiency

Example: Solar panel manufacturing
• Costs decline 20%+ per doubling of volume
• First movers accumulate experience advantage

3. CAPACITY UTILIZATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fixed costs spread over more output
Higher utilization = lower unit costs

Example: Airlines
• Southwest 12+ hours/day aircraft utilization
• Industry average: 8-10 hours

4. LINKAGES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Coordinating activities reduces total costs
Upstream/downstream integration

Example: Zara
• Vertical integration (design, manufacture, retail)
• Fast fashion = less markdowns = lower total cost

5. INTERRELATIONSHIPS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sharing activities with other business units
Common distribution, manufacturing, etc.

Example: P&G
• Shared sales force across brands
• Common distribution network

6. TIMING
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
First mover in processes
Capturing favorable locations or resources

7. POLICY CHOICES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Deliberate decisions to lower costs
Product design, level of service, delivery method
```

### Cost Leadership Example: Costco

```
COST DRIVERS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Limited SKUs (4,000 vs. 50,000 at supermarket)
• Warehouse format (no frills)
• Bulk packaging (customer does labor)
• High volume per SKU (negotiating power)
• Efficient labor (high pay → low turnover)
• Low marketing (word of mouth)
• Membership model (customer lock-in)

COST POSITION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Operating expense: 10% of sales (vs. 20%+ traditional)
Gross margin: 11% (vs. 25%+ grocery)
Net margin: 2-3% (sustainable on volume)

STRATEGIC TRADE-OFFS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Limited selection (no variety)
• Bulk only (not for small households)
• No delivery (until recently)
• Sparse experience (warehouse)

These REINFORCE cost advantage
Cannot serve everyone, and that's the point
```

## Differentiation

### Strategic Logic

```
GOAL: Offer unique attributes valued by customers
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Command price premium above cost of differentiation
Customers choose you despite higher price
Create barriers through customer loyalty/switching costs

VALUE CREATION:
Customer WTP: Above industry average
Cost: At or above industry average
Value captured: Above industry average (from premium)
```

### Sources of Differentiation

```
1. PRODUCT FEATURES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unique functionality, performance, design
Quality and reliability
Innovation and technology

Example: Dyson
• Bagless technology
• Design innovation
• Premium pricing ($500 vs. $100)

2. SERVICE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Customer support, customization, speed
Expertise and consultation
Problem resolution

Example: Nordstrom
• Liberal return policy
• Personal shoppers
• Employee empowerment

3. BRAND AND REPUTATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Signal of quality, status, identity
Emotional connection
Trust and consistency

Example: Apple
• Brand commands 30%+ premium
• Aspirational identity
• Ecosystem loyalty

4. NETWORK EFFECTS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Value increases with number of users
Creates switching costs

Example: Microsoft Office
• Universal standard
• Network of compatible users
• Training and familiarity

5. CUSTOMIZATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tailored to individual customer needs
Mass customization capabilities

Example: Dell (historically)
• Build-to-order
• Customer configuration
• Direct relationship

6. CONVENIENCE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ease of purchase, access, use
Time savings for customer

Example: Amazon
• Prime delivery
• One-click ordering
• Vast selection
```

### Differentiation Example: Starbucks

```
DIFFERENTIATION DRIVERS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• "Third place" experience (not home, not work)
• Consistent quality globally
• Customization (millions of drink variations)
• Brand identity and status
• Location convenience
• Loyalty program

PREMIUM CAPTURED:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starbucks latte: $5.00
Dunkin' latte: $3.50
McDonald's latte: $2.50
Home-made: $0.50

Premium: 40%+ over competition

COST OF DIFFERENTIATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Real estate (prime locations)
• Labor (barista training)
• Store design and ambiance
• Coffee sourcing (ethical/quality)

Net margin: ~15% (premium exceeds costs)

STRATEGIC TRADE-OFFS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Cannot be cheapest option
• Limited food menu (core is coffee)
• Longer wait times (customization)
• Polarizing for some customers
```

## Focus Strategies

### Narrow Scope Logic

```
GOAL: Serve a specific segment better than broad players
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Segment has distinct needs
Broad competitors under-serve or over-serve
Focused player can optimize for segment

TYPES:
Cost Focus: Lowest cost for that segment
Differentiation Focus: Best for that segment's needs
```

### Focus Strategy Examples

```
COST FOCUS: Spirit Airlines
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Segment: Ultra-price-sensitive leisure travelers
Strategy: Bare-bones service, lowest fares

Trade-offs:
• No free carry-on (extra fee)
• Tight seating (more passengers)
• Limited routes (high-density only)
• No connections (point-to-point)

Result: Lowest costs in industry
        Serves segment broad carriers ignore

DIFFERENTIATION FOCUS: Porsche
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Segment: Sports car enthusiasts
Strategy: Ultimate driving experience

Trade-offs:
• Not practical for families
• High price ($60K+)
• Limited model range
• Specific use case

Result: Highest margins in auto industry
        Dominant in chosen segment
```

## Stuck in the Middle

### The Danger Zone

```
DEFINITION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Company pursuing multiple strategies simultaneously
Not the lowest cost, not differentiated
Average at everything, excellent at nothing

SYMPTOMS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• "We compete on price AND quality"
• Inconsistent positioning messages
• Conflicting operational priorities
• Average margins, average growth
• Losing to focused competitors on all fronts

WHY IT HAPPENS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Fear of trade-offs
2. Organic growth leads to drift
3. Belief that "we can do both"
4. Reaction to competitive pressure
5. Acquisition integration challenges
```

### Stuck in the Middle Example: Sears

```
HISTORICAL CONTEXT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1960s-1980s: Dominant retailer
Strategy: Middle-class department store

THE SQUEEZE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cost leaders: Walmart, Target
Differentiated: Nordstrom, Macy's
Category killers: Home Depot, Best Buy

Sears response:
• Neither cheapest (Walmart beat them)
• Nor best quality/experience
• Undifferentiated brands
• Aging stores and demographics
• Unclear value proposition

RESULT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2018: Bankruptcy
From Fortune 1 → gone
Could not compete on any dimension
```

### Avoiding Stuck in the Middle

```
REQUIREMENTS FOR SUSTAINABLE POSITION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Make clear trade-offs
   "We will NOT do X in order to excel at Y"

2. Align all activities
   Every decision supports chosen strategy

3. Resist "me too" moves
   Don't copy competitors' strategies

4. Communicate positioning clearly
   Customers and employees understand

5. Accept some customers aren't for you
   Focus on target segment
```

### Hybrid Strategy

```
CAN YOU PURSUE BOTH COST AND DIFFERENTIATION?
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Traditional view: No, trade-offs required
Contemporary view: Sometimes possible with:

1. TECHNOLOGICAL INNOVATION
   Reduce costs while improving quality
   Example: Toyota Production System

2. PROCESS INNOVATION
   New ways of delivering value
   Example: Amazon (selection + low prices)

3. FIRST MOVER ADVANTAGES
   Scale + innovation simultaneously
   Example: Tesla (EV + premium)

4. RESOURCE ABUNDANCE
   Reinvest profits in both dimensions
   Rare and usually temporary

CAUTION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Hybrid positions often unstable
Competitors specialize and attack
Must have sustainable source of both advantages
```

## Strategic Trade-offs

### Why Trade-offs Are Essential

```
TRADE-OFFS CREATE SUSTAINABLE ADVANTAGE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. INCOMPATIBILITY
   Activities contradict each other
   Cannot do both well simultaneously

   Example: IKEA
   • Self-service (cost reduction)
   • No delivery (cost reduction)
   • Incompatible with full-service model

2. INCONSISTENCY IN IMAGE
   Confuses customers
   Dilutes brand meaning

   Example: Mercedes-Benz
   • Premium brand
   • Budget models damaged perception
   • Confused positioning

3. INTERNAL COORDINATION
   Different activities require different resources
   Optimization for one hurts other

   Example: Newspaper
   • Digital (speed, breadth)
   • Print (depth, permanence)
   • Different skills, processes, economics
```

## Positioning Analysis Framework

### Step-by-Step Approach

```
STEP 1: MAP THE COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Identify key competitors
• Plot on positioning matrix
• Identify strategic groups

STEP 2: UNDERSTAND CUSTOMER SEGMENTS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Who are buyers?
• What do they value?
• How do they make decisions?
• What would they pay premium for?

STEP 3: ANALYZE COMPETITIVE POSITIONS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• What is each competitor's position?
• What are their trade-offs?
• Where are gaps in the market?

STEP 4: ASSESS YOUR POSITION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• What is your current position?
• Is it defensible?
• Are trade-offs clear and reinforcing?

STEP 5: IDENTIFY POSITIONING OPTIONS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• What positions are available?
• What resources required?
• What trade-offs necessary?

STEP 6: EVALUATE AND CHOOSE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Which position best matches capabilities?
• Which is most defensible?
• Which creates most value for customers?
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Generic strategies are archetypes** - Cost leadership, differentiation, or focus
2. **Trade-offs are essential** - Cannot be best at everything
3. **Stuck in the middle is deadly** - Clear positioning required
4. **Sources of advantage differ** - Cost from scale, learning; differentiation from features, brand
5. **Focus can be powerful** - Segment specialists can outperform generalists
6. **Hybrid is possible but rare** - Requires special circumstances
7. **Positioning must be consistent** - All activities align with strategy
8. **Markets evolve** - Positions must adapt over time
