# Value Chain Analysis

## Learning Objectives

- Deconstruct business activities into primary and support functions
- Identify value-creating activities and cost drivers
- Analyze linkages between activities for competitive advantage
- Map activity systems to understand strategic fit
- Apply value chain analysis to competitive positioning decisions

---

## Introduction

The value chain disaggregates a firm into its strategically relevant activities to understand the behavior of costs and identify sources of differentiation. Competitive advantage arises from performing these activities better than competitors—either at lower cost or in ways that create superior customer value. Understanding your value chain is essential for making strategic choices about where to compete and how to win.

## Activity Linkages

### Internal Linkages

```
DEFINITION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Relationships between activities where the performance
of one activity affects the cost or effectiveness of another

EXAMPLE: MANUFACTURING + QUALITY
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Higher quality in operations →
  Fewer defects →
  Lower service costs →
  Better customer satisfaction →
  Lower marketing costs (word of mouth)

Cost in one area saves money in another

EXAMPLE: INVENTORY + DISTRIBUTION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
More inventory →
  Faster delivery →
  Higher customer satisfaction
BUT
More inventory →
  Higher carrying costs →
  Higher obsolescence risk

Optimization requires understanding trade-offs
```

### Vertical Linkages

```
DEFINITION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Relationships between firm's value chain and supplier
or buyer value chains

SUPPLIER LINKAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Supplier quality affects your operations
• Supplier delivery affects your inventory
• Supplier R&D affects your product development

Example: Toyota and suppliers
• Deep integration
• Joint development
• Shared cost reduction targets
• Quality at source

BUYER LINKAGES:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Your product affects buyer's value chain
• Understanding buyer's activities reveals value creation opportunities

Example: GE selling to hospitals
• Not just selling equipment
• Helping reduce total cost of care
• Training, maintenance, analytics
• Value beyond the product
```

## Activity Systems

### The Concept of Fit

```
STRATEGIC FIT:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Activities reinforce each other
Whole is greater than sum of parts
Very difficult for competitors to replicate

TYPES OF FIT:
1. Simple consistency
   Activities consistent with overall strategy

2. Reinforcement
   Activities strengthen each other

3. Optimization of effort
   Coordination reduces redundancy
```

## Value Chain Reconfiguration

### Disrupting Industry Value Chains

```
TRADITIONAL BROKERAGE VALUE CHAIN:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[Research] → [Advisory] → [Order Execution] → [Settlement]

High-cost activities:
• Extensive research departments
• Personal brokers (expensive)
• Physical branches
• Paper-based settlement

DISCOUNT BROKER RECONFIGURATION:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[Minimal Research] → [Self-Service] → [Electronic Execution]

Removed activities:
• No advisory services
• No research provision
• No personal brokers

Result: 90% cost reduction in trade execution
        Democratization of investing
        Charles Schwab, E*Trade, Robinhood
```

### Value Chain Disaggregation

```
TRADITIONAL COMPUTER VALUE CHAIN:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[Components] → [Assembly] → [Distribution] → [Retail]
All performed by integrated manufacturers (IBM)

DISAGGREGATED MODEL:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Intel: Microprocessors
Samsung: Memory
Foxconn: Assembly
Dell: Integration/Brand
Best Buy: Retail
Each specialist excels at narrow activity

IMPLICATIONS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Specialists outperform generalists in each activity
• Integration no longer source of advantage
• Coordination becomes the challenge
• New players can enter at any layer
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Value chain disaggregates activities** - Understand cost and value creation
2. **Primary and support activities** - Both contribute to competitive advantage
3. **Cost drivers vary by activity** - Scale, learning, linkages, timing
4. **Differentiation comes from activities** - What you do creates customer value
5. **Linkages create advantage** - Activities reinforce each other
6. **Fit makes imitation hard** - System of activities, not single activity
7. **Value chains evolve** - Technology enables reconfiguration
8. **Vertical linkages matter** - Supplier and buyer chains interconnect
