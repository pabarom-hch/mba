# Business Cycles

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the phases of the business cycle
- Identify where we are in the current cycle
- Recognize leading indicators of cycle turns
- Develop cycle-appropriate business strategies
- Apply NBER recession criteria

---

## Introduction

Business cycles—the recurring expansion and contraction of economic activity—are among the most important forces shaping business strategy. Cycles determine demand for your products, availability of capital, competitive intensity, and strategic opportunities.

Great CEOs don't just react to cycles; they anticipate them. This allows for countercyclical investing, opportunistic hiring, and strategic positioning that creates lasting competitive advantage.

## Core Concepts

### The Business Cycle

**Definition:** Recurring fluctuations in economic activity, characterized by periods of expansion and contraction.

**Visual Representation:**
```
Economic
Activity
    │         Peak
    │          ∧
    │         / \        Peak
    │        /   \        ∧
    │       /     \      / \
    │      /       \    /   \
    │     /         \  /     \
    │    /   Trough  \/       \
    │   /             ∨        \
    │  /                        \
    └──────────────────────────────────▶ Time

    |←Expansion→|←Recession→|←Expansion→|
```

### Four Phases of the Cycle

**1. Expansion (Recovery)**
- GDP growth accelerating
- Unemployment falling
- Business investment rising
- Consumer confidence improving
- Credit conditions easing

**2. Peak**
- Economy at full capacity
- Unemployment at lows
- Inflation pressures building
- Asset prices elevated
- Central bank tightening

**3. Contraction (Recession)**
- GDP declining
- Unemployment rising
- Business investment falling
- Consumer spending weakening
- Credit tightening

**4. Trough**
- Economy bottoming
- Unemployment peaks
- Inventories depleted
- Valuations attractive
- Policy support maximum

### NBER Recession Definition

The National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) officially dates US recessions based on:

**Criteria:** "A significant decline in economic activity that is spread across the economy and lasts more than a few months."

**Key Indicators Monitored:**
1. Real personal income less transfers
2. Nonfarm payroll employment
3. Real personal consumption expenditures
4. Wholesale-retail sales adjusted for inflation
5. Industrial production
6. Real GDP

**Note:** NBER typically declares recessions 6-12 months after they begin.

| Recession | Duration | GDP Decline | Peak Unemployment |
|-----------|----------|-------------|------------------|
| 1948-49 | 11 months | -1.7% | 7.9% |
| 1953-54 | 10 months | -2.7% | 6.1% |
| 1957-58 | 8 months | -3.7% | 7.5% |
| 1960-61 | 10 months | -1.6% | 7.1% |
| 1969-70 | 11 months | -0.6% | 6.1% |
| 1973-75 | 16 months | -3.2% | 9.0% |
| 1980 | 6 months | -2.2% | 7.8% |
| 1981-82 | 16 months | -2.7% | 10.8% |
| 1990-91 | 8 months | -1.4% | 7.8% |

| 2007-09 | 18 months | -5.1% | 10.0% |
| 2020 | 2 months | -9.1% | 14.7% |

**Key Observations:**
- Average recession: 10 months
- Average expansion: 64 months (getting longer)
- Depth varies enormously
- Each cycle has unique characteristics

### Leading Indicators of Cycle Turns

**Signs of Peak (Recession Coming):**

| Indicator | Warning Signal |
|-----------|---------------|
| Yield curve | Inversion (short > long rates) |
| Credit spreads | Widening |
| Leading Economic Index | 3+ months of decline |
| Initial jobless claims | Rising trend |
| ISM Manufacturing PMI | Below 50 |
| Housing starts | Declining |
| Consumer confidence | Falling expectations |
| Corporate profits | Declining |
| Stock market | Significant decline |

**The Yield Curve's Track Record:**
- Inverted before every recession since 1955
- Average lead time: 12-18 months
- False positive: 1966 (no recession)

**Signs of Trough (Recovery Coming):**

| Indicator | Recovery Signal |
|-----------|----------------|
| Initial claims | Peak and decline |
| Stock market | Rally off lows |
| Credit spreads | Narrowing |
| Consumer confidence | Rising expectations |
| Housing starts | Bottoming |
| Inventory levels | Very low |
| Policy stance | Maximum stimulus |

### Cycle Duration Analysis

**What Ends Expansions?**
1. **Fed tightening** (most common) - Rate hikes to fight inflation
2. **Asset bubbles** - When they pop (2000 tech, 2008 housing)
3. **External shocks** - Oil crises, pandemics, wars
4. **Financial crises** - Banking/credit system failures

**What Ends Recessions?**
1. **Policy response** - Rate cuts, fiscal stimulus
2. **Inventory cycle** - Restocking after depletion
3. **Pent-up demand** - Deferred purchases resume
4. **Natural adjustment** - Prices and wages find equilibrium

## Consulting Application

### Cycle Assessment Framework

**Step 1: Identify Current Phase**

| Phase | GDP Growth | Unemployment | Inflation | Credit |
|-------|------------|--------------|-----------|--------|
| Early Expansion | Accelerating | High, falling | Low | Easing |
| Mid Expansion | Solid | Moderate | Moderate | Normal |
| Late Expansion | Slowing | Low | Rising | Tightening |
| Recession | Negative | Rising | Various | Tight |

**Step 2: Forecast Duration**
- How long has current phase lasted?
- What's causing current phase?
- What would change it?

**Step 3: Assess Client Sensitivity**
- How cyclical is the business?
- What's the lead/lag vs. economy?
- Which customer segments are most affected?

### Cyclicality Analysis

**Classifying Industries:**

| Cyclicality | Examples | Economic Beta |
|-------------|----------|---------------|
| **Highly Cyclical** | Autos, housing, luxury goods, travel | 1.5-2.5x |
| **Cyclical** | Retail, manufacturing, advertising | 1.0-1.5x |
| **Stable** | Consumer staples, utilities, healthcare | 0.5-1.0x |
| **Counter-Cyclical** | Discount retail, debt collection, bankruptcy | Negative |

**Client Example:**
```
Question: How will a recession affect our auto parts client?

Analysis:
- Auto sales are highly cyclical (2x GDP beta)
- New car sales can drop 30-40% in recession
- But: Aftermarket parts are less cyclical (people repair vs. replace)
- Client mix: 60% OEM, 40% aftermarket

Conclusion: Expect 15-20% revenue decline in recession
Recommendation: Shift mix toward aftermarket
```

### Cycle-Appropriate Strategy

| Phase | Strategic Priority | Capital Allocation |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Early Expansion** | Invest aggressively, gain share | Increase capex, hire ahead |
| **Mid Expansion** | Optimize operations, build cash | Selective investment, returns |
| **Late Expansion** | Prepare for downturn, harvest | Reduce capex, build liquidity |
| **Recession** | Survive and acquire | Preserve cash, opportunistic M&A |

## CEO Application

### Countercyclical Strategy

**The Best CEOs Are Contrarian:**
- Invest when others retreat
- Build cash when others spend
- Hire talent when others lay off
- Acquire when prices are low

**Warren Buffett:** "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful."

**Practical Implementation:**

| Cycle Phase | Conventional Wisdom | Contrarian Action |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Late expansion | "This time is different" | Build cash reserves |
| Recession | "Hunker down, survive" | Acquire competitors, hire talent |
| Early recovery | "Wait for clarity" | Invest aggressively |
| Mid-cycle | "Everything is fine" | Plan for next downturn |

### Recession Playbook

**Pre-Recession (Late Cycle) Preparation:**

1. **Financial Fortress**
   - Build cash reserves (12+ months expenses)
   - Extend debt maturities
   - Secure credit lines while available
   - Reduce leverage

2. **Operational Flexibility**
   - Increase variable vs. fixed costs
   - Build supplier optionality
   - Cross-train employees
   - Identify cost reduction opportunities (don't execute yet)

3. **Strategic Positioning**
   - Identify acquisition targets
   - Build M&A capabilities
   - Strengthen customer relationships
   - Document talent you'd want to hire

**During Recession:**

1. **Early Actions (First 3 months)**
   - Implement pre-planned cost reductions
   - Accelerate collections
   - Communicate with stakeholders
   - Protect key talent and relationships

2. **Mid-Recession**
   - Evaluate acquisition opportunities
   - Renegotiate supplier contracts
   - Invest in efficiency
   - Hire selectively

3. **Late Recession (Trough)**
   - Execute acquisitions
   - Accelerate investment
   - Lock in talent
   - Position for recovery

### Recovery Strategy

**First 6 Months of Recovery:**
- Rebuild inventory (carefully)
- Restore marketing spend
- Begin hiring
- Invest in capacity

**Common Mistake:** Waiting for "confirmation" of recovery means missing the early gains. Markets and business conditions improve before data confirms it.

## Worked Example

### Situation

It's January 2024. You're CEO of a commercial construction company. Economic data shows:

- GDP growth: 2.5% (slowing)
- Unemployment: 3.7% (near record lows)
- Inflation: 3.5% (elevated)
- Fed funds rate: 5.25% (highest in 22 years)
- Yield curve: Inverted for 18 months
- Leading Economic Index: Declined 8 consecutive months

### Cycle Analysis

**Current Phase Assessment:**

| Indicator | Reading | Signal |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| GDP growth | Positive but slowing | Late expansion |
| Unemployment | Very low | Late expansion |
| Inflation | Elevated | Late expansion |
| Fed policy | Tight | Late expansion |
| Yield curve | Inverted | Recession warning |
| LEI | Declining | Recession warning |

**Conclusion:** Late-cycle expansion with elevated recession risk in next 12-18 months.

**Commercial Construction Specifics:**
- Construction typically lags economy by 6-12 months
- Current backlog: Strong (projects sold in 2022-23)
- New orders: Softening
- Credit conditions: Tightening for real estate

### Strategic Recommendations

**Immediate Actions (Q1):**
1. Freeze non-essential hiring
2. Review credit facility covenants
3. Accelerate receivables collection
4. Complete in-progress projects on time

**6-Month Actions:**
1. Build cash to 18 months expenses
2. Identify 15% cost reduction plan (ready to execute)
3. Negotiate supplier terms while still strong
4. Extend debt maturities
5. Document M&A targets

**If Recession Hits:**
1. Execute cost plan in 30 days
2. Maintain core project teams
3. Pursue distressed acquisitions (competitors, land)
4. Deepen government/infrastructure relationships (counter-cyclical)

**Recovery Preparation:**
1. Keep estimating capacity for quick ramp
2. Maintain relationships with key subcontractors
3. Build land bank opportunistically
4. Position for infrastructure spending

### Financial Model Scenarios

| Scenario | Probability | 2024 Revenue | 2025 Revenue | Action |
|----------|-------------|--------------|--------------|--------|
| Soft landing | 40% | +5% | +3% | Moderate investment |
| Mild recession | 45% | +2% | -15% | Execute cost plan |
| Deep recession | 15% | -5% | -30% | Major restructuring |

**Expected Value Planning:** Weight all scenarios, but have detailed plans ready for each.

## Common Mistakes

### 1. "This Time Is Different"
**Mistake:** Believing current expansion will continue indefinitely.
**Reality:** Every expansion ends. The only questions are when and how.

### 2. Waiting for Official Recession Call
**Mistake:** Waiting for NBER to declare recession before acting.
**Reality:** NBER calls recessions 6-12 months late. By then, damage is done.

### 3. Cutting to Profitability in Recession
**Mistake:** Deep cuts that damage long-term capabilities.
**Reality:** Cut intelligently—preserve strategic capabilities for recovery.

### 4. Missing the Recovery
**Mistake:** Remaining in defensive mode too long.
**Reality:** Early recovery offers best opportunities. Act before data confirms.

### 5. Procyclical Behavior
**Mistake:** Investing at peaks (when everyone else does) and cutting at troughs.
**Reality:** Best returns come from countercyclical behavior.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Cycles are inevitable** - Plan for them, don't be surprised
2. **Four phases** - Expansion, peak, contraction, trough—know where you are
3. **Lead times vary** - Yield curve inverts 12-18 months before recession
4. **Your business has unique sensitivity** - Know your economic beta
5. **Countercyclical strategy wins** - Invest when others retreat
6. **Prepare before the storm** - Build cash and flexibility in late cycle
7. **Don't miss recovery** - Act before data confirms the turn
