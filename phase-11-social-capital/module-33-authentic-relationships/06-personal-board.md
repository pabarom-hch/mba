# Building Your Personal Board of Directors

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the different roles people play in your development
- Distinguish between mentors, sponsors, advisors, and peers
- Build a personal board that supports your growth
- Maintain these relationships over time
- Contribute value while receiving guidance

---

## Introduction

No successful person builds their career alone. Behind every achievement is a network of supporters—mentors who taught, sponsors who advocated, advisors who counseled, and peers who challenged. This lesson shows you how to intentionally build and nurture your own personal board of directors.

---

### Mentors

**What They Provide:**
- Wisdom from experience
- Long-term perspective
- Career guidance
- Life lessons
- Emotional support

**How to Find Them:**
- People who've walked paths you want to walk
- Those who inspire you
- Those who take interest in others' development
- Often found through existing networks

**How to Approach:**
- Be specific about what you admire
- Ask for limited time, not indefinite commitment
- Come with specific questions
- Show you've done your homework
- Express genuine gratitude

**How to Maintain:**
- Update them on progress
- Thank them for specific impact
- Don't overuse their time
- Eventually, become a mentor to others in their honor

**The Ask:**
"I've followed your career and deeply admire [specific thing]. I'm at a point where I'm navigating [challenge]. Would you be willing to meet for coffee once to share your perspective?"

### Sponsors

**What They Provide:**
- Advocacy in rooms you're not in
- Recommendations for opportunities
- Their reputation on your behalf
- Access to their network

**How They Differ from Mentors:**
| Mentor | Sponsor |
|--------|---------|
| Teaches you | Advocates for you |
| You approach them | They choose you |
| Relationship-focused | Results-focused |
| Gives advice | Takes action |

**How to Earn Sponsors:**
You don't ask for sponsors; you earn them. Sponsors take risk by advocating for you. They must believe you'll make them look good.

- Deliver excellent work consistently
- Make your boss look good
- Build reputation within organizations
- Be visible to potential sponsors
- Show potential worth championing

**How to Nurture:**
- Deliver results that justify their faith
- Keep them informed of your progress
- Never embarrass them
- Eventually, become a sponsor for others

### Advisors

**What They Provide:**
- Subject matter expertise
- Specific guidance in their domain
- Access to their knowledge network
- Perspective on technical challenges

**Types of Advisors:**
- Financial advisor (wealth management)
- Legal advisor (legal matters)
- Career advisor (specific industry)
- Technical advisor (domain expertise)
- Strategic advisor (business decisions)

**How to Select:**
- Track record in their domain
- Reputation for integrity
- Willingness to tell you hard truths
- Alignment with your values

**How to Engage:**
- Be clear about what you need
- Respect their time
- Compensate appropriately (if professional)
- Implement their advice (or explain why not)

### Peers

**What They Provide:**
- Mutual support and understanding
- Real-time challenge and feedback
- Shared journey
- Candid perspective
- Long-term relationships

**Why Peers Matter:**
Your peers today are the leaders tomorrow. The relationships you build with equals now become your senior network later.

**How to Build Peer Relationships:**
- Invest genuinely, not strategically
- Help them without expectation
- Celebrate their successes
- Be honest about your challenges
- Maintain contact over years

**The Peer Group:**
Consider creating or joining a peer group:
- 4-8 people at similar career stages
- Meet regularly (monthly, quarterly)
- Share challenges and victories
- Hold each other accountable
- Confidentiality is essential

### Truth-Tellers

**What They Provide:**
- Honest feedback you might not want to hear
- Reality checks
- Challenge to your assumptions
- Protection from self-deception

**Why They Matter:**
The more successful you become, the less honest feedback you receive. People tell you what you want to hear. Truth-tellers counter this.

**How to Find Them:**
- Old friends who knew you before success
- Colleagues without political agenda
- Family members who stay grounded
- Anyone who's not impressed by your title

**How to Protect the Relationship:**
- Thank them for honesty
- Never punish difficult truths
- Explicitly invite their feedback
- Act on their input when valid

## Mentor Perspectives

### Warren Buffett on Mentorship

*"The best investment I ever made was spending time with people I admired and wanted to be like. My mentor, Benjamin Graham, shaped how I think about investing. But it wasn't just business—it was how to think, how to live. Everyone should have people they admire and want to emulate."*

### The Rothschild Family Tradition

*"Our family has always believed in the counsel of wise advisors. No one—not even the head of the family—makes major decisions alone. We surround ourselves with people who see what we cannot, who challenge our assumptions, who speak truth even when it's unwelcome. This is how we've endured."*

### Warren Buffett on Peers

*"Charlie Munger is my partner, but more than that, he's my intellectual sparring partner. He makes me better. Everyone needs someone who will tell them they're wrong, who will argue the other side, who won't just agree because it's easier."*

## CEO Application

### Building Your Board Intentionally

**Take Inventory:**
- Who currently mentors you?
- Who sponsors you?
- Who advises you?
- Who are your true peers?
- Who tells you hard truths?

**Identify Gaps:**
Where do you need support that you don't have?
- Specific expertise areas?
- Career stages you've not navigated?
- Industries you don't know?
- Perspectives you lack?

**Build Systematically:**
- Identify specific people who could fill gaps
- Build relationships genuinely (not transactionally)
- Be patient—these relationships take time
- Contribute value, don't just extract it

### Creating a Formal Advisory Board

For executives and entrepreneurs, a formal advisory board can be valuable:

**Structure:**
- 3-6 advisors with diverse expertise
- Clear expectations for time and contribution
- Regular meetings (quarterly is common)
- Compensation (equity, fees, or reputation)

**Selection Criteria:**
- Relevant expertise
- Complementary perspectives
- Willingness to challenge you
- Reputation that adds credibility
- Chemistry with you and each other

### Being a Board Member for Others

As you advance, you become someone else's board member:

**As a Mentor:**
- Share wisdom generously
- Invest time in those with potential
- Don't expect return on investment

**As a Sponsor:**
- Advocate for deserving people
- Stake your reputation on their success
- Open doors you've walked through

## Worked Example

### Building a Personal Board

**The Situation:**
Sarah is a 35-year-old director at a tech company considering a move to a leadership role at a larger company or an entrepreneurial venture.

**Her Current Board:**
- Mentor: Her former VP from her first job (has known her for 10 years)
- Sponsor: Her current CEO (advocates for her internally)
- Peers: Three friends from business school (monthly dinners)
- Truth-teller: Her brother (no filter)

**Her Gap Analysis:**
- No mentor who's navigated the specific transition she's considering
- No advisor with startup experience
- No sponsor at the potential larger companies

**Her Plan:**
1. Through her mentor, get introduced to someone who's made a similar transition
2. Join a peer group for executives considering entrepreneurship
3. Build relationships at target companies through industry events (long-term)
4. Seek a startup advisor through her network

**Six Months Later:**
Sarah has added:
- A mentor who transitioned from corporate to startup (connected through existing mentor)
- Joined an entrepreneur peer group (monthly meetings)
- Begun building relationships at two target companies (no immediate ask)

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Transactional Approach
**The Error:** Approaching potential board members with clear asks immediately.
**The Fix:** Build relationship first. The ask comes later, if at all.

### Mistake 2: Extracting Without Contributing
**The Error:** Only reaching out when you need something.
**The Fix:** Contribute value consistently. Be useful to them.

### Mistake 3: Not Diversifying
**The Error:** Building a board of people just like you.
**The Fix:** Seek diverse perspectives—different backgrounds, experiences, viewpoints.

### Mistake 4: Neglecting Peers
**The Error:** Focusing on senior relationships, ignoring peers.
**The Fix:** Invest in peers. They're your future senior network.

### Mistake 5: Dismissing Truth-Tellers
**The Error:** Avoiding or resenting those who give hard feedback.
**The Fix:** Treasure them. They're increasingly rare as you advance.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Different roles, different people** - Know what each type of support provides

2. **You earn sponsors, you approach mentors** - Understanding the dynamic matters

3. **Peers are your future** - Today's equals are tomorrow's leaders

4. **Truth-tellers are gold** - Protect relationships with honest people

5. **Contribute while receiving** - Add value to those who support you

6. **Build intentionally** - Know your gaps, fill them systematically
