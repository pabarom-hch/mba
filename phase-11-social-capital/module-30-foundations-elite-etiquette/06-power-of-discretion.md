# The Power of Discretion

## Learning Objectives

- Understand why discretion is the ultimate currency in elite circles
- Learn what to share, what to withhold, and how to deflect gracefully
- Master the art of keeping confidences
- Recognize why loose lips close doors
- Apply discretion in an age of oversharing

---

## Introduction

In elite circles, information is currency—and discretion is how you prove you can be trusted with it. The ability to keep confidences, avoid gossip, and reveal nothing that shouldn't be revealed is perhaps the most valued trait in these environments.

This isn't about being secretive or cold. It's about demonstrating the judgment that comes with maturity and the trustworthiness that enables real relationships. In a world of social media oversharing, discretion has become increasingly rare—and therefore increasingly valuable.

---

## Core Concepts

### Why Discretion Matters

**The Trust Economy**

In UHNW circles, real business often happens through relationships. These relationships only form when trust exists. Trust requires confidence that sensitive information—about deals, families, personal matters—stays private.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                 THE DISCRETION-TRUST CYCLE                       │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                  │
│   Discretion demonstrated → Trust extended                       │
│                     ↓                                           │
│   Trust extended → Access granted                                │
│                     ↓                                           │
│   Access granted → Sensitive information shared                  │
│                     ↓                                           │
│   Sensitive information → Discretion tested                      │
│                     ↓                                           │
│   Discretion maintained → Deeper trust/access                    │
│                                                                  │
│   OR                                                             │
│                                                                  │
│   Indiscretion → Trust destroyed → Access revoked → Reputation  │
│                  damaged → Doors closed permanently              │
│                                                                  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**The Compound Effect**

Reputation for discretion compounds. Word spreads (quietly) that you can be trusted. Doors open. Conversations happen that wouldn't otherwise. The compound effect of a decade of discretion is access you couldn't buy at any price.

Conversely, one indiscretion can destroy years of relationship building.

### What Discretion Covers

**Financial Information**

Never discuss:
- Others' wealth, income, or finances
- Deal terms you're privy to
- Investment returns (yours or others')
- What someone paid for something
- Compensation information

**Personal and Family Matters**

Never share:
- Health issues of others
- Family conflicts or struggles
- Relationship problems
- Children's difficulties
- Personal vulnerabilities shared with you

**Business Intelligence**

Never reveal:
- Non-public company information
- M&A activity (even rumors)
- Personnel decisions before announced
- Strategic discussions
- Private conversations with principals

**Social Intelligence**

Be careful with:
- Who was at what event
- Who was with whom
- What happened at private gatherings
- Details of others' homes, properties, lifestyles

### The Art of Deflection

When asked about sensitive topics, deflect without appearing evasive:

**Redirect to Public Information:**
Q: "What do you think the Johnsons are worth?"
A: "I really don't know—but I've read their foundation does remarkable work."

**Acknowledge Without Revealing:**
Q: "Were you at their party last weekend?"
A: "I try to keep social calendars private—I'm sure you understand."

**Express Ignorance (Real or Diplomatic):**
Q: "I heard Company X is being sold—what do you know?"
A: "That's news to me. I tend to be the last to hear these things."

**Gentle Boundary:**
Q: "What did James tell you about his divorce?"
A: "I don't feel comfortable discussing James's personal matters. How about that game last night?"

### The Gossip Trap

Gossip is seductive. It feels like connection and inside access. But engaging in it signals poor judgment.

**How to Handle Gossip**

1. **Don't initiate** - Never be the source
2. **Don't amplify** - Don't add to or forward gossip
3. **Change the subject** - Steer conversation elsewhere
4. **Express discomfort** - "I'd rather not discuss them when they're not here"
5. **Refuse to engage** - "I really don't know anything about that"

**The Test**

People sometimes share gossip to test your discretion. If you engage enthusiastically, they know you can't be trusted. The appropriate response—disinterest or deflection—passes the test.

### Discretion in Writing

Written communication creates permanent records.

**Email and Text Caution:**
- Assume everything can be forwarded or leaked
- Never put truly sensitive information in writing
- Keep messages brief and factual
- Avoid speculation, opinions about others, or sensitive details

**Social Media:**
- Minimal presence in UHNW circles is expected
- Never post about exclusive events or gatherings
- Never tag or photograph others without permission
- Never check in at private locations

**Even Private Messages:**
- Can be screenshotted
- Can be subpoenaed
- Can be hacked
- Write as if they're public

### The Positive Expression of Discretion

Discretion isn't just about withholding. It's also about:

**Protecting Others' Privacy Proactively:**
- Not asking intrusive questions
- Not pressing when someone deflects
- Respecting unspoken boundaries
- Giving others room to share (or not) on their terms

**Creating Safe Spaces:**
- People share more when they sense you're discreet
- Your reputation enables others' openness
- This creates deeper relationships and more meaningful access

**Demonstrating Judgment:**
- Knowing what matters and what doesn't
- Understanding context and appropriateness
- Reading social cues about sensitivity

## Mentor Perspectives

### Warren Buffett on Reputation

*"It takes 20 years to build a reputation and five minutes to ruin it."*

Buffett understands that reputation for discretion is built slowly and destroyed instantly. One leak, one gossip session, one careless comment can undo decades of relationship building.

### The Rothschild Principle

*"Never discuss family business outside the family."*

The Rothschilds' legendary discretion is central to their multi-generational success. What happens inside stays inside—period.

### Emily Post on Privacy

*"A person of good breeding never discusses money—either their own or anyone else's."*

Post's rule extends beyond money: a person of good breeding doesn't discuss the private matters of others.

### Benjamin Franklin

*"Three may keep a secret, if two of them are dead."*

Franklin's cynicism is instructive: be careful who you share secrets with, and be the person others can trust implicitly.

## CEO Application

### Board Confidentiality

As CEO, board discussions are privileged:
- Never share board deliberations outside the boardroom
- Personnel discussions, especially, are sacred
- Strategic discussions remain confidential until publicly announced
- Even the existence of certain discussions may be sensitive

### Investor Relations

With current and potential investors:
- Share what you should, withhold what you must
- Don't discuss other investors' positions or views
- Don't share non-public information (legal and reputational risk)
- Be known for appropriate discretion, not loose talk

### Personal Relationships

In social settings where you might discuss business:
- Keep confidential matters confidential, even over drinks
- Don't use personal relationships to extract business intelligence
- Be aware of who's listening
- Remember that spouses and friends can inadvertently share

### Media Relations

With press or media:
- Be thoughtful about what's on/off record
- Build relationships with journalists based on mutual trust
- Never leak to damage others—it will come back to you
- Protect your sources as you'd want to be protected

## Worked Example

### Scenario

You're at a dinner party. Another guest, who knows you're connected to a prominent family, asks: "I hear the Williams family is selling their company—what do you know?"

You do, in fact, know something. The Williams asked you for confidential advice. The deal hasn't been announced.

### Wrong Responses

**Confirmation:** "Yes, they're looking at offers around $500 million."
→ Betrays confidence, possibly securities violation.

**Wink-and-nod:** "I couldn't possibly comment, but watch the news next week."
→ Still signals confirmation; not actually discreet.

**Visible discomfort:** Looking uncomfortable and saying nothing.
→ Your discomfort signals you know something.

### Right Response

**Calm ignorance:** "I haven't heard that. Where did you read it?"

Then smoothly transition: "Speaking of business news, did you see that piece in the Journal about..."

### Why This Works

- No confirmation or denial
- No visible discomfort (discomfort reveals knowledge)
- Suggests you're out of the loop (even if you're not)
- Moves conversation to neutral ground
- Leaves no impression you're keeping secrets

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Thinking "Just This Once" Is OK
**Why it happens:** The situation feels safe, the listener seems trustworthy.
**The reality:** Once is all it takes to establish you can be compromised. And you never fully know if the listener is trustworthy.

### Mistake 2: Trading Information for Access
**Why it happens:** Sharing inside information seems like a way to build relationships.
**The reality:** It marks you as untrustworthy. The right people want discreet connections, not leaky ones.

### Mistake 3: Confusing Venting with Discretion Failure
**Why it happens:** Everyone needs to process frustrations.
**The fix:** Vent only to completely trusted advisors (therapist, spouse, very close friend outside the circle). Even then, be careful.

### Mistake 4: Written Documentation
**Why it happens:** Convenience, habit, wanting records.
**The fix:** Sensitive matters should be handled in person or by phone. Never in writing.

### Mistake 5: Social Media
**Why it happens:** Everyone else is sharing.
**The fix:** Dramatically reduce or eliminate social media presence. Never post about exclusive settings, people, or events.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Discretion is currency** - It's how you prove trustworthiness and gain access to real relationships and opportunities.

2. **Reputation compounds** - Years of discretion build reputation; one slip destroys it.

3. **Never confirm or deny** - The art is appearing ignorant, not evasive.

4. **Written = permanent** - Assume all written communication can become public.

5. **Gossip is a trap** - Engaging tests your judgment and finds it wanting.

6. **Discretion is active** - It's not just withholding but creating safe space for others.

## The Discretion Checklist

Before sharing any information, ask:

- [ ] Is this my information to share?
- [ ] Would the subject want this shared?
- [ ] What's my motivation for sharing?
- [ ] Could this harm anyone if it traveled?
- [ ] Would I be comfortable if this got back to the source?
- [ ] Am I creating a record (written, screenshot-able)?
- [ ] Is this person truly trustworthy?

If you hesitate on any answer, don't share.
