# Forms of Address and Titles

## Learning Objectives

- Navigate the use of titles, honorifics, and names correctly
- Understand when to use first names vs. surnames
- Handle professional and aristocratic titles appropriately
- Know the rules for written correspondence addressing
- Avoid common mistakes that signal unfamiliarity

---

## Introduction

In casual modern settings, we default to first names almost immediately. In elite circles, particularly those with older generations, European connections, or formal traditions, the rules are more nuanced. Getting forms of address wrong signals unfamiliarity; getting them right signals respect and awareness.

This lesson covers the norms for American and European contexts, with emphasis on practical application.

---

## Core Concepts

### The Default: Mr./Mrs./Ms. + Surname

**When in Doubt, Be Formal**

Until invited otherwise, use:
- **Mr.** + surname for men
- **Mrs.** + surname for married women
- **Ms.** + surname when marital status is unknown or woman prefers
- **Miss** + surname for unmarried women (less common today)

**Example:**
Even if introduced as "James Matthews," address him as "Mr. Matthews" until he says "Please, call me James."

**Never Assume First Names**

Common mistake: Someone introduces themselves with their full name ("I'm James Matthews"), and you immediately call them "James." In formal contexts, they've given you their full name; use the surname until invited to use the first.

### Professional Titles

**Doctors**

- **Medical doctors and PhDs**: "Dr. [Surname]"
- In social contexts, MD trumps PhD in usage
- Never call yourself "Doctor" in social settings (let others use it)
- If unsure about their preference, use Dr. initially

**Judges**

- **Active judges**: "Judge [Surname]" or "Your Honor" in court
- **Retired judges**: "Judge [Surname]" (title retained)
- **Supreme Court Justices**: "Justice [Surname]"

**Military (Retired)**

- Retired officers often retain their rank socially: "Colonel Williams"
- If unsure, use the rank if they're introduced with it

**Clergy**

- **Priests**: "Father [Surname]" (Catholic, Orthodox) or "Reverend [Surname]"
- **Bishops**: "Bishop [Surname]" or "Your Excellency"
- **Ministers**: "Reverend [Surname]" or "Pastor [Surname]"
- **Rabbis**: "Rabbi [Surname]"

### Political and Diplomatic Titles

**US Political Figures**

```

```

**Key Point:** Former presidents, governors, senators, and ambassadors generally retain their titles in address.

### Aristocratic and Honorary Titles (British/European)

In international contexts, you may encounter:

**British Peerage (in descending order):**
- **Duke/Duchess**: "Your Grace" or "Duke/Duchess [of Territory]"
- **Marquess/Marchioness**: "Lord/Lady [Territory]"
- **Earl/Countess**: "Lord/Lady [Territory]"
- **Viscount/Viscountess**: "Lord/Lady [Territory]"
- **Baron/Baroness**: "Lord/Lady [Surname]"

**Knights and Dames:**
- **Sir/Dame** [First name] [Surname] - e.g., "Sir David Smith"
- After introduction: "Sir David" (never "Sir Smith")

**European Variations:**
- German: "Herr/Frau" + title + surname (Herr Dr. Schmidt)
- French: "Monsieur/Madame" + title
- Italian: "Signor/Signora" + title

**When Uncertain**

If introduced to European aristocracy and unsure:
- Listen carefully to how they're introduced
- Follow how others address them
- "Lord [Surname]" or "Lady [Surname]" is generally safe
- When in doubt, ask quietly: "How should I address the Countess?"

### The Transition to First Names

**How It Happens**

The higher-status or older person initiates:
- "Please, call me Sarah"
- "I'm James to my friends"
- "No need for the formality—Elizabeth is fine"

**Until then:** Maintain Mr./Mrs./Ms./Title.

**Generational Differences**

- Older generations (70+): More likely to maintain formal address longer
- Boomers (60-70): Often transition quickly but appreciate initial formality
- Younger generations: May find sustained formality awkward—read their cues

**Business vs. Social**

Sometimes you're on first-name basis in business but should revert to formality in social settings with their family, or vice versa. Read the context.

### Written Correspondence

**Addressing Envelopes:**

```
Mr. and Mrs. James William Matthews
47 East 64th Street
New York, New York 10065

The Honorable Sarah L. Wilson
United States Senate
Washington, D.C. 20510

His Excellency James R. Thompson
Ambassador of the United States
[Embassy Address]
```

**Salutations in Letters:**

| Addressee | Salutation |
|-----------|------------|
| Social acquaintance | Dear Mr. Matthews, |
| Close friend | Dear James, |
| Couple | Dear Mr. and Mrs. Matthews, |
| Judge | Dear Judge Williams, |
| Senator | Dear Senator Wilson, |
| Ambassador | Dear Mr./Madam Ambassador, |

**Closings:**

- Formal: "Sincerely," "Respectfully,"
- Less formal: "Warmly," "Best regards,"
- Never: "Cheers," "Best," "Thanks" (too casual for formal correspondence)

## Mentor Perspectives

### Emily Post on Names

*"To address a person by the wrong name is to say 'You don't matter enough for me to remember.' To address them correctly is a small gift of recognition."*

Post reminds us that forms of address are fundamentally about respect and attention.

### Letitia Baldrige on Transitions

*"The art is in the transition—knowing when formality serves and when it creates distance. A young executive calling everyone by first name may seem presumptuous; an older one clinging to formality may seem cold."*

Baldrige emphasizes reading context and adapting appropriately.

### European Perspective

*"In America, you use first names after five minutes. In Europe, you might wait five years."* — Common observation

Understanding these differences matters in international contexts.

## CEO Application

### Board Relationships

- Initially address board members as Mr./Ms./Mrs. [Surname]
- Follow their lead on transitioning to first names
- In board meetings, follow established norms (observe the chair's usage)
- Privately may be "Sarah"; in meetings may be "Mrs. Wilson"

### Investor Relations

- Formal address until invited otherwise
- Family office principals may prefer formality (generational wealth)
- International investors may have specific cultural expectations

### Social Events

- Err on the side of formality with anyone you don't know well
- If you've been on first-name basis in business, take cues at social events
- When meeting someone's spouse/family, revert to formal

### Correspondence

- Written communications should generally be more formal than spoken
- Thank you notes: err formal
- Email: context-dependent, but when in doubt, formal initially

## Worked Example

### Scenario

You're attending a charity gala where you'll meet several people:
1. A former US Senator, now 78
2. A British businessman who was knighted
3. The wife of your business partner, whom you've never met
4. A contemporary who runs a major foundation

### Correct Address

**Former Senator Williams (78):**
- "Senator Williams, what an honor to meet you."
- Continue "Senator Williams" unless he insists otherwise
- Even then, he may prefer the title socially

**Sir David Thompson (British knight):**
- "Sir David, how do you do." (Never "Sir Thompson")
- Continue "Sir David" throughout

**Partner's wife, Mrs. Julia Chen:**
- "Mrs. Chen, I've so looked forward to meeting you."
- Wait for her to offer first name

**Contemporary, Sarah Mitchell (Foundation CEO):**
- "Sarah—wonderful to see you" (if you already have that relationship)
- If new: "Ms. Mitchell, I've followed your foundation's work"
- She'll likely transition quickly to first names

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Premature First Names
**Why it happens:** American informality, wanting to be friendly.
**The fix:** Wait for invitation. Formality shows respect; rushing to first names can seem presumptuous.

### Mistake 2: "Sir" + Surname
**Why it happens:** Confusion about knight conventions.
**The fix:** "Sir David" not "Sir Thompson." First name always follows Sir/Dame.

### Mistake 3: Dropping Titles for Former Officials
**Why it happens:** Thinking "former" means title no longer applies.
**The fix:** Presidents, senators, governors, ambassadors, and judges retain their titles.

### Mistake 4: Inconsistent Usage
**Why it happens:** Forgetting, or context-switching errors.
**The fix:** When in doubt, formal. If you've transitioned to first name, stick with it consistently.

### Mistake 5: Over-Using Names
**Why it happens:** Sales training that emphasizes name usage.
**The fix:** Use names naturally, not repetitively. Once or twice in a conversation, not every sentence.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Default to formal** - Mr./Mrs./Ms./Title + Surname until invited otherwise.

2. **The superior invites informality** - Higher rank, older age, or host initiates first-name basis.

3. **Titles are retained** - Former presidents, judges, senators, ambassadors keep their titles.

4. **Sir/Dame + First Name** - Never Sir/Dame + Surname.

5. **Context matters** - You may be "James" in business but should be "Mr. Matthews" with their spouse.

6. **When uncertain, ask** - "How should I address the Ambassador?" is better than guessing wrong.
