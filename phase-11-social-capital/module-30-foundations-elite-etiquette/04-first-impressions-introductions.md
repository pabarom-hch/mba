# First Impressions and Introduction Protocol

## Learning Objectives

- Master the art of the proper introduction in formal settings
- Understand the hierarchy of introductions (who introduces whom to whom)
- Execute handshakes, greetings, and initial conversations appropriately
- Make strong first impressions without trying too hard
- Navigate self-introductions gracefully

---

## Introduction

First impressions in elite circles form quickly and persist stubbornly. Unlike casual social settings where second chances abound, formal introductions carry weight—they're remembered, discussed, and they shape whether doors open or close.

The good news: proper introduction protocol is learnable. The challenge: execution must appear effortless. This lesson teaches both the rules and the ease.

---

## Core Concepts

### The Mechanics of Introduction

**Who Introduces Whom**

Introductions follow a hierarchy—you "present" the person of lower rank to the person of higher rank:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    INTRODUCTION HIERARCHY                        │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                  │
│   Present TO the person of higher precedence:                   │
│                                                                  │
│   • Younger TO older                                            │
│   • Lower rank TO higher rank                                   │
│   • Non-official TO official                                    │
│   • Fellow citizen TO foreign dignitary                         │
│   • Male TO female (in purely social contexts)                  │
│   • Less familiar TO more familiar with the host                │
│                                                                  │
│   Example: "Mrs. Whitney, may I present John Matthews."         │
│   (John is presented to Mrs. Whitney)                           │
│                                                                  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**The Language of Introduction**

Formal: *"Mrs. Whitney, may I present John Matthews. John, this is Mrs. Whitney."*

Less formal: *"Mrs. Whitney, this is John Matthews. John—Sarah Whitney."*

Casual-formal: *"Sarah, do you know John Matthews? John—Sarah Whitney."*

**What to Include**

A good introduction provides context that facilitates conversation:
- *"Mary, this is James Thornton. James was just telling me about his work in conservation."*
- *"James, Mary Blackwell chairs the museum's board."*

Avoid: excessive biographical detail, achievements listing, or anything that feels like a sales pitch.

### Being Introduced

**The Response**

When introduced:

1. **Make eye contact** with the new person
2. **Smile genuinely** but not excessively
3. **Repeat their name**: "How do you do, Mrs. Whitney"
4. **Offer a handshake** when appropriate (see below)
5. **Add something brief** that opens conversation

**What to Say**

Good responses:
- "How nice to meet you."
- "I've heard wonderful things about your work with [organization]."
- "Sarah was just telling me about [conversation topic]—I'm fascinated."

Avoid:
- "I've heard so much about you!" (puts pressure on them)
- Launching immediately into your own background
- "Finally!" (suggests prior failed attempts)
- Over-familiar responses to someone you're meeting formally

### The Handshake

**The Proper Handshake**

The handshake remains the standard greeting in business and formal social settings:

- **Firmness**: Firm but not crushing, not limp
- **Duration**: 2-3 seconds
- **Grip**: Full hand, not just fingers
- **Movement**: Slight up-and-down, not pumping
- **Eye contact**: Maintained throughout
- **Smile**: Pleasant, genuine

**When to Initiate**

- Generally, the higher-ranking person or woman offers their hand first
- When in doubt, wait a beat—the other person will usually offer
- If they don't offer, don't force it—a warm nod is also acceptable

**The Double Hand**

Adding your left hand over the clasped hands ("the politician's handshake") is too familiar for first meetings. Save this for established relationships expressing warmth.

### Social vs. Business Introductions

**Social Introductions**

In purely social contexts, traditional gender deference applies:
- Men are introduced to women
- Younger people to older
- Focus on names and perhaps one connecting detail

**Business Introductions**

In business contexts, rank takes precedence over gender:
- Junior employees introduced to senior
- Outsiders introduced to insiders
- Clients generally receive deference as "higher rank"

### Self-Introduction

Often you'll need to introduce yourself. This requires particular skill.

**The Approach**

Don't hover. If you want to meet someone:
1. Wait for a natural pause in their conversation
2. Approach confidently (not aggressively)
3. Make brief eye contact before speaking
4. Introduce yourself with your name only
5. Add minimal context if needed

**Example Self-Introduction**

*"Hello, I'm James Matthews."* (Pause, let them respond)

If context is needed: *"Hello, I'm James Matthews—I'm a friend of Sarah's."*

If at an event: *"Hello, I'm James Matthews. I'm here with the conservation group."*

**What Not to Do**

- Don't lead with your title or company
- Don't explain why you're approaching them
- Don't mention that you've "wanted to meet them"
- Don't apologize for interrupting (you've waited for a pause)
- Don't launch into your background

### The Art of the Group Introduction

When introducing multiple people:

1. **Identify the highest-ranking person** - Others are introduced to them
2. **Go around methodically** - Don't leave anyone out
3. **Provide helpful context** - But keep it brief
4. **Help the conversation start** - Note a shared interest

**Example:**

*"Senator Wilson, let me introduce the group. This is James Matthews, who chairs our environmental committee, Mary Chen, who just joined us from the conservation fund, and David Park, our longtime counsel. Everyone—Senator Wilson, who's been a great supporter of our work."*

## Mentor Perspectives

### Emily Post on Introductions

*"No one should ever be left standing without introduction. The introduction may be brief, but it must be made."*

Post's emphasis: introductions are fundamentally about kindness—ensuring no one feels invisible or excluded.

### Letitia Baldrige on First Impressions

*"You have about ten seconds to make a first impression. In those ten seconds, everything matters—your posture, your smile, your handshake, your eye contact, your first words. Make them count."*

Baldrige reminds us that first impressions are holistic—not just what you say but how you present yourself entirely.

### On Remembering Names

*"A person's name is to them the sweetest sound in any language."* — Dale Carnegie

Using someone's name (correctly) after introduction signals attention and respect. Not using it—or worse, getting it wrong—signals the opposite.

## CEO Application

### Board Member Introductions

When introducing new board members or investors:
- Lead with their contribution to the company, not their bio
- Make the introduction warm but not effusive
- Provide context that helps conversation flow
- Never oversell or flatter

### Event Navigation

At industry events or galas:
- Have a strategy for who you want to meet
- Don't interrupt important conversations
- Use natural transitions (drinks, food, moving between rooms)
- Prepare one-line context about yourself that invites questions

### Being Introduced as CEO

When you're the one being introduced:
- Respond with genuine warmth, not executive distance
- Don't correct an introduction that understates you
- Show interest in the other person immediately
- Remember: the CEO title can intimidate—warm behavior offsets this

## Worked Example

### Scenario

You're at a private dinner where you know the host but few other guests. You want to meet Mrs. Caroline Ashford, a notable philanthropist.

### Wrong Approach

Walking directly up to Mrs. Ashford who is in conversation:
*"Mrs. Ashford? I'm James Matthews. I've been wanting to meet you—I've followed your foundation's work for years and would love to discuss some ideas with you."*

**Problems:**
- Interrupted her conversation
- Self-introduced without facilitation
- Immediately made it about what you want
- Put pressure on her
- No graceful exit for her

### Right Approach

Wait until Mrs. Ashford's conversation ends naturally or she moves to get a drink. Approach the host:

*"Margaret, what a lovely evening. I don't believe I've met Mrs. Ashford—would you be willing to introduce us?"*

Margaret introduces you: *"Caroline, this is James Matthews—he runs Thornton Industries. James, Caroline Ashford."*

You: *"How do you do, Mrs. Ashford. Margaret tells me you're involved with the children's hospital—it's such an important institution."*

**Why this works:**
- Used a facilitator (the host)
- Let someone else establish your credibility
- Focused on her interests, not yours
- Asked for nothing
- Left room for natural conversation

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: The Premature Business Card
**Why it happens:** Habit from casual business contexts.
**The fix:** In social-formal settings, never offer a card unless explicitly asked. Even then, be subtle.

### Mistake 2: The Lingering Handshake
**Why it happens:** Trying to make a strong impression.
**The fix:** 2-3 seconds, then release. Holding too long creates discomfort.

### Mistake 3: The Background Dump
**Why it happens:** Wanting to establish credibility.
**The fix:** Less is more. Your name, perhaps one context point. Let conversation reveal more.

### Mistake 4: Interrupting for Introduction
**Why it happens:** Eagerness, not reading social cues.
**The fix:** Wait for natural pauses. It's better to miss an opportunity than to interrupt.

### Mistake 5: Forgetting Names
**Why it happens:** Normal human fallibility.
**The fix:** Repeat the name immediately when introduced. Use it once in conversation. If you forget, admit it gracefully: "I'm so sorry—remind me of your name?"

## Key Takeaways

1. **Introductions follow hierarchy** - Present lower rank to higher rank, younger to older, outsider to insider.

2. **Less is more** - A good introduction is brief, contextual, and conversation-enabling.

3. **Self-introductions require care** - Minimal information, no agenda, focus on them.

4. **The handshake matters** - Firm, brief, with eye contact. Wait for social cues on who initiates.

5. **Use facilitators when possible** - Asking a host to introduce you is better than approaching cold.

6. **First impressions are holistic** - Posture, eye contact, dress, energy all matter as much as words.

## Practice Exercise

### The Introduction Drill

Before your next formal social event:

**Prepare:**
1. A one-sentence self-introduction (name + minimal context)
2. Two questions you can ask anyone
3. Identification of someone you'd like to meet
4. Understanding of who might introduce you

**Execute:**
1. Greet the host and ask about other guests
2. Request an introduction if appropriate
3. Practice your handshake (firm, 2-3 seconds)
4. Use the new person's name in conversation
5. Gracefully exit after 5-10 minutes

**Debrief:**
1. How did your self-introduction land?
2. Did you remember names?
3. What felt natural? What felt forced?
4. What will you adjust next time?
