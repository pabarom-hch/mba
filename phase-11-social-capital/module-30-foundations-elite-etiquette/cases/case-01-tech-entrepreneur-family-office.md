# Case Study: The Tech Entrepreneur at the Family Office

## Case Overview

- **Setting:** Initial meeting at a prominent family office in Manhattan
- **Key Players:** Alex Chen (tech entrepreneur), the Worthington Family Office team
- **The Challenge:** Making a strong first impression while navigating significant cultural differences
- **Stakes:** Potential $50M investment for Series C funding

---

## Background

**Alex Chen** is a 34-year-old founder of DataFlow, a rapidly growing enterprise software company. Alex grew up middle-class in California, attended Stanford on scholarship, and has built a company valued at $300M. DataFlow has raised $80M from traditional VCs and is now seeking its Series C.

Alex's current investor, a partner at a top VC firm, has made an introduction to the **Worthington Family Office**, which manages a portion of the Worthington family's multi-generational wealth (original fortune from textiles in the 1800s). The family office has recently begun making selective direct investments in technology companies.

**The Worthington Family Office:**
- Manages $3B+ in family assets across four generations
- Based in a discreet building on Park Avenue (no signage)
- Led by Harrison Worthington IV (65), third cousin to the family principals
- Investment decisions involve Harrison plus approval from the Family Investment Committee
- Historically invested in real estate, public equities, and bonds
- Tech investing is new; they've made only three direct investments

---

## The Situation

Alex arrives at the Worthington Family Office for a 10 AM meeting on a Tuesday in February.

### What Alex Observes

**The Building:**
The address is a pre-war building with a small, elegant lobby. No directory listing for the family office—Alex had to be told which floor. The elevator opens directly into a reception area decorated with oil portraits and antique furniture. A receptionist (older woman, impeccably dressed) greets Alex by name.

**The Wait:**
Alex is offered coffee or tea and waits for approximately 10 minutes in a sitting area with leather chairs, copies of The Economist and Town & Country, and more oil paintings. No company logos, no technology, no flat screens.

**The Meeting Room:**
Harrison Worthington enters, introduces himself, and leads Alex to a wood-paneled conference room. Also present: Elizabeth Warren (no relation to the senator), the family office's investment director. Both are dressed conservatively—Harrison in a navy suit, Elizabeth in a dress and cardigan.

### The Conversation

Harrison begins with pleasantries about the weather, asks how Alex found the building, and inquires about Alex's investor (whom he knows socially). Fifteen minutes pass before any mention of DataFlow.

When business does come up, Harrison's questions are different from VC pitches Alex is used to:
- "Tell us about yourself—where did you grow up?"
- "What drew you to this particular problem?"
- "How do you think about building something that lasts?"
- "What would you be doing if you weren't running DataFlow?"

Elizabeth asks more technical questions about the business, but the tone remains conversational rather than interrogatory.

After 75 minutes, Harrison suggests they "continue the conversation over lunch" at his club next month. He provides no indication of next steps, timeline, or interest level.

## Discussion Questions

### Question 1: First Impressions

What should Alex have done to prepare for this meeting? What signals should Alex be reading about the environment and the people?

**Consider:**
- Dress and appearance choices
- Research approach (what to know, what not to mention)
- Mental preparation for pace and style differences
- Expectations about the meeting structure

### Question 2: Cultural Navigation

How should Alex handle the 15 minutes of "small talk" before business? What are the risks of trying to move the conversation to business more quickly?

**Consider:**
- The purpose of "small talk" in this context
- What Harrison is actually assessing
- How Alex can add value in non-business conversation
- The signal sent by impatience

### Question 3: Self-Presentation

How should Alex talk about personal background and success without either underselling or appearing to brag?

**Consider:**
- Old money attitudes toward self-made success
- The Stanford credential (mention it? how?)
- The middle-class background (asset or liability?)
- The appropriate level of achievement discussion

### Question 4: The "Club Lunch" Invitation

What does the club lunch invitation signal? How should Alex prepare for it?

**Consider:**
- The difference between office meetings and club meetings
- What's being evaluated at a social lunch
- Dress code considerations
- Behavior in a private club environment
- What not to do

## Analysis

### What Alex Did Well

**In this scenario, let's assume Alex:**
- Arrived dressed appropriately (suit, conservative, no flashy watch or accessories)
- Made genuine small talk without rushing to business
- Shared personal background when asked without excessive detail
- Asked thoughtful questions about the family office's approach
- Expressed genuine interest in building something lasting
- Did not mention the company's valuation or press coverage
- Did not push for a timeline or commitment
- Accepted the lunch invitation with simple thanks

### What Could Go Wrong

**Common mistakes in this scenario:**

**Overdressing tech-style:**
Showing up in a designer hoodie or with obvious status accessories (new Rolex, designer sneakers) would signal either ignorance or deliberate rejection of their norms.

**Rushing the pleasantries:**
Trying to "get down to business" would signal impatience and transactional thinking—exactly what family offices try to avoid.

**The elevator pitch:**
Launching into a polished VC pitch would miss the conversational tone entirely. They want a dialogue, not a presentation.

**Asking about timeline:**
"So what's your process? When might you make a decision?" signals impatience and prioritizes your timeline over theirs.

**Mentioning valuation or press:**
"We're valued at $300M" or "TechCrunch just wrote about us" would seem like bragging and new-money status-seeking.

**Over-preparing visibly:**
Mentioning obscure details about the Worthington family history or investments would seem like stalking rather than preparation.

### The Club Lunch: What It Means

The club lunch invitation is significant:
- It moves the relationship from purely professional to social-professional
- It's a further assessment of Alex as a person, not just a founder
- Harrison is likely testing whether Alex can navigate his social environment
- It may involve introduction to other members (more evaluation)

**Preparation for the club lunch:**
- Research the club's dress code (likely business casual, possibly jacket required)
- Arrive on time but not early
- Let Harrison lead to the table, order first, set the pace
- Expect minimal business talk
- Do not photograph, post, or check in
- Send a handwritten thank-you note within 48 hours

---

## Key Lessons

### Lesson 1: Different Evaluation Criteria

Family offices evaluate differently than VCs:
- VCs: metrics, traction, market size, team
- Family offices: also character, patience, alignment, relationship potential

**Implication:** Be prepared for personal questions and extended relationship-building.

### Lesson 2: Pace Matters

The slower pace isn't inefficiency—it's how trust is built. Rushing signals:
- Impatience (you're not a long-term thinker)
- Transaction focus (you want their money, not a relationship)
- Insecurity (you need this deal too much)

**Implication:** Match their pace. Let the relationship develop.

### Lesson 3: Authenticity Over Performance

Harrison has seen every pitch, every smooth operator, every performative humility. What's rare is genuine:
- Genuine curiosity about their perspective
- Genuine interest in building something that lasts
- Genuine comfort with who you are
- Genuine respect for their approach

**Implication:** Be yourself—a thoughtful, accomplished version of yourself.

### Lesson 4: The Long Game

Even if this meeting doesn't lead to investment, Harrison knows many people. His assessment of Alex will inform whether he makes introductions, mentions Alex favorably, or warns others away.

**Implication:** Every interaction is reputation-building, not just deal-making.

---

## Epilogue

Six months later, the Worthington Family Office invested $20M in DataFlow's Series C. But the investment wasn't approved until after:
- Two more lunches (one at Harrison's home)
- A call with Elizabeth and two family principals
- Alex attending a charity event as Harrison's guest
- Extensive legal due diligence

The total process took seven months from first meeting to signed term sheet. A typical VC process takes six to eight weeks.

Harrison later told Alex's original investor: "What I liked was that he never rushed us. He seemed to understand that we do things our own way."
