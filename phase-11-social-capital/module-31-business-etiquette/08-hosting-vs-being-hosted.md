# Hosting vs. Being Hosted

## Learning Objectives

- Excel as a host in business and social-business settings
- Be the ideal guest when hosted by others
- Understand the reciprocal obligations of hospitality
- Navigate transitions between host and guest roles
- Apply hospitality principles to build relationships

---

## Introduction

Hospitality is relationship currency. How you host reveals your character and generosity; how you behave as a guest reveals your grace and awareness. Mastering both roles—and knowing when you're in each—is essential for elite social navigation.

---

## Core Concepts

### The Art of Hosting

**The Host's Responsibilities:**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    HOST RESPONSIBILITIES                         │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                  │
│   BEFORE                                                        │
│   ├── Clear invitation with details                             │
│   ├── Dietary/preference inquiries                              │
│   ├── Venue appropriate to guests and occasion                  │
│   └── Contingency planning                                      │
│                                                                  │
│   DURING                                                        │
│   ├── Greet each guest personally                               │
│   ├── Make introductions                                        │
│   ├── Ensure guests' comfort                                    │
│   ├── Manage pace and flow                                      │
│   └── Handle any problems discreetly                            │
│                                                                  │
│   AFTER                                                         │
│   ├── Gracious farewell to each                                 │
│   ├── Follow up as appropriate                                  │
│   └── Handle any administrative matters (tips, etc.)            │
│                                                                  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Restaurant Hosting:**

When hosting a business dinner at a restaurant:

1. **Reservation:** Book well ahead, confirm details, request good table
2. **Arrival:** Arrive first, give card to maître d' for the check
3. **Greeting:** Stand to greet each guest as they arrive
4. **Seating:** Direct guests to seats (honor position to your right)
5. **Ordering:** Guide wine selection, encourage guests to order freely
6. **Pace:** Monitor service, intervene quietly if issues
7. **Check:** Handle discreetly (excuse yourself or have it brought to you)
8. **Departure:** See guests off, ensure coats/transport arranged

**Home Hosting:**

When hosting at your home:

- Clean, prepared environment
- Welcome guests at door personally
- Take coats, offer drinks immediately
- Make introductions
- Ensure no guest is stranded
- Attend to dietary needs
- Create environment for good conversation
- Gracious farewell, follow-up thanks for their presence

**Event Hosting:**

When hosting larger events:
- Professional planning as needed
- Personal greeting (stand at entrance early)
- Ensure name tags if appropriate
- Circulate throughout
- Introduce people who should meet
- Address the group if appropriate
- Thank attendees

### Being the Ideal Guest

**The Guest's Responsibilities:**

**Before:**
- Respond promptly to invitations
- Communicate dietary restrictions/preferences
- Confirm attendance closer to date
- Arrive appropriately (not too early, not late)

**During:**
- Bring hostess gift (if at a home)
- Greet host first upon arrival
- Circulate and engage
- Be an easy guest (low maintenance)
- Eat what's served (within reason)
- Express genuine appreciation
- Depart at appropriate time

**After:**
- Thank host before leaving
- Send thank-you note within 48 hours
- Follow up on any connections made

**The Easy Guest:**

Hosts love guests who:
- Arrive on time
- Don't require special handling
- Engage with other guests
- Help conversation flow
- Show appreciation
- Leave at the right time
- Follow up with thanks

Hosts dread guests who:
- Arrive late (or too early)
- Have complicated needs
- Monopolize the host
- Create social awkwardness
- Don't engage with others
- Overstay
- Don't acknowledge the effort

### Reciprocity in Hospitality

**The Unwritten Ledger:**

While hospitality shouldn't be transactional, there's an implicit reciprocity:
- If hosted repeatedly, you should reciprocate
- If you can't host at same level, find other ways to reciprocate
- Never become purely a taker

**Ways to Reciprocate:**

If you can't match the venue/scale:
- "Let me take you to lunch"
- Host at a restaurant
- Send tickets to an event
- Contribute meaningfully to a cause they care about
- Be helpful in other ways (introductions, advice)

**The Exception:**

Some relationships are inherently asymmetric (mentor/mentee, significant age/wealth gaps). In these cases:
- Express profound gratitude
- Look for ways to add value (even small)
- Don't insist on reciprocating at inappropriate level
- Let the senior party set the pattern

### Transitions and Ambiguity

**Who's Hosting?**

Sometimes it's unclear:
- "Let's have dinner" - who's hosting?
- "Join me at my club" - they're hosting
- "Let's grab coffee" - typically whoever asked, but often split

**Clarifying:**
"I'd like to take you to dinner—my treat."
"Shall I make a reservation, or would you prefer to choose?"
"Let me host you at [restaurant]."

**The Check Dance:**

When it's genuinely ambiguous:
- If you invited, reach for check
- If they reach, one offer to split
- If they insist, accept graciously
- Don't engage in prolonged battle

## CEO Application

### Hosting Clients

As CEO, hosting clients/partners:
- Represents your company
- Sets tone for relationship
- Should be done well (venue, details)
- Personal attention matters even at scale

### Hosting Board Members

Board dinners and events:
- High touch required
- Personal acknowledgment of each
- Appropriate venues
- Attention to preferences and comfort

### Being Hosted

When clients or partners host you:
- Be the easy guest
- Express genuine appreciation
- Follow up appropriately
- Reciprocate in kind

### Company Events

Hosting company-wide events:
- Be present and visible
- Engage across levels
- Remember names
- Thank attendees

## Worked Example

### Scenario

You're hosting an important client and their spouse for dinner at your home, along with your spouse and another couple who are mutual friends.

### The Host Playbook

**Two Weeks Before:**
- Confirm date and time
- Inquire about dietary preferences
- Plan menu around preferences
- Ensure home will be ready

**Day Before:**
- Confirm with guests
- Prepare what can be prepared
- Set table, arrange flowers
- Brief spouse on guests (conversation topics, sensitivities)

**Day Of:**
- Final preparation
- Quick personal preparation
- House ready 30 minutes before

**Arrival:**
- You (or spouse) at door to greet
- Take coats
- Offer drinks immediately
- Make introductions all around
- Initial conversation in living area

**Dinner:**
- Host indicates move to table
- Direct seating (honor guest at your right)
- Serve or have served
- Facilitate conversation across groups
- Pace courses appropriately
- Handle any issues discreetly

**After Dinner:**
- Move to another area for coffee/dessert
- Don't rush, but read when guests are ready to leave
- See them to door
- Thank them for coming

**Next Day:**
- Brief thank you text is appropriate (even as host)
- "We so enjoyed having you last night"
- Guest will send thank you note

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Under-Preparing (as Host)
**Fix:** Lists, timelines, contingencies. Preparation enables grace.

### Mistake 2: Being a High-Maintenance Guest
**Fix:** Minimize requests, be adaptable, don't complain

### Mistake 3: Ambiguous Hosting
**Fix:** Clarify in advance who's hosting and where

### Mistake 4: Not Reciprocating
**Fix:** Track hospitality received; reciprocate appropriately

### Mistake 5: Overstaying
**Fix:** Read cues; leave at high point, not when host is exhausted

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Hosts control everything** - From invitation to farewell, the host shapes the experience

2. **Guests should be easy** - Low maintenance, grateful, engaging

3. **Preparation enables grace** - The well-prepared host appears effortless

4. **Reciprocity matters** - Track and respond to hospitality appropriately

5. **Thank-you notes close the loop** - Guests send notes within 48 hours

6. **Clarify who's hosting** - Ambiguity creates awkwardness
