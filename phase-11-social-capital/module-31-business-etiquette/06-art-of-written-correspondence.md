# The Art of Written Correspondence

## Learning Objectives

- Master the handwritten thank-you note
- Understand when to use handwritten, typed, or digital communication
- Learn proper formats for social and business correspondence
- Write appropriate condolence notes
- Develop a system for timely, thoughtful follow-up

---

## Introduction

In an age of instant digital communication, handwritten correspondence has become rare—and therefore powerful. A properly written note signals attention, effort, and respect. It stands out in a sea of emails and texts, creating disproportionate impact.

This lesson covers the essentials of written correspondence: when to write, how to write, and how to make your correspondence memorable.

---

## Core Concepts

### Why Handwritten Notes Matter

**The Effort Signal**

Handwriting a note takes time. In an era where time is precious, spending it on a handwritten note communicates:
- You matter enough for my time
- I'm thoughtful about our relationship
- I operate by old-school standards

**The Permanence Factor**

Unlike emails, handwritten notes are often kept. They sit on desks, go into files, get saved. Your note may be remembered long after hundreds of emails are deleted.

**The Differentiation**

How many handwritten notes does a typical executive receive per week? One? None? Your note stands out purely by existing.

### When to Write

**Always Write:**
- Thank-you after dinner parties or overnight stays
- Thank-you after interviews (both directions)
- Congratulations on major life events (promotion, marriage, birth)
- Condolences on loss
- Thank-you for gifts
- Thank-you after being someone's guest at a club or event

**Consider Writing:**
- Thank-you after significant business meetings
- Follow-up to a meaningful conversation
- Holiday greetings (though cards are acceptable)
- Congratulations on professional achievements

**When Digital Is Fine:**
- Routine business follow-up
- Scheduling and logistics
- Quick thank-yous for minor favors
- Communications requiring immediate response

### The Anatomy of a Thank-You Note

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    THE PERFECT THANK-YOU NOTE                    │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                  │
│   Dear [Name],                                                   │
│                                                                  │
│   [1. Express thanks specifically]                               │
│   Thank you for the lovely dinner Saturday evening.              │
│                                                                  │
│   [2. Add something personal/specific]                           │
│   Your observation about the Renaissance influence on            │
│   modern architecture has given me much to think about.          │
│                                                                  │
│   [3. Brief close]                                               │
│   I hope we can continue the conversation soon.                  │
│                                                                  │
│   [Closing],                                                     │
│   [Your name]                                                    │
│                                                                  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Length:** 3-5 sentences is ideal. Enough to be substantive; not so much that it becomes an essay.

**Timing:** Within 48 hours of the occasion. Sooner is better. Late is still better than never.

### Example Thank-You Notes

**After a Dinner Party:**

*Dear Margaret,*

*Thank you for a delightful evening. Your home was warm and welcoming, and the conversation sparkled. I particularly enjoyed meeting the Thompsons—what a fascinating background in marine biology.*

*With warm regards,*
*James*

**After a Business Meeting:**

*Dear Mr. Harrison,*

*Thank you for taking the time to meet with me yesterday. Your perspective on the industry's future was illuminating, and I appreciate your candor about the challenges ahead.*

*I look forward to continuing our conversation.*

*Sincerely,*
*Sarah Chen*

**After a Gift:**

*Dear Elizabeth,*

*Thank you for the beautiful book on Japanese gardens. You remembered our conversation from months ago—what a thoughtful gesture. I've already begun reading and am transported.*

*Warmly,*
*David*

### The Condolence Note

Condolence notes are perhaps the most important and most difficult form of correspondence.

**The Formula:**

1. Acknowledge the loss directly
2. Share something specific about the deceased (if you knew them)
3. Offer support without empty promises
4. Close simply

**Example:**

*Dear Caroline,*

*I was saddened to learn of William's passing. His kindness at the board retreats meant more to me than he knew—he made a newcomer feel welcome when he didn't have to.*

*Please know I'm thinking of you during this difficult time.*

*With deepest sympathy,*
*Robert*

**What to Avoid:**
- "I know how you feel" (you don't)
- Religious platitudes (unless you know their beliefs)
- "Call me if you need anything" (empty offer)
- Long paragraphs about yourself

**What Works:**
- Brief, genuine, specific
- A memory or quality of the deceased
- Simple expression of sympathy
- No demands on the grieving

### Stationery and Presentation

**Paper Quality:**

Good stationery signals seriousness. Invest in:
- Heavy stock (28-32 lb or higher)
- Classic size (correspondence cards or fold-over notes)
- Quality paper (cotton or linen content)
- Neutral colors (white, ecru, light gray)

**Personalization:**

- Engraved or printed name/monogram (most formal)
- Simple printed name and address
- Quality blank cards are also acceptable

**Handwriting:**

- Legible is more important than beautiful
- Use blue or black ink
- Practice if your handwriting has atrophied

### Addressing Envelopes

**Social Correspondence:**

```
Mr. and Mrs. James William Harrison
47 East 64th Street
New York, New York 10065
```

**Professional Correspondence:**

```
Ms. Sarah Chen
Managing Director
Thornton Partners
100 Park Avenue, 40th Floor
New York, New York 10017
```

**Titles and Honors:**
- Medical doctors: Dr. James Harrison
- PhDs: James Harrison, PhD (or Dr. on social correspondence)
- Judges: The Honorable Sarah Williams
- Clergy: Various by denomination

### Salutations and Closings

**Salutations (in order of formality):**

| Formality | Salutation |
|-----------|------------|
| Most formal | Dear Mr. Harrison, |
| Formal social | Dear James, |
| Less formal | Dear James— |
| Casual (risky) | James, |

**Closings (in order of formality):**

| Formality | Closing |
|-----------|---------|
| Most formal | Respectfully yours, |
| Formal | Sincerely, |
| Warm formal | Sincerely yours, |
| Friendly | Warmly, |
| Casual | Best, |

**When Uncertain:** "Sincerely" or "Warmly" are safe middle-ground options.

### Building a Correspondence Habit

**The System:**

1. Keep stationery and stamps accessible (desk drawer, travel kit)
2. Schedule 15 minutes weekly for correspondence
3. Maintain a list of notes you owe
4. Write immediately when the impulse strikes
5. Stock up on stamps (it's usually the stamps you're missing)

**The 48-Hour Rule:**

If someone does something that warrants a note, write it within 48 hours. The longer you wait, the less likely you'll do it.

## Mentor Perspectives

### Emily Post on Thank-You Notes

*"No letter is too short to be important if it's sincere. A brief note is far better than no note at all, and infinitely better than one that's delayed so long it becomes awkward."*

### Letitia Baldrige on Impact

*"I've seen careers advanced by consistent, thoughtful correspondence. The person who writes thank-you notes stands out because so few do. It's an easy competitive advantage."*

### Modern Application

*"Email has raised the bar for handwritten notes. They used to be expected; now they're exceptional. Use that to your advantage."* — Executive coach observation

## CEO Application

### After Board Meetings

A brief note to board members after key meetings:
- Not expected, therefore notable
- Reinforces relationship beyond the boardroom
- Can reference a specific point from discussion
- Keep professional, not effusive

### Investor Relations

Notes to key investors:
- After significant meetings
- During challenging times (shows confidence)
- Holiday cards (though less intimate than notes)
- Never used to ask for things

### Customer Relationships

With key customers:
- After major transactions
- When they've referred business
- On significant milestones
- Personal occasions you learn about

### Internal Notes

To key team members:
- Exceptional performance
- Personal milestones (marriage, birth, loss)
- Departures (good wishes)
- These humanize leadership

## Worked Example

### Scenario

You attended a private dinner at the home of potential investor Charles Worthington and his wife Eleanor. It was an intimate gathering—12 guests, four courses, excellent wine. You had a good conversation with Charles about your business, but also talked with Eleanor about her foundation work and with another guest about sailing.

### The Note

*Dear Charles and Eleanor,*

*Thank you for including me at your beautiful dinner Thursday evening. The warmth of your home and the thoughtfulness of the gathering made for a memorable night.*

*Eleanor, I was moved by your description of the foundation's work with young artists—what a meaningful way to invest in the future. Charles, I appreciated your insights on long-term business building. Your observation about "the patience premium" gave me much to consider.*

*With warm thanks,*

*James Matthews*

### Why This Works

- Thanks both hosts by name
- References something specific about each
- Indicates genuine attention to the evening
- Doesn't mention business opportunity (too transactional)
- Length is appropriate (not too long, not too brief)
- Will be remembered

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Waiting Too Long
**Fix:** The 48-hour rule. Better brief and on time than elaborate and late.

### Mistake 2: Too Generic
**Fix:** Always include something specific about the occasion, gift, or conversation.

### Mistake 3: Email When You Should Handwrite
**Fix:** For social invitations, significant gifts, and condolences—always handwrite.

### Mistake 4: Asking for Something
**Fix:** Never include a request in a thank-you note. It undermines the gratitude.

### Mistake 5: Excessive Length
**Fix:** 3-5 sentences is ideal. A note is not a letter.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Handwritten notes create disproportionate impact** - So few people write them that yours will stand out.

2. **Specific beats generic** - Reference something particular about the occasion.

3. **Timing matters** - Within 48 hours; sooner is better.

4. **Quality stationery signals seriousness** - Invest in good paper.

5. **Condolence notes are critical** - Brief, specific about the deceased, no demands on the grieving.

6. **Build a system** - Keep supplies accessible; schedule correspondence time.
