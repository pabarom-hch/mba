# Wine, Spirits, and Toasting Protocol

## Learning Objectives

- Understand wine service protocol and appropriate responses
- Navigate ordering wine in business settings
- Execute proper toasting etiquette
- Handle spirits and cocktails appropriately
- Know when and how to decline alcohol gracefully

---

## Introduction

Wine and spirits are woven into formal entertaining. You don't need to be an expert, but you do need to navigate these situations with confidence. This means understanding service protocols, managing your consumption appropriately, and executing toasts with grace.

This lesson provides the practical knowledge for any beverage situation in formal settings.

---

## Core Concepts

### Wine Service Protocol

**The Basics of Wine Service:**

1. **Presentation:** Server shows bottle to host (or orderer)
2. **Opening:** Cork removed tableside
3. **Tasting:** Small pour to host for approval
4. **Service:** Guests served, host served last
5. **Refills:** Server monitors and refills as needed

**When You're the Host (Ordering):**

If hosting a business dinner and expected to select wine:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    WINE ORDERING FRAMEWORK                       │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                  │
│   1. ASK PREFERENCES                                            │
│      "Does anyone have a preference for red or white?"          │
│                                                                  │
│   2. CONSIDER THE FOOD                                          │
│      White with fish/chicken, Red with beef/lamb (general)      │
│                                                                  │
│   3. CONSULT THE SOMMELIER                                      │
│      "We're having the fish and the beef—what would you         │
│      recommend in the $X range?"                                │
│      (Point to price on menu rather than saying number aloud)   │
│                                                                  │
│   4. ORDER ENOUGH                                               │
│      Roughly one bottle per 2-3 people for a full dinner        │
│                                                                  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**The Tasting Ritual:**

When you're offered the taste:
1. Look at the wine (checking for clarity)
2. Swirl gently, smell briefly
3. Take a small sip
4. Nod approval: "That's lovely, thank you"

**Important:** This ritual is to check the wine isn't flawed (corked, oxidized), not whether you like it. Unless there's an actual flaw, accept it.

**When You're a Guest:**

- Wait to be served
- Don't pour for yourself unless invited to
- Don't ask about the wine's cost or vintage
- Moderate consumption (see below)
- Follow the host's pace

### Wine Glass Handling

**Hold by the Stem:**
- Prevents warming the wine with your hand
- Keeps glass free of fingerprints
- Standard practice for all wine glasses

**Glass Positions:**
- Water glass: directly above knife
- White wine: right and slightly forward
- Red wine: right of white, slightly back

**When Finished:**
- Leave glass in place
- Server will know to stop refilling if you leave it untouched

### Managing Consumption

**The Business Dinner Balance:**

Alcohol relaxes conversation but impairs judgment. The right balance:

- **Match or be under** the table's consumption
- **Never be the most intoxicated** person present
- **Two drinks maximum** for most business occasions
- **Slow down** by drinking water between sips of wine
- **Eat adequately** to moderate alcohol's effects

**If You Don't Drink:**

Perfectly acceptable. Handle gracefully:
- "I'll have sparkling water, please"
- No explanation required
- Never apologize or make it awkward
- Don't draw attention to others' drinking

**If You Drink Very Little:**

- Accept a pour but nurse it
- It's fine to leave wine in your glass
- Don't announce you're not drinking much

### Toasting Protocol

**The Structure of a Toast:**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    ANATOMY OF A PROPER TOAST                     │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                  │
│   1. GET ATTENTION                                              │
│      Stand (optional), raise glass, wait for quiet              │
│                                                                  │
│   2. BRIEF REMARKS                                              │
│      30-60 seconds maximum                                      │
│      Personal but appropriate                                   │
│      End with clear toast phrase                                │
│                                                                  │
│   3. THE TOAST ITSELF                                           │
│      "To [person/occasion]"                                     │
│      Raise glass toward honoree                                 │
│      Make eye contact                                           │
│                                                                  │
│   4. THE DRINK                                                  │
│      All raise glasses, say "cheers" or echo toast              │
│      Sip (don't drain)                                          │
│                                                                  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Giving a Toast:**

Keep it:
- **Brief** - Under one minute
- **Personal** - Specific to the person/occasion
- **Positive** - No backhanded compliments or inside jokes
- **Sober** - In content and delivery

Example:
*"I'd like to propose a toast. James, in the three years we've worked together, I've watched you build something remarkable—not just a company, but a culture of integrity. Here's to your continued success. To James."*

**Receiving a Toast:**

If you're being toasted:
- Remain seated
- Do not drink (you don't toast yourself)
- Make eye contact with the toaster
- Nod in acknowledgment
- After others drink, you may rise and respond briefly: "Thank you—I'm grateful for this team"

**Clinking Glasses:**

- Make eye contact while clinking
- Clink bowl-to-bowl, gently
- Not necessary in large groups (raise toward honoree instead)
- In formal European settings, clinking may be avoided

### Spirits and Cocktails

**Reception Drinks:**

Common offerings at formal events:
- Champagne
- Wine (usually one white, one red)
- Classic cocktails (martini, manhattan, gin & tonic)
- Non-alcoholic options

**Ordering:**

If asked what you'd like:
- Name a specific drink: "A gin and tonic, please"
- Or accept offered champagne/wine
- Avoid complicated custom orders
- Never order shots in formal settings

**Whiskey/Brandy After Dinner:**

Digestifs (after-dinner spirits) are common:
- Port, brandy, scotch, cognac
- Served in appropriate glasses
- Sipped slowly, not drunk quickly
- Acceptable to decline: "I'm content with coffee, thank you"

**Cigars:**

In some circles, cigars accompany after-dinner drinks:
- Accept only if you smoke
- Follow the host's lead on where smoking occurs
- Never a requirement

## CEO Application

### Hosting Clients

When you're the host selecting wine:
- Ask the sommelier for help (shows confidence, not ignorance)
- Choose mid-upper range (not cheapest, not most expensive)
- Order enough for the table
- Be generous but not ostentatious

### International Considerations

- **France:** Wine is serious; follow local customs
- **Japan:** Sake may be served; pour for others, not yourself
- **Middle East:** Many don't drink; never pressure
- **UK:** Pub culture differs from formal dining

### The Toast as Leadership Moment

Toasts are mini-speeches—opportunities to:
- Recognize team members
- Set tone at company events
- Honor clients or partners
- Demonstrate grace and warmth

Prepare key toasts in advance for important occasions.

## Worked Example

### Scenario

You're hosting a client dinner at an upscale restaurant. Four guests, expecting to discuss a potential partnership.

### Wine Approach

**Before Dinner:**
"Would anyone like to start with a cocktail, or shall we go straight to wine?"
(Guests prefer wine)

**Ordering:**
"Let's see what they have. Does anyone have a strong preference?"
(Mixed preferences—some fish, some beef ordered)

To sommelier: "We have fish and beef at the table. What would you recommend that works with both, or should we do one of each?"

Sommelier suggests a white and a red. You agree.

**The Tasting:**
You swirl, sniff, taste. "Excellent, thank you."

**During Dinner:**
You ensure glasses are filled but don't push.
You pace your own drinking to stay sharp.

**Toast Moment:**
Before dessert, you raise your glass:
"Before we wrap up, I want to say—we've talked a lot about business tonight, but what's struck me is how aligned our values are. Here's to what I hope is the beginning of a great partnership. To new beginnings."

Glasses raised, sipped.

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Swirling Too Aggressively
**Fix:** Gentle swirl on the table or small rotation in the air

### Mistake 2: Over-Drinking
**Fix:** Two-drink maximum; alternate with water; eat adequately

### Mistake 3: Long Toasts
**Fix:** Under one minute. Write it out if needed, then summarize.

### Mistake 4: Drinking to a Toast in Your Honor
**Fix:** Don't drink when you're being toasted. Acknowledge and respond.

### Mistake 5: Wine Snobbery
**Fix:** Appreciation is charming; expertise display is not. Keep it simple.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Hold wine by the stem** - Prevents warming; shows awareness

2. **The tasting is for flaws, not preference** - Accept unless wine is actually defective

3. **Match or stay under table consumption** - Never be the most intoxicated

4. **Toasts should be brief** - Under one minute, specific, positive

5. **Don't drink to your own toast** - Acknowledge, don't participate

6. **Declining is acceptable** - No explanation needed; don't make it awkward
