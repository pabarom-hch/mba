# International Business Etiquette

## Learning Objectives

- Navigate key business etiquette variations across major cultures
- Avoid serious cultural missteps in international settings
- Adapt your approach to different cultural expectations
- Build relationships across cultural boundaries
- Apply a framework for approaching unfamiliar cultures

---

## Introduction

What signals respect in one culture may signal disrespect in another. The direct eye contact valued in America can be aggressive in Japan. The firm handshake can be too forward in parts of Asia. This lesson provides practical guidance for navigating the most common international business contexts.

## Core Concepts

### The Cultural Framework

**Understanding Cultural Dimensions:**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    KEY CULTURAL DIMENSIONS                       │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                  │
│   HIERARCHY: How much is formal rank respected?                 │
│   ├── High: Japan, Korea, China, Middle East                    │
│   └── Lower: US, Australia, Scandinavia                         │
│                                                                  │
│   DIRECTNESS: Is direct communication valued?                   │
│   ├── Direct: US, Germany, Netherlands                          │
│   └── Indirect: Japan, China, many Asian cultures               │
│                                                                  │
│   RELATIONSHIP: Does business require relationship first?       │
│   ├── Relationship: China, Middle East, Latin America           │
│   └── Task: US, Germany, Scandinavia                            │
│                                                                  │
│   TIME: How is time perceived and managed?                      │
│   ├── Punctual: Germany, Switzerland, Japan                     │
│   └── Flexible: Middle East, Latin America, Africa              │
│                                                                  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Japan

**Key Principles:**
- Respect for hierarchy is paramount
- Indirect communication; read between the lines
- Group harmony over individual expression
- Patience required; decisions take time

**Business Cards (Meishi):**
- Present and receive with both hands
- Bow slightly while exchanging
- Study received card carefully
- Never write on a card in front of giver
- Place on table during meeting; store carefully after

**Greetings:**
- Bow is traditional; handshake acceptable for Westerners
- Exchange business cards immediately after greeting
- Use surname + "san" (Mr./Ms.)

**Meetings:**
- Punctuality is essential
- Hierarchy determines seating and speaking order
- Silence is not awkward; it's contemplation
- "Yes" may mean "I hear you," not agreement
- Decisions often made outside the room

**Dining:**
- Wait to be seated; there's a hierarchy
- Don't pour your own drink
- "Kampai" for toasts
- Finish everything on your plate
- Chopstick etiquette matters greatly

**Gift-Giving:**
- Important and ritualized
- Wrap beautifully (presentation matters)
- Give/receive with both hands
- Gifts opened privately typically
- Avoid sets of 4 (unlucky)

### China

**Key Principles:**
- "Guanxi" (relationships) is everything
- Face (dignity, respect) must be preserved
- Hierarchy is respected but less rigid than Japan
- Patience required; build relationship first

**Business Cards:**
- Both hands for presentation and receipt
- Chinese side facing recipient (if bilingual)
- Study received card
- Don't write on cards

**Greetings:**
- Handshake common; lighter than American
- Slight nod is respectful
- Titles matter; use them

**Meetings:**
- Punctuality expected
- Senior person typically leads
- Build relationship before diving into business
- Saying "no" directly is rare; read indirect cues

**Dining (Banquets):**
- Guest of honor seated facing door
- Host orders; dishes shared
- Toast with "Ganbei" (bottoms up with baijiu)
- Leaving food shows abundance
- Business discussed later in meal or after

**Gift-Giving:**
- Thoughtful gifts appreciated
- Wrap in red (lucky)
- Avoid white/black wrapping (funerary)
- Don't give clocks (associated with death)
- Gifts often declined initially (offer again)

### Middle East (Gulf States)

**Key Principles:**
- Relationships before business
- Hospitality is sacred
- Patience and respect essential
- Religious observance impacts scheduling

**Greetings:**
- Men shake hands; may be prolonged
- Same-gender greetings only (typically)
- Don't shake hands with opposite gender unless initiated
- "As-salamu alaykum" (peace be upon you) appreciated

**Meetings:**
- Punctuality: you're on time; they may not be
- Small talk required before business
- Interruptions are normal (hospitality to others)
- Decisions can take time; patience

**Dining:**
- Lavish hospitality is common
- Accept offered coffee/tea
- Use right hand for eating/giving
- Don't discuss business at dinner
- No alcohol in many contexts

**Other Considerations:**
- Friday is holy day; Saturday-Thursday workweek
- Ramadan affects business hours and energy
- Dress conservatively (especially women)
- Prayer times are honored

### Germany

**Key Principles:**
- Efficiency and directness valued
- Punctuality is respect
- Formality in titles and address
- Thoroughness over speed

**Greetings:**
- Firm handshake
- Use "Herr/Frau" + surname until invited otherwise
- Academic titles used (Herr Doktor)
- First names take time

**Meetings:**
- Be on time (early is better)
- Agenda followed precisely
- Direct communication expected
- Decisions may be thorough but binding

**Dining:**
- "Guten Appetit" before eating
- Keep hands on table (wrists, not elbows)
- Finish everything on plate
- Wait for "Prost" before drinking

### United Kingdom

**Key Principles:**
- Understated over obvious
- Politeness and restraint
- Humor (especially self-deprecating) valued
- Class awareness still exists

**Greetings:**
- Moderate handshake
- "How do you do" is rhetorical
- Use titles appropriately
- Understatement is expected

**Meetings:**
- Punctual but not rigid
- Indirect disagreement common
- Self-deprecation shows confidence
- Don't brag; let achievements speak

**Dining:**
- Continental style (fork in left, knife in right throughout)
- "Cheers" for toast
- Don't discuss money

### Latin America

**Key Principles:**
- Relationships are essential
- Warmth and personal connection valued
- Punctuality more flexible
- Family and personal life matter

**Greetings:**
- Warm handshake; may include embrace among friends
- Cheek kisses common in social settings
- Titles respected
- Use both surnames initially

**Meetings:**
- Be on time (though they may not be)
- Small talk important before business
- Relationships build slowly
- Don't rush to business

**Dining:**
- Dinners are late (9 PM+)
- Meals are long; enjoy
- Family topics welcome
- Don't rush

### Universal Principles

**When in Doubt:**

1. **Observe first** - Watch what locals do
2. **Ask** - "What should I know?" is respectful
3. **Err conservative** - More formal is usually safer
4. **Apologize genuinely** - If you misstep, acknowledge
5. **Show curiosity** - Interest in their culture is appreciated

## Worked Example

### Scenario

You're visiting Tokyo for your first meeting with a potential Japanese partner company.

### Preparation

**Before Trip:**
- Order bilingual business cards (English/Japanese)
- Research company and executives
- Learn basic phrases (greetings, thank you)
- Review gift customs; purchase appropriate gift
- Plan conservative business attire

**At Meeting:**

**Arrival:**
- Arrive 10 minutes early
- Announce yourself to reception
- Wait to be escorted to meeting room

**Greeting:**
- Bow (slight nod is fine for Westerners)
- Exchange business cards immediately
- Both hands, Japanese side facing them
- Study their cards carefully
- Place on table in front of you

**During Meeting:**
- Follow their lead on seating
- Expect small talk before business
- Don't rush; patience is essential
- Silence is normal—don't fill it
- Take detailed notes
- Don't expect immediate decisions

**Ending:**
- Exchange thanks
- Don't push for commitment
- They'll walk you out
- Bow at departure

**After:**
- Thank you note (or email)
- Follow up as appropriate
- Be patient for response

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Assuming American Norms Are Universal
**Fix:** Research each culture specifically

### Mistake 2: Rushing Relationship-Building
**Fix:** In many cultures, business follows relationship. Be patient.

### Mistake 3: Being Too Direct
**Fix:** Adapt communication style to context; indirect cultures require reading between lines.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Hierarchy
**Fix:** In hierarchical cultures, address and defer to senior people appropriately.

### Mistake 5: Cultural Missteps in Dining
**Fix:** Research dining customs before trips; observe and follow host's lead.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **No universal rules** - What's polite in one culture may offend in another

2. **Do your homework** - Research before every international engagement

3. **Observe and adapt** - Watch how locals behave; follow their lead

4. **Relationships matter** - Many cultures require relationship before business

5. **Patience is essential** - Timelines vary; don't rush

6. **Err formal** - When uncertain, more formal is usually safer
