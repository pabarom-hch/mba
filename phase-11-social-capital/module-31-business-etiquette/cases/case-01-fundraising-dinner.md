# Case Study: The Fundraising Dinner

## Case Overview

- **Setting:** Annual gala dinner for a major cultural institution
- **Key Players:** Michael Chen (tech entrepreneur), various UHNW donors and board members
- **The Challenge:** Navigate a high-stakes social-business event while making meaningful connections
- **Stakes:** Board membership consideration and philanthropic relationships

---

## Background

**Michael Chen** is a 45-year-old founder who sold his fintech company for $800 million three years ago. He's now focused on philanthropy, particularly arts and education. He's been cultivating a relationship with the Museum of Contemporary Art and has been invited to their annual gala as a guest of Board Chair Elizabeth Harrington.

**The Event:**
- 400 guests at the museum
- Cocktail reception (90 minutes)
- Seated dinner (4 courses)
- Program with speeches and paddle raise
- After-party in the galleries

**Michael's Goals:**
- Deepen relationship with Elizabeth Harrington
- Meet other board members and major donors
- Learn about the institution's needs
- Position himself for eventual board membership

---

## The Situation

### Part 1: Arrival and Cocktails

Michael arrives at 6:45 PM for a 7:00 cocktail reception. He's wearing a well-fitted tuxedo (black tie was specified). His wife Sarah is in a cocktail-length dress with modest jewelry.

**The Greeting Line:**
At the entrance, there's a receiving line with Elizabeth Harrington and Museum Director James Foster. Michael and Sarah wait their turn.

**At the Bar:**
After greeting the hosts, Michael approaches the bar. A server offers champagne; he accepts. He and Sarah survey the room.

**A Challenge:**
A man approaches Michael enthusiastically: "Michael Chen! I've been wanting to meet you. I have an incredible investment opportunity..." He launches into a pitch.

### Part 2: Dinner

**Finding the Table:**
Dinner is announced. Michael finds his place card at Table 3, a prominent table near the front. He's seated between a museum board member (Margaret Whitmore, 72) and a younger donor (James Park, hedge fund). Sarah is across the table.

**The Courses:**

*First Course (Soup):*
Margaret turns to Michael and asks about his background. James Park is on his phone under the table.

*Second Course (Fish):*
A server approaches to refill wine. Michael's glass is still half full.

*Main Course:*
James Park, having finished his phone activity, leans over: "So, you sold to PayPal, right? What was the multiple?" Margaret is listening.

*Dessert:*
Elizabeth Harrington, seated at another table, catches Michael's eye and nods warmly.

### Part 3: The Program

**The Paddle Raise:**
After dinner, the program begins. After speeches, the auctioneer announces a paddle raise for the museum's education programs.

"Who will start us at $100,000?"

Several paddles go up. The auctioneer works down: "$50,000?" "$25,000?" "$10,000?"

Michael hasn't discussed this with Sarah beforehand. The moment is pressing.

### Part 4: After-Party

**In the Galleries:**
The formal portion concludes. Guests migrate to galleries for drinks and viewing. Elizabeth Harrington approaches Michael.

"Michael, thank you for coming. I'd love for you to meet our Director of Development. We should talk about getting you more involved."

---

## Discussion Questions

### Question 1: The Pitch at Cocktails

How should Michael handle the aggressive investor/pitcher who approached him during cocktails?

**Consider:**
- Not causing a scene
- Protecting his own time and goals
- Potentially maintaining the relationship (the person might be well-connected)
- Signaling to others nearby

### Question 2: Dinner Conversation

How should Michael handle Margaret's questions about his background? The goal is to be genuine without appearing to brag.

**Consider:**
- The sociology of wealth dynamics
- Margaret's likely perspective (old money vs. new)
- Appropriate understatement
- Turning the conversation to her

### Question 3: The Price Question

James Park asks directly about the sale multiple. This is a clear etiquette violation. How should Michael respond?

**Consider:**
- Not embarrassing James
- Not answering the inappropriate question
- Potentially redirecting James
- Being overheard by Margaret

### Question 4: The Paddle Raise

Michael and Sarah haven't discussed a budget. The moment is pressing. What should Michael do?

**Consider:**
- Not being unprepared for this moment
- What amount is appropriate given his wealth and goals
- The visibility of the moment
- His wife's involvement in the decision

### Question 5: Elizabeth's Approach

Elizabeth wants to get Michael "more involved." How should he respond?

**Consider:**
- Expressing interest without appearing eager
- Not committing to anything in the moment
- Following up appropriately
- The long game of board membership

---

## Analysis

### The Cocktail Pitch

**Recommended Response:**

"That's kind of you to think of me. I'm fully committed to my current projects, so I'm not looking at new opportunities. But let me let you get back to the party—I'm sure there are many people here you'd like to meet. Enjoy the evening."

Then excuse yourself: "Sarah, I believe I see the Harringtons—shall we say hello?"

**Why This Works:**
- Polite but definitive closure
- No door left open
- No embarrassment
- Graceful exit

### Dinner Conversation with Margaret

**Recommended Approach:**

When Margaret asks about background, keep it modest:

"I was fortunate to work on some interesting technology problems, and we found a good outcome. But I'm really more interested in what comes next—particularly supporting institutions like this one. Tell me, how long have you been involved with the museum?"

**Why This Works:**
- Answers without dwelling
- "Fortunate" and "we" show humility
- Pivots to future and her expertise
- Shows genuine interest in her

### The Price Question

**Recommended Response:**

"Oh, James, I never talk numbers—it's bad luck." [smile] "But tell me about your work—I understand you're doing interesting things in credit markets."

**Why This Works:**
- Deflects without embarrassing
- Light humor softens
- Redirects to him
- Margaret overhearing sees Michael handled it gracefully

### The Paddle Raise

**Lesson:** Never attend a fundraising event without discussing budget beforehand.

**In the Moment:**
Make quick eye contact with Sarah. If no signal, choose a modest amount you're comfortable with regardless ($5,000-10,000). Raise paddle confidently at that level.

**Better Preparation:**
Before any gala, discuss with spouse:
- What's our giving budget for this cause?
- What level feels right for this event?
- What's our paddle raise maximum?

### Elizabeth's Approach

**Recommended Response:**

"Elizabeth, thank you—what a wonderful evening. I'd welcome the chance to learn more about the museum's priorities. I'm sure your Director of Development can fill me in. Should I reach out to your office to arrange a conversation?"

**Why This Works:**
- Expresses interest without desperation
- Puts the ball in their court appropriately
- Doesn't commit to anything specific
- Shows he's a thoughtful potential partner

**Follow-Up:**
- Thank-you note to Elizabeth within 48 hours
- Mention specific conversation points
- Express interest in continued involvement
- Wait for them to propose next steps

---

## Key Lessons

### Lesson 1: Preparation Matters

The paddle raise moment catches many people unprepared. Before any fundraising event:
- Know the cause and giving levels
- Discuss budget with spouse/partner
- Have a number in mind

### Lesson 2: Graceful Deflection

Multiple moments required deflection:
- The pitcher at cocktails
- James's price question
- Any premature commitment

Develop stock phrases that redirect gracefully.

### Lesson 3: Understatement in Achievement Discussion

When asked about success:
- Answer briefly
- Use modest language ("fortunate," "we")
- Pivot to the future or to them
- Never volunteer numbers

### Lesson 4: The Long Game

Michael's board membership goal will take 1-3 years. This evening is one step. The approach should be:
- Build relationship genuinely
- Demonstrate commitment through giving
- Be a valuable presence (not a self-promoter)
- Let the invitation come to him

---

## Epilogue

Michael sent a handwritten note to Elizabeth the next day, mentioning their conversation and his interest in learning more about the museum's education programs. He followed up with the Director of Development and made a $50,000 gift to the education fund.

Over the following year, he attended two more museum events and hosted a small dinner for Elizabeth and three board members. Eighteen months later, he was invited to join the board.

James Park later apologized for his "blunt question" at dinner. Michael simply said, "No worries—it was a great evening. Let's grab coffee sometime."
