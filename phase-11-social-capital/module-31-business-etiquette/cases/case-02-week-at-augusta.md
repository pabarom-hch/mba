# Case Study: A Week at Augusta

## Case Overview

- **Setting:** Augusta National Golf Club during Masters week
- **Key Players:** David Kim (private equity partner), Robert Ashworth (potential LP, Augusta member)
- **The Challenge:** Navigate an ultra-exclusive environment while building a critical business relationship
- **Stakes:** $100M LP commitment for new fund

---

## Background

**David Kim** is a 48-year-old partner at a mid-sized private equity firm. His firm is raising a new $2 billion fund. He's been cultivating a relationship with **Robert Ashworth**, a third-generation industrialist whose family office has historically invested in private equity.

Robert is a member of Augusta National and has invited David to be his guest for three days during Masters week—not the tournament days, but practice rounds early in the week, which include playing the course.

**The Stakes:**
- Robert's family office typically commits $75-100M to funds they back
- Robert has invested with several competing firms
- This invitation represents significant relationship advancement
- David has never been to Augusta or any ultra-exclusive club

---

## The Situation

### Day 1: Arrival

**Getting There:**
David flies into Augusta on Monday morning. Robert has arranged for a car to pick him up. The driver, employed by Robert, says little but is professional.

**Arrival at Augusta:**
The car approaches Magnolia Lane. The entrance is understated—just a guard house. David's name is checked; the gate opens. The lane is as iconic as its reputation—azaleas, magnolias, the clubhouse emerging at the end.

**The Clubhouse:**
Robert meets David in the lobby. The clubhouse is impressive but not ostentatious—comfortable rather than grand. Robert is in khakis and a golf shirt. David is wearing dress slacks and a blazer, uncertain of dress code.

"David, welcome to Augusta. Let me show you to your cabin, and we'll get you settled."

**The Cabin:**
David is staying in one of the club's cabins—small houses on property for members and their guests. It's comfortable but not luxurious. No television. A single phone.

### Day 2: Playing the Course

**Morning:**
David and Robert are scheduled for an early round. Robert loans David a set of clubs (David had offered to bring his own but Robert said "We have everything you need").

In the locker room, David notices:
- Quiet, reverent atmosphere
- Members acknowledge each other subtly
- No business talk
- His locker has his name on it temporarily

**The Round:**
The course is immaculate. David is a competent golfer (12 handicap) but overwhelmed by the setting. He makes some poor shots early.

Robert is patient. At the 3rd hole, he says: "Don't worry about score. Just enjoy being here."

They're joined by Robert's friend Charles, also a member. Charles is a recently retired Fortune 500 CEO.

**Conversations:**
Throughout the round, conversation is light:
- Golf shots and course appreciation
- The azaleas and the history
- Light personal topics (families, travel)
- No business whatsoever

**Amen Corner:**
At the famous 12th hole, David pulls out his phone to take a photo.

**The Dinner:**
Evening dinner in the main dining room. Robert has invited three other guests, including Charles from the round. All are in jackets (no ties). Dinner is excellent but not showy. Conversation ranges widely—art, politics (carefully), travel, families.

At one point, another guest asks David: "What brings you to Augusta?" Robert interjects: "David and I have been getting to know each other. I thought he'd appreciate seeing the place."

### Day 3: Practice Round Viewing

**The Experience:**
It's Tuesday, a practice round day. The crowds are smaller than tournament days but still significant. Robert has obtained credentials for prime viewing.

**A Moment:**
While watching at the 16th, David spots a famous athlete also watching. His instinct is to approach for a photo or introduction.

**Departure:**
Late afternoon, David prepares to leave. Robert walks him to the car.

"David, it was a pleasure having you. I've enjoyed getting to know you. Let's continue the conversation when we're both back in New York."

---

## Discussion Questions

### Question 1: Dress Code Arrival

David was uncertain about dress code and overdressed. How could he have handled this better? What does his arrival attire signal?

### Question 2: The Phone Photo

David's instinct to photograph at the 12th hole. What are the implications, and how should he have handled the impulse?

### Question 3: Golf Performance Pressure

David was nervous and played poorly early. How should he think about performance anxiety in these settings?

### Question 4: No Business Discussion

Three days together and no direct business discussion. Is this a failure? How should David interpret this?

### Question 5: The Celebrity Sighting

Should David have approached the famous athlete? What would that signal?

### Question 6: The Follow-Up

Robert said "let's continue the conversation." How should David follow up?

---

## Analysis

### Dress Code

**The Mistake:**
David arrived in dress slacks and blazer. Robert was in khakis and golf shirt. David was overdressed.

**Better Approach:**
Research or ask: "Robert, what should I wear when I arrive?" Most hosts appreciate the question—it shows care.

**The Lesson:**
When uncertain, ask. Being overdressed is generally safer than underdressed, but asking is safest.

### The Phone Photo

**The Rule:**
Augusta National prohibits cell phones on property (with limited exceptions). This is well-known. David should have known and left his phone in the cabin.

**The Save:**
If caught pulling out phone, apologize immediately: "I'm sorry—I forgot the policy. It won't happen again." Put phone away without taking photo.

**The Lesson:**
Research club policies before arrival. At ultra-exclusive clubs, phone policies are strict. Violation marks you as unaware.

### Golf Performance

**The Reality:**
Robert doesn't care about David's score. He invited David because he's considering a relationship, not because he needs a good golf partner.

**The Right Mindset:**
- Play your game
- Maintain good etiquette (pace, conduct)
- Be pleasant company
- Enjoy the experience
- Don't apologize excessively for poor shots

**What Robert's Watching:**
- How David handles adversity (poor shots)
- Whether David is pleasant company
- David's respect for the course and traditions
- David's character under light pressure

### No Business Discussion

**The Interpretation:**
This is not a failure—it's how these relationships work.

Robert invited David to assess him as a person, not to negotiate terms. Three days of personal interaction tells Robert more about David's character than any pitch would.

**The Subtext:**
If Robert enjoys David's company and trusts his character, business will follow. If not, no pitch would have worked anyway.

**The Lesson:**
In relationship-first cultures (old money, family offices), business is earned through relationship, not pursued directly.

### Celebrity Approach

**The Answer:**
Absolutely not.

Approaching a celebrity at Augusta would signal:
- You're impressed (you should act like you belong)
- You're there for celebrity access
- You don't understand the privacy norms
- You're not yet comfortable in these settings

**The Right Move:**
Notice, appreciate internally, say nothing. If the celebrity approaches you through Robert, engage normally.

### The Follow-Up

**Immediate (within 48 hours):**
Handwritten note to Robert:

*"Dear Robert, Thank you for an unforgettable few days at Augusta. Your hospitality was extraordinary, and I especially enjoyed our conversation about [specific topic]. The experience will stay with me. With appreciation, David"*

**No business mention. No "next steps." Pure gratitude.**

**Two Weeks Later:**
Brief email or call: "Robert, I'm back in New York. Wanted to say hello and see how you're doing after the Masters. If you have time for lunch in the next few weeks, I'd enjoy that."

**One Month Later:**
If lunch happens, let Robert raise business. If he doesn't, continue relationship-building.

---

## Key Lessons

### Lesson 1: The Invitation Is the Evaluation

Being invited to Augusta (or any ultra-exclusive setting) is itself the evaluation. Your behavior during the experience determines whether the relationship advances.

### Lesson 2: No Phones, No Photos

Ultra-exclusive settings have strict privacy norms. Research and comply absolutely. Violations are remembered.

### Lesson 3: Performance Doesn't Matter; Character Does

In golf, business, and social settings, hosts are evaluating character—how you handle pressure, treat others, carry yourself. Performance is secondary.

### Lesson 4: Business Follows Relationship

In old money contexts, business is never discussed until relationship is established. This can take months or years. Patience is required and expected.

### Lesson 5: Act Like You Belong

Don't be impressed, take photos, approach celebrities, or behave like a tourist. Act like these experiences are comfortable (even if they're not).

---

## Epilogue

David's note arrived while Robert was still at Augusta. Robert mentioned to his wife: "That David Kim has good manners."

A month later, they had lunch in New York. Robert asked about the fund. David gave a brief overview. Robert said he'd review the materials with his investment team.

Three months later, Robert's family office committed $100 million to the fund.

Robert later told a friend: "What I liked about David is he just enjoyed being there. He wasn't working me. He wasn't trying to impress me. He was just himself."
