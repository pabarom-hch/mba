# Social Media and Privacy in Elite Settings

## Learning Objectives

- Navigate social media use in high-net-worth circles
- Understand privacy expectations and norms
- Manage your own digital presence appropriately
- Avoid common social media missteps
- Build reputation while maintaining discretion

---

## Introduction

The UHNW community has a complex relationship with social media. While some embrace it, many view oversharing as a mark of lower status. Understanding when, what, and how to share—and more importantly, what not to share—is essential for maintaining trust and access in elite circles.

### The Old Money View of Social Media

**The Prevailing Perspective:**
- Discretion is a sign of class
- Sharing is often seen as status-seeking
- True status doesn't need validation
- Privacy protects family and safety
- What you don't share can't be used against you

**The Practical Concern:**
- Security risks from location sharing
- Kidnapping and targeting concerns
- Resentment from wealth display
- Loss of mystique through over-exposure

### What Not to Share

**Never:**
- Photos inside someone else's home (without explicit permission)
- Private events (especially someone else's)
- Other people's children
- Wealth displays (private jets, jewelry, price tags)
- Location in real-time at private venues
- Details about security arrangements
- Other people's personal information

**Exercise Extreme Caution:**
- Club interiors and events
- Charity gala photos (some welcome, some don't)
- Travel photos while traveling (security risk)
- Photos with celebrities or notable people
- Behind-the-scenes of exclusive events

### The Phone Rule at Events

**The Standard:**
At elite social events, phones should be:
- Silent or off
- In pocket or bag
- Not on the table
- Not used for photos without permission

**Photography Guidelines:**
- Never photograph at private clubs (many prohibit it)
- Ask before photographing people
- Ask before photographing art (galleries often restrict)
- Never share photos without subject's permission
- Group photos at events: coordinate who will share

**The "Ask Before Sharing" Protocol:**
If you've taken a photo with or of someone:
- Ask before posting: "Is it okay if I share this?"
- Respect any hesitation as a no
- Offer to send them the photo privately instead

### Managing Your Own Digital Presence

**The Curated Approach:**
If you choose to have social media presence:
- Less is more
- Quality over quantity
- Professional and tasteful
- No controversial opinions
- No wealth displays
- No location broadcasting

**What to Share (If Anything):**
- Professional milestones (briefly)
- Charitable involvement (humbly)
- Cultural engagement (art, theater, music)
- Travel (after you return)
- Family (carefully, with their consent)

**What Creates a Strong Impression:**
Minimal presence often creates intrigue. When someone searches for you:
- A professional LinkedIn is sufficient for most
- A clean, professional image
- No embarrassing content
- No political controversies
- No excessive self-promotion

### Googling Etiquette

**The Reality:**
Everyone searches everyone. Before meeting someone:
- Expect to be Googled
- Expect your social media to be reviewed
- Expect your professional history to be checked

**Your Due Diligence:**
- Know what appears when you're searched
- Clean up old content that doesn't serve you
- Maintain consistent, professional presence
- Set up Google alerts for your name

**The Don't-Mention Rule:**
If you've Googled someone:
- Don't reference what you found
- Let them tell you about themselves
- Don't say: "I saw on your Instagram that..."
- Exception: Referencing their professional work is acceptable

### Social Media and Philanthropy

**The Tension:**
Charities want publicity; donors often want privacy.

**Navigate By:**
- Asking the organization's preference
- Asking your own comfort level
- Being generous without being public
- If you do share, focusing on the cause, not yourself

**Acceptable:**
- Sharing an organization's general message
- Promoting a cause you care about
- Thanking an organization publicly (with their permission)

**Often Inappropriate:**
- Sharing your specific donation amount
- Photos of you presenting large checks
- Extensive coverage of your charitable "sacrifice"

### Crisis Management

**If Something Goes Wrong:**
- Inappropriate post you made: Delete and apologize privately if needed
- Tagged in something inappropriate: Untag and ask poster to remove
- Negative content about you: Assess whether to address or ignore
- Private information leaked: Address quickly, professionally

**The Best Defense:**
Don't create the problem. Be thoughtful before posting.

## Mentor Perspectives

### The Rothschild Tradition of Privacy

*"Our family has thrived for generations in part because we maintain our privacy. What people don't know about us is as valuable as what they do. Social media is antithetical to this principle. We don't share our homes, our events, or our family moments publicly. Privacy is a discipline that protects."*

### Paul Fussell on Status and Display

*"The upper class doesn't need to prove itself. That's what defines it. Social media is essentially proof—proof of experiences, proof of possessions, proof of connections. The more you prove, the less you have. True status is quiet, assumed, and doesn't need documentation."*

### Modern Adaptation: Balanced Perspective

*"There's a middle ground. A minimal, professional presence can serve you without overexposure. The key is intentionality. Ask: does this serve me, or am I seeking validation? If the latter, don't post."*

## CEO Application

### Professional Social Media Strategy

**LinkedIn:**
- Maintain professional profile
- Thoughtful about connections
- Minimal personal content
- Share professional milestones appropriately

**Twitter/X:**
- Optional for most executives
- Industry commentary (careful)
- Avoid political controversy
- Brief, professional if present

**Instagram:**
- Often unnecessary for executives
- If present, highly curated
- Minimal wealth display
- No real-time locations

**Facebook:**
- Often personal/family only
- Strict privacy settings
- Not necessary for professional purposes

### Privacy and Security Considerations

**As Wealth Increases:**
- Home locations become sensitive
- Family becomes targets of interest
- Travel patterns are valuable information
- Security requires more discretion

**Practical Measures:**
- Don't broadcast travel in real-time
- Don't share home interiors
- Don't share routine patterns
- Brief family on sharing guidelines

### When Publicity Serves You

Sometimes visibility is strategic:
- Building professional platform
- Promoting causes
- Thought leadership
- Company representation

**The Key:**
Control the narrative. Share what serves your goals. Protect what doesn't.

## Worked Example

### The Gala Photo Decision

**The Situation:**
Jennifer attended a high-profile charity gala. She took photos with the host, a billionaire philanthropist, and with a group of other attendees. She'd like to share something on social media.

**The Analysis:**
1. Photo with host: She asks the host's assistant if sharing is acceptable. Answer: "Mr. Smith prefers not to appear on social media." Photo not shared.

2. Group photo: She texts the group: "Is everyone okay with me sharing this on Instagram?" Two say yes, one says "I'd prefer not to be in photos online." Photo not shared.

3. Photo of the venue/art: She checks the event guidelines—no photography of art was permitted. Not shared.

4. Photo of herself at the event: She could share this, but asks: what does it communicate? Decision: She shares a simple mention of supporting the cause, without photos.

**Her Post:**
"Grateful to support [Organization]'s mission to [cause]. What wonderful work they do."

**The Result:**
She's publicly supported the cause without oversharing, displaying wealth, or violating others' privacy. Her discretion is noted positively.

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Real-Time Location Sharing
**The Error:** Broadcasting where you are as you're there.
**The Fix:** Share travel after you return, if at all.

### Mistake 2: Photos Without Permission
**The Error:** Posting photos with people who didn't consent.
**The Fix:** Always ask. Respect any hesitation.

### Mistake 3: Wealth Display
**The Error:** Posting private jets, jewelry, expensive purchases.
**The Fix:** These mark you as status-seeking. Don't post.

### Mistake 4: Private Event Documentation
**The Error:** Treating private events as content opportunities.
**The Fix:** Private means private. Experience it, don't broadcast it.

### Mistake 5: Over-Curation Backfiring
**The Error:** Creating obviously staged, curated content that seems fake.
**The Fix:** If you share, be genuine. Better to share rarely and authentically.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Discretion signals class** - Less sharing is generally more respected

2. **Ask before sharing others** - Consent is essential

3. **Protect private spaces** - Someone's home is never your content

4. **Think security** - Location and pattern sharing creates risk

5. **Curate intentionally** - Every post should serve you

6. **When in doubt, don't post** - You can never un-share
