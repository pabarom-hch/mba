# Charity Galas and Fundraising Events

## Learning Objectives

- Navigate the structure and expectations of charity galas
- Understand paddle raises, silent auctions, and live auctions
- Participate appropriately in fundraising without overcommitting
- Build relationships in philanthropic settings
- Avoid common gala missteps

---

## Introduction

Charity galas represent a unique intersection of philanthropy, social positioning, and business networking. Understanding how to navigate these events—from the cocktail reception through the paddle raise—separates the comfortable participant from the uncertain newcomer.

### Before the Event: Preparation

**Research the Organization:**
- Understand their mission
- Know their major programs
- Research leadership and board
- Review recent news or achievements

**Know the Giving Levels:**
- What are the table prices?
- What are typical individual gifts?
- What are paddle raise levels?
- What's the "anchor gift" range?

**Prepare Your Budget:**
- Discuss with spouse/partner before arrival
- Set a paddle raise maximum
- Allocate for silent auction (if participating)
- Know your comfort zone

**The Cardinal Rule:**
*Never attend a fundraising event without knowing your giving budget in advance.*

### Silent Auctions

**How They Work:**
- Items displayed during cocktails
- Guests write bids on sheets or use mobile bidding
- Bidding continues until stated close time
- Highest bidder wins

**Strategy:**
- Browse early to identify items of interest
- Bid strategically, not emotionally
- Remember: retail value is ceiling, not floor
- Don't get in bidding wars for ego
- Unique experiences often better values than items

**Etiquette:**
- Don't bid to raise the price for others
- Don't bid if you don't intend to pay
- Be gracious whether you win or lose
- Items are for charitable giving, not bargain hunting

### Live Auctions

**How They Work:**
- Auctioneer presents 5-10 premium items
- Guests bid with paddles or hand signals
- Often includes experiences (dinners, trips, access)
- Typically highest-value items

**Participation:**
- Bid only on items you genuinely want
- Know your maximum before bidding starts
- Raise paddle clearly and confidently
- Stop at your maximum—don't get emotional
- If you win, smile and acknowledge graciously

**If You Don't Bid:**
- That's completely acceptable
- Show interest and engagement
- Applaud winning bidders
- No explanation needed

### The Paddle Raise (Fund-A-Need)

**What It Is:**
The paddle raise is direct fundraising—no auction item, just donations to the cause. The auctioneer asks for gifts at descending levels.

**How It Works:**
```
"Who will give $100,000 to support our education programs?"
[Paddles go up, acknowledged by name]

"Who will give $50,000?"
[More paddles]

"$25,000?"
[More paddles]

"$10,000... $5,000... $2,500... $1,000..."
```

**Key Points:**
- Higher-level gifts are often pre-arranged
- You can give at any level
- No shame in any amount—all gifts matter
- Give at a level that's meaningful to you and the organization

**Strategy:**
- Know your number before the paddle raise starts
- When your level is called, raise immediately and confidently
- Don't hesitate—it signals uncertainty
- Once you've given, you're done (no need to keep bidding up)

**What Not to Do:**
- Don't give beyond your budget to keep up
- Don't abstain entirely if you can give something
- Don't make excuses or explanations
- Don't look around to see what others are doing

### Table Dynamics

**If You Bought a Table:**
- You're the host for your guests
- Ensure everyone is comfortable
- Make introductions around the table
- Lead engagement with auction and paddle raise

**If You're a Guest at Someone's Table:**
- Thank your host appropriately (note afterward)
- Engage fully with the evening
- Participate in paddle raise (you're expected to)
- Don't outshine your host in giving unless invited

**Seated with Strangers:**
- Introduce yourself to tablemates
- Find common ground
- Be a good dinner companion
- Exchange cards if appropriate

## Mentor Perspectives

### Warren Buffett on Philanthropic Participation

*"The wealthy have a responsibility to give back, but giving should be thoughtful, not performative. Give to causes you understand and believe in. The paddle raise is not a competition—it's a collective effort."*

### The Rothschild Approach to Charitable Engagement

*"Our family's philanthropic giving has always been strategic and sustained. One gala gift means little; consistent commitment means everything. Be known for what you support over time, not for what you gave at one event."*

### Letitia Baldrige on Gala Conduct

*"At charity events, your conduct reflects both on you and on the organization. Be generous, be gracious, and be genuine. The best donors are those who make others comfortable giving alongside them."*

## CEO Application

### Philanthropic Strategy for Business Leaders

**Build a Giving Framework:**
1. Identify 2-3 causes aligned with your values
2. Research organizations in those areas
3. Make multi-year commitments (not one-off gifts)
4. Build relationships with leadership over time

**The Strategic Benefits:**
- Board positions create business relationships
- Fellow donors are often business peers
- Philanthropic reputation enhances business reputation
- Giving creates access to otherwise closed circles

**The Authentic Foundation:**
All strategy aside, give to what matters to you. Authenticity in philanthropy is visible and valued.

### Corporate vs. Personal Giving

**Corporate Giving:**
- Tax advantages
- Brand alignment
- Employee engagement
- Stakeholder relations

**Personal Giving:**
- More private
- More flexible
- Builds personal relationships
- Signals personal values

Many leaders do both, keeping clear separation between the two.

## Worked Example

### The Museum Gala

**The Situation:**
Sarah, a tech executive, has been invited to a major art museum's annual gala. Her friend, a board member, bought a table and invited her. The paddle raise will support the museum's education programs.

**Her Preparation:**
1. She researches the museum's education programs
2. She learns the typical giving levels ($25,000 / $10,000 / $5,000 / $2,500 / $1,000)
3. She discusses with her husband and sets a $5,000 budget
4. She reviews the silent auction catalog and notes two items of interest

**At the Event:**
- Cocktails: She bids on a photography print (max $1,500) and a restaurant experience (max $500)
- Dinner: She engages with tablemates, asks about their museum involvement
- Program: She listens attentively to the mission presentation
- Paddle Raise: When $5,000 is called, she raises her paddle immediately and confidently
- After: She thanks her host and congratulates other donors

**The Follow-Up:**
- Handwritten note to her friend (the table host) within 48 hours
- If interested in deeper involvement, she asks for introduction to development director

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Attending Unprepared
**The Error:** Arriving without knowing giving levels or having set a budget.
**The Fix:** Research and prepare before every gala. Know your number.

### Mistake 2: Competitive Giving
**The Error:** Giving more than comfortable to keep up with others.
**The Fix:** Give meaningfully within your means. No one is keeping score.

### Mistake 3: Abstaining Entirely
**The Error:** Not participating in paddle raise at all.
**The Fix:** Every gift matters. Give at whatever level is appropriate for you.

### Mistake 4: Business Focus at Charity Events
**The Error:** Using the gala primarily for business development.
**The Fix:** Focus on the cause first. Relationships develop naturally from shared commitment.

### Mistake 5: Forgetting to Follow Up
**The Error:** Not thanking hosts or following through on auction wins.
**The Fix:** Send notes within 48 hours. Pay auction items promptly.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Prepare before you arrive** - Know the cause, the levels, and your budget

2. **Give within your means** - No competition, just contribution

3. **Participate fully** - Silent auction, conversation, paddle raise

4. **Focus on the cause** - Not the social positioning

5. **Follow up properly** - Thank-you notes and prompt payment

6. **Build relationships over time** - One gala is a beginning, not an end

## Quick Reference: Paddle Raise Protocol

| Situation | Appropriate Action |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Level called is your budget | Raise paddle immediately, confidently |
| Level is above your budget | Keep paddle down, stay engaged |
| You've already given | No need to raise again at lower levels |
| You can't give tonight | Attend graciously, participate in conversation |
| You want to give more than called | Ask about "anchor gifts" before event |
